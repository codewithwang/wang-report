China Fires a Pacific Missile While Hormuz's New Landlord Cashes In

A submarine-launched test off Australia's doorstep and a quiet tanker fortune off Hormuz both say the same thing: the map of who profits from instability is being redrawn overnight.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

China test-fired a long-range submarine-launched ballistic missile into the South Pacific overnight, timed almost exactly with Canberra signing a new mutual defence pact with Fiji. Australia called the test destabilising. The more useful number is the one Bloomberg's tanker reporting surfaced: the shadow fleet now shuttling crude out of the Strait of Hormuz has made at least one Gulf producer's owner personally hundreds of millions since the war began. The chokepoint everyone still calls a crisis is already a functioning business for whoever got there first. The Hormuz story is no longer about whether oil moves. It is about who owns the workaround. Kyiv took its second major missile barrage in a week ahead of the NATO summit. Eleven people were killed and apartment blocks were destroyed. The summit opens under fire rather than under a ceasefire clock. Iran's leadership transition is still unsettled two days after Khamenei's funeral, with Tehran publicly rejecting Washington's ultimatum language. Sanctions-screening models built on the assumption that the strait is closed are already stale. What to watch by evening: whether Canberra names China directly in a formal démarche. The language will tell APAC insurers how much to charge for Pacific war-risk cover this quarter.
