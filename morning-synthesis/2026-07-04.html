Hormuz Toll Fee Emerges as Iran Buries Its Supreme Leader

While Tehran hosts days of funeral ceremonies, Iran and Oman are quietly floating a transit fee for the Strait of Hormuz, the chokepoint through which a fifth of the world's oil moves.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

The overnight cycle has two clocks running at once. In Tehran, the funeral procession for Khamenei stretches into its second day, foreign delegations filing through under heavy security, most Western coverage treating it as ceremony. Reuters and NBC both report a separate story: Iran and Oman are floating a fee plan for vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz, the chokepoint carrying roughly a fifth of global oil supply. That is a toll booth on the world's energy supply chain, proposed in the same week our own coverage shows Asia accelerating a shift away from just-in-time logistics after the Hormuz scare. Charmaine Lo's weekend column on Hong Kong grading itself against exams it already rewrote applies to Tehran's own stress test too. Elsewhere, Putin toured the Donbas front vowing more territory while Warsaw's PM warns of a hard summer ahead, NATO's European members insisting they have absorbed the US drawdown without naming the shortfall. Watch whether Iran and Oman's fee plan gets a number attached, and whether Gulf shippers start rerouting before it does.
