THORChain's Open Ledger Beats the AML Rulebook Twice

North Korea moved its two biggest crypto hauls through the same laundering protocol in plain sight, and the compliance frameworks APAC banks lean on still have no lever for it.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

The overnight wire is thin on new fires and thick on old ones still burning: Iran's succession stays opaque with Mojtaba absent from a second day of mourning, and Ukraine's drones keep hitting Russian energy infrastructure. Venezuela's earthquake toll has passed 3,000. But this morning's cyber column carries the story that changes a compliance officer's Monday. Pyongyang ran its two largest crypto thefts through THORChain, a decentralized exchange with no operator to subpoena, and did it twice in full public view. The AML rulebooks HK and Singapore banks build their sanctions screening on assume a chokepoint they can freeze or a firm they can serve papers on. THORChain has neither. A compliance head at a licensed virtual asset exchange must decide by end of week whether their transaction monitoring flags DEX routing at all, because the current playbook only watches centralized rails. Elsewhere, CISA's three-day patch order on SharePoint reads routine until you clock the vendor's July 14 support cliff nine days out, a capex line few CFOs have budgeted. Regulators will keep writing AML rules for exchanges that can say no, while the protocol that actually moved Pyongyang's money answers to nobody. Watch whether Treasury's OFAC names THORChain directly this week, the first test of whether sanctions can reach a protocol instead of a company.
