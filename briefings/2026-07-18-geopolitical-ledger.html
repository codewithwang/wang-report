GEOPOLITICAL Vincent Lai Geopolitical Desk, Occasional Contributor Saturday, July 18, 2026

US Central Command's expanded strikes on Iranian positions near the Strait of Hormuz land first on Governor Pan Gongsheng's reserve management desk, not on the Foreign Ministry's statement drafters. The PBOC's foreign currency committee has run a weekly hedging cycle against Gulf energy-import costs since the March liquidity signals Premier Li's economic working group sent through tools the diplomatic corps does not monitor, and an escalation that raises the probability of a Hormuz closure event reprices that hedge before it reprices anything Beijing might say about Taiwan. Taipei tracked nine PLA aircraft and accompanying vessels on a single day this week, a sortie count the PLA has run long enough that Pan's desk does not treat it as new information. The Hormuz numbers are.

The distinction that matters is which timeline the PBOC is actually paid to watch. Mei Chen's read of this week, drone procurement on Taipei's clock against rehearsal cadence on Beijing's, describes a contest neither side is pricing through the currency desk. Pan's committee is pricing something else: a Strait of Hormuz closure risk that moves dollar-funding costs and energy-import bills before the next reserve report, due at the PBOC's regular monthly disclosure window in early August. Premier Li's working group set the precedent in March that monetary tools move on energy and funding stress, not on sortie counts or joint statements, or, more precisely, that the tools move on the stress a closure would create, not the diplomatic noise around it. The next figure worth reading is not a warplane count. It is whatever the FX committee's August reserve disclosure says about the energy-cost hedge Pan's desk has been running since March.
