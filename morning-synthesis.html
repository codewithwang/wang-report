Iran's Barakah Strike Lifts Oil to $110, Breaks the Bond Market

A strike on the UAE's only operating nuclear facility has set oil above $110 and pushed 30-year Treasury yields to a two-decade high, repricing the cost of capital for every APAC portfolio holding duration.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

The overnight signal: oil above $110 after an Iranian strike on the UAE's Barakah nuclear facility, with bond markets simultaneously pushing 30-year yields toward their highest level in two decades. These are not separate stories. The Iran war has become the fiscal event that markets priced as tail risk and are now pricing as base case: structurally higher energy costs, higher defence spending, persistently elevated borrowing costs.

The Beijing summit produced no breakthroughs. Trump called Xi a friend and left with nothing concrete. Mei Chen's column today is essential reading before that headline: the NDAA markup that arrived during the summit has already converted Taiwan's arms transfers from a treaty obligation into a trade concession. Taiwan's defence planners know it. Beijing knows it. The friendship language is price discovery, not diplomacy.

In Hong Kong courts this morning, Evergrande's liquidators open their case against PwC, testing how far auditor liability runs on the mainland property collapse. Australia's second forced divestiture of Chinese rare-earth stakes in two years confirms supply-chain decoupling is moving below the headline commodity level. Korean equities, down sharply on yield pressure, confirm the AI rally is running directly into the bond market repricing that the Iran war is driving.
