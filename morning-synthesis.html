Iran Buries Its Supreme Leader as the Toll Fee Still Has No Number

Khamenei's funeral exposes a leadership vacuum in Tehran just as Iran and Oman float a Hormuz shipping fee with no figure attached, leaving Asian trade desks waiting on a number Muscat has not named.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

Khamenei is buried this weekend, and the more telling story is who did not show up. His son Mojtaba's absence from the funeral procession has Iranian political circles openly asking who is actually running the country, the New York Times reports, a succession question that matters more to APAC energy buyers than the ceremony itself. Meanwhile the Hormuz toll fee this column flagged Thursday still carries no figure: Iran and Oman have floated the plan but named no number, so Gulf shippers have nothing to hedge against. Charmaine Lo's weekend column on Hong Kong rebuilding its own walls reads well against both stories, a city retrofitting systems built for a threat that already moved on. Ukraine disputes Putin's claim of taking Kostiantynivka in Donetsk, a logistics hub whose fall would harden Russia's negotiating position either way. Trump's 260 FBI analysts landing in Georgia is a domestic story with an export version: any APAC regulator watching US institutional stability for sovereign risk purposes should clock it. A Singapore-based commodities trader now has one job before Monday's open: find out if Muscat has attached a dollar figure to that Hormuz fee, because a toll with no number attached is not a cost anyone can hedge.
