North Korea Retrains Its Malware to Fool the AI Watching For It

Kyiv absorbs its worst bombardment of the war and Brussels lands a record antitrust fine, but the story that should reorder a CISO's morning is about the detector, not the attacker.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

The loud stories overnight are Russia's largest bombardment of Kyiv to date, an EU fine of 4.1 billion euros against Google over Android antitrust breaches, and Washington's refusal to renew USMCA, the trade pact underpinning North American commerce since 2020. The one worth reading before the desk opens is Kai Tanner's column on North Korean macOS malware built specifically to fool the AI models now doing first-pass threat triage. The industry has spent two years selling AI detection as the fix for alert fatigue. The vulnerability did not close, it moved up a layer, from the endpoint to the model judging the endpoint. A bank CISO running an AI-assisted SOC now has to ask whether a missed alert is adversary tradecraft or a blind spot in the tool bought to prevent it. Elsewhere, the alleged Scattered Spider hacker's extradition to the US closes one case as an 81 million-attempt password-spray campaign against Microsoft 365 opens the next, credential hygiene remains the cheapest fix nobody funds. Hong Kong's asset pool crossed US$5.38 trillion under management this quarter, a APAC wealth base worth tracking against every one of tonight's headlines. Watch whether any major EDR vendor responds publicly to the Tanner findings by end of day.
