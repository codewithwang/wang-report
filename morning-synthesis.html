Iran Deal Signed, Hormuz Already in Dispute Between Tehran and IRGC

The gap between Iran's elected government and the Revolutionary Guard on Hormuz transit is the energy and shipping variable that APAC compliance desks and asset managers must resolve before Monday open.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

The US-Iran nuclear agreement, signed overnight, caps Tehran's uranium enrichment at five percent in exchange for phased sanctions relief. The deal fractured before the communique circulated. Iran's Foreign Ministry declared the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly a third of global seaborne oil transits daily, remains open. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, the parallel military structure that answers to Supreme Leader Khamenei rather than the elected president, simultaneously announced mandatory insurance fees and transit permissions for all passing vessels. The IRGC controls the physical checkpoints. Compliance officers and asset managers running Q3 risk models in Singapore and Hong Kong should register that two Iranian institutions are giving different answers to the same operative question.

Israel declared the agreement a catastrophic capitulation and struck southern Lebanon within hours, killing eighteen people. Vance cancelled his Geneva follow-up trip. Japan's Defense Minister Koizumi told the BBC overnight that accelerating rearmament is now critical, the clearest Tokyo statement yet that western Pacific deterrence cannot rest on Washington alone. A CISO tracking state-linked threat actors should note: a third suspect was charged in Melbourne over the synagogue arson Australian authorities attribute to Iranian state direction, operational reach into the Five Eyes intelligence-sharing perimeter that APAC security teams are now assessing.
