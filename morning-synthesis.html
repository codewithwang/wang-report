Hormuz Ceasefire Holds as China Quietly Books the Gain

A U.S.-Iran halt ends the shooting but not the leverage game; the Strait question unresolved, Seoul's $880 billion chip bet lands, and a Washington court just reminded every central banker in Asia what independence costs.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

The overnight story is a ceasefire, not a settlement. Washington and Tehran have agreed to halt exchanges over the Strait of Hormuz, the 21-mile chokepoint through which roughly a third of the world's seaborne oil passes, but Iran's negotiating position, as the NYT reports, rests on keeping that chokepoint in play. High-level talks are now scheduled in Doha. A Hong Kong private banker with Gulf-linked credit exposure rechecks those positions this morning, not next week. The NYT also flags what Memeorandum missed: China emerges from the Hormuz crisis in a structurally stronger position, having secured discounted Iranian crude throughout the exchange while competitors absorbed spot-price volatility. That asymmetry is the APAC read this morning.

In Seoul, the government has unveiled an $880 billion chip and AI investment plan, the largest single industrial commitment in Korean history, timed against the regional technology race with Taiwan and China. A Singapore asset manager running exposure to Korean semiconductor names gets a clearer demand signal; the plan pulls forward capex decisions that were sitting in the pending column.

In Washington, the Supreme Court blocked Trump from removing the Federal Reserve governor, preserving the convention that the Fed operates at arm's length from the executive. Every HKMA counterpart and MAS official watching U.S. monetary independence read that ruling before the close. The Court also widened presidential power to dismiss other agency heads, a holding that cuts the other way and complicates the picture for foreign counterparts trying to read the durability of U.S. regulatory commitments.

Chinese vessels shadowed U.S.-Philippine naval drills near Scarborough Shoal over the weekend. The South China Sea file did not close while attention was on the Gulf.

Watch by end of day: whether Doha talks produce even a procedural communique. A blank outcome reopens the Hormuz risk premium before the Asian close.
