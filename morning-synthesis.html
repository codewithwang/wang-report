Starmer Falls, Hormuz Closes Again, Oil Retreats on Iran Talks

Three simultaneous shocks reshape the overnight risk picture: a UK leadership vacuum, a second Strait of Hormuz closure, and US-Iran diplomatic progress already moving crude prices.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

Britain woke to its seventh prime minister in a decade after Keir Starmer announced his resignation from Downing Street late Monday. Andy Burnham, the Greater Manchester mayor, is reported as frontrunner to succeed him, with Wes Streeting seen as next chancellor. The pound fell on the news while gilts held steady, a spread that tells a fixed-income manager the market reads this as political noise rather than fiscal risk. A Hong Kong private bank running GBP positions adjusts that assumption this morning. Meanwhile Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz closed for a second time, the 21-mile chokepoint through which roughly 20 percent of global seaborne oil passes, before a first round of US-Iran talks in Switzerland produced what Qatari and Pakistani mediators called encouraging progress. Oil retreated on that signal. A Singapore trade-finance lender with Hormuz-exposed letters of credit is reviewing counterparty exposure before the Asian close. On the technology axis, China restricted exports to American defense firms in retaliation for US sanctions on Chinese tech companies, a tit-for-tat sequence the SCMP rare-earth fragile truce story makes concrete: Beijing is methodically expanding the list of pressure points. South Korea's former justice minister drew 25 years for his role in the 2024 martial law episode, the most consequential judicial sentence from that crisis and a signal to APAC compliance officers that political-risk models for Northeast Asia now require criminal liability scenarios. Watch whether the Iran talks session two, expected today, produces a ceasefire framework in Lebanon. If it does, the Hormuz closure becomes a negotiating artifact rather than a supply shock, and oil traders will move fast.
