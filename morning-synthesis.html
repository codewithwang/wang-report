Hong Kong Claims Cross-Border Wealth Crown as Iran Strikes Reshape Hormuz Risk

The private banker's hub of record shifted overnight as Boston Consulting Group placed Hong Kong ahead of Switzerland, while US strikes on Iran move Hormuz disruption from tail risk to summer assumption.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

US strikes on Iranian missile sites and naval vessels landed overnight, and Trump said economic costs will not accelerate his deal timeline. For the compliance officer tracking Hormuz, disruption is no longer a tail risk; it graduates to a summer planning assumption. South Korea's move to commercialise the Arctic shipping route by 2030 is the supply chain pivot already in motion. China's electronic interference against a Dutch warship near the Paracel Islands, the South China Sea chain Beijing claims as sovereign territory, adds a second maritime chokepoint for shipping and insurance desks.

The finance story is in Hong Kong. Boston Consulting Group's annual wealth report puts the city ahead of Switzerland as the world's top cross-border wealth hub, the first such lead for any Asian centre. The HKMA, Hong Kong's central bank, launches a gold clearing system in the next two months that settles bullion trades locally without routing through London. The private banker and the asset manager both have a changed picture this morning. Today's column covers the 18,000 ride-hailing permit holders absorbing CLP's 5.4% June fuel increase into margins already under pressure. Watch for White House language by end of day on whether last night's Iran strikes are a sustained military posture or a bounded action.
