Hormuz Attack Tests US-Iran Deal as Asia Tech Sells Off

A confirmed Iranian strike on a cargo ship and a broad AI cost reckoning hit APAC markets simultaneously, forcing a read on both shipping risk and portfolio exposure before the weekend.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

Two fault lines cracked open overnight. Iran struck a commercial vessel in the Strait of Hormuz, the waterway carrying roughly 20 percent of global oil trade, hours after Washington was seeking Gulf-state support for its ceasefire framework. The attack is confirmed by both US and Iranian officials per the New York Times. Oil initially spiked then resumed its weekly decline as transits continued, per Bloomberg, but the episode is not resolved: a cargo ship was hit, and APAC trade-finance desks with letters of credit on Hormuz-routed cargo need to re-rate that exposure. Any lender with positions on Gulf shipping routes reviews that book this weekend.

Asia tech stocks tumbled after Apple and Microsoft raised product prices, stoking concern that rising memory and component costs are compressing AI economics, Bloomberg reports. Chinese hedge fund managers publicly called the AI boom a bubble, also Bloomberg. The sell-off reinforces what our homepage is already running on the chipmaker drag and the OpenAI GPT-5.6 rollout restriction: AI cost reality is arriving faster than the upgrade cycle assumed. An APAC asset manager long on AI-adjacent equities has a positioning question before Monday open.

The World Cup group stage produced USMNT's first loss and several knockout-round confirmations, noise against the signal. The geopolitical frame holds: Hormuz disruption and AI cost compression arrived in the same overnight session, and neither has resolved.
