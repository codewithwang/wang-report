Won at 2009 Low, Rupiah Through 18,000 as Iran War Stress Spreads East

Overnight currency breaks in Seoul and Jakarta arrive as the July 1 catastrophe reinsurance renewal approaches, leaving two in three APAC weather-loss dollars without cover.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

South Korea's won fell to its lowest since 2009, Seoul's finance ministry pledging action against excessive volatility. Indonesia's rupiah broke 18,000 per dollar, the level where Bank Indonesia, the country's central bank, faces pressure to intervene visibly; Jakarta equities hit a near six-year low alongside. For an asset manager running Korean or Indonesian exposure, hedging costs at these levels change the return calculation on open positions.

Magnus Honeyfield's reinsurance column today arrives at the right moment. Catastrophe reinsurance, the layer of cover that insurers themselves buy against large weather losses, reprices at July 1 for APAC contracts. One dollar in three of regional losses sees new pricing at renewal; the other two sit with buyers who did not come to the table. Any risk officer carrying uncovered weather exposure into a summer the UN assigns 80% El Nino probability has a concrete renewal decision now.

In Hong Kong, the 37th Tiananmen anniversary brought police interception of an artist marking the date with red thread, and warnings to victims' families against cemetery visits. Beijing barred four New Zealand lawmakers for visiting Taiwan. The IAEA, the UN's nuclear watchdog, placed nuclear risk inside the Iran conflict calculus; Rubio described Tehran as ready to negotiate while Tehran declared talks over. Watch rupiah and won at the afternoon fix.
