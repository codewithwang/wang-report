Hormuz Tanker Strike Puts the Strait Back Into the Price

A US-Iran exchange of strikes after a tanker hit in the Strait of Hormuz puts every APAC trade-finance desk back on Hormuz exposure this Sunday morning.
Walter Wang · WR Editor-in-Chief · Hong Kong

The overnight story is kinetic: a tanker struck in the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow chokepoint through which roughly 20 percent of global seaborne oil transits, triggered a US strike on Iranian-linked targets and an Iranian counter-strike on US-associated positions. CBS and Reuters are running parallel feeds with conflicting casualty claims. The ceasefire architecture that held since earlier this year now has a visible crack, and it cracked on a weekend when Strait insurance premiums were already elevated from the prior month's close passes. A Singapore trade-finance lender re-checks Hormuz-exposed letters of credit this week; a Hong Kong energy trader revises physical cargo hedges before Monday's open. The Nikkei dropped more than three percent in early Tokyo trading, the sharpest single-session tech-weighted fall in six months, compounding a Wall Street week where Nasdaq and the S&P 500 had already stalled on AI valuation questions and the reported OpenAI IPO delay. The Trump administration's decision to restrict OpenAI's latest model launch while simultaneously permitting limited access to Anthropic's Mythos for trusted US organisations signals an export-control posture being assembled in real time with asymmetric windows. That asymmetry connects the column this morning: Magnus Honeyfield's piece on APAC's nine-cent storm coverage gap runs alongside this Hormuz reopening because the underlying logic is the same. Protection architecture built for the last risk regime is meeting a new one before the paperwork is signed. Two things to watch before end of day: whether OPEC-plus makes any informal statement on supply before Asia markets open Monday, and whether the Nikkei's early move holds or reverses as Tokyo mid-session approaches.
