Taiwan's Window Narrows While Washington Does Triage

The Iran campaign exposed what Beijing calculated years ago — American precision munitions and strategic attention are finite, and Taiwan is still debating its defense budget.
By Wang Report Chief  •  Saturday, April 25, 2026  •  The Wang Report

One Magazine, Two Wars

The debate about missile inventory depletion is not new, but the Iran scenario put numbers to what had previously been classified anxiety. US strike packages targeting Iranian infrastructure drew down precision munitions at rates that alarmed logistics planners — and that was a contained, relatively predictable campaign. Pentagon planners running Taiwan contingencies have long understood that a high-intensity Taiwan Strait conflict burns through JASSM, Tomahawk, and SM-6 inventory at rates current production cannot sustain past the first two weeks. Hegseth's shoot-to-destroy framing on Hormuz mining was presented as deterrence posture; it also reads as a tacit acknowledgment that kinetic options in a two-front scenario are structurally constrained. A senior defense contact I spoke with at a Singapore security forum last year put it plainly: the US can win one war, or lose two slowly. The Pentagon memo on NATO burden-sharing lands inside this same logic. Washington is not managing alliances — it is doing triage, and the order of priority is becoming legible to anyone who pays attention.

Beijing Reads the Room

Beijing is not watching this passively. The PLA's recent exercises simulating seizure of Taiwan's eastern approaches, the deployment of a drone carrier into the South China Sea, and live-fire activity near Luzon form a coherent operational conditioning program — not a signal, a rehearsal. Xi's simultaneous outreach to Taiwan opposition figures runs a parallel track: demonstrate military credibility while fracturing political will, so that Taipei's internal debate migrates from how to defend to whether defending is viable. The budget paralysis in Taiwan's legislature is not an internal squabble — it is the target. A large regional financial institution I advise raised recently whether Taiwan's political dysfunction should change their cross-strait scenario planning. My answer was that it doesn't alter Beijing's timeline, but it measurably improves Beijing's confidence. A US admiral publicly pressing Taipei to pass its defense appropriation is unusual enough to register; it suggests private conversations have not been productive, and that Washington is now trying public pressure because the quiet channels have not moved the needle.

The hellscape drone concept — flooding the strait with autonomous systems to confound PLA amphibious math — is tactically serious and strategically incomplete. Deterrence requires political will that survives an election cycle, and a guarantor whose strategic attention isn't being rationed across three simultaneous theaters. Beijing has studied both variables carefully and for a long time. The question worth carrying out of this week is whether the window for deterrence through credible, sustained commitment has already passed its optimal point — and whether anyone in Taipei or Washington has honestly done that calculation.
