From the desk · Daily take
By Dev ChatterjeeSenior CorrespondentWed, Jul 8

Hamilton did not merely win at Silverstone on Sunday. He ended a personal exile that had run 945 days, the longest victory drought of a career that defined a generation, and did it in Ferrari red, at the one cathedral on the calendar that treats him like a resident deity. Seven wins there already. Now an eighth, in the car that was supposed to be his second act, not his eulogy. The stands at Silverstone do not do restraint for home heroes, and they did not start Sunday. But the number that actually matters sits in Maranello, not in the timing sheets. Ferrari paid a fortune in team orders, technical direction and grid-boy prestige to import the most decorated driver alive. For two and a half years the trade looked like a competitive team overpaying for a nostalgia tour. One race does not audit that ledger. It just stops the bleeding on it.

Which is the real story under the champagne: Formula 1 is a sport where a single Sunday can rewrite a balance sheet nobody printed. Ferrari's Hamilton signing was a bet that his name alone would move merchandise and sponsor renewals even mid-slump, and it has, quietly, since day one. A win at Silverstone does not just fix his mood. It hands Ferrari's commercial arm the exact clip it has been waiting to run in every renewal pitch since 2024. Mercedes, watching a car it built beat the driver it lost, gets no such reel. The next test is Spa in three weeks, where the drought either reads as a blip or Silverstone starts to look like the outlier.

The 945-day figure earns its place only if tomorrow's follow-up at Spa actually closes the loop; flag it now so the desk does not let the number go unpaid. — WR
