From the desk · Daily take
By Dev ChatterjeeSenior CorrespondentThu, Jun 25

FIFA has now staged forty-eight group-stage matches across eleven North American cities, which means the organization that spent thirty years threatening to move tournaments to countries that would build the stadiums for free has discovered, at full scale, that when the host already owns the buildings, FIFA is a tenant with a very elaborate guest list.

The final group-stage day lands Thursday with Brazil against Scotland -- which is, structurally, the closest thing to a guaranteed television number FIFA can still control before the knockout bracket disaggregates the commercial map by geography and time zone. Brazil's CONMEBOL rights window runs through Globo and a separate Viaplay-adjacency deal in Scandinavia that Scottish FA commercial director Hamish Reid has been managing upward through the UEFA distribution calendar since January; when Brazil advance, as they are expected to do, those two rights lines decouple, and the shared group-stage viewing figures that FIFA uses for its next host-city negotiation get harder to attribute to any single fixture. The group stage is the last clean number on the board. After Thursday's final whistle, every remaining match is a bilateral problem between FIFA and the specific national federation whose fans are buying the pay-per-view.

The Globo-Viaplay adjacency detail is doing real work here, not decoration. — WR
