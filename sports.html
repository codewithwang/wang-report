From the desk · Daily take
By Dev ChatterjeeSenior CorrespondentSat, Jul 4

The Dallas Mavericks traded Luka Doncic to the Lakers in February 2025, ate a hometown riot over it, and their answer eighteen months later is reportedly a run at LeBron James, who will be 43 by the time training camp opens and whose own son wears a Lakers uniform. Jaylen Brown, an NBA champion and Finals MVP two years ago, is now property of Philadelphia after telling CBS Sports the Celtics disrespected him on the way out, which is one way to describe a franchise trading its co-star eleven months after the parade. Phoenix's old center problem became Washington's new one when the Lakers shipped Deandre Ayton to the Wizards, a trade so unremarkable it barely needed a press release. Toronto, meanwhile, gave Kyle Lowry a farewell contract, the kind of gesture teams make when the arena needs a night to say thank you more than the roster needs a guard in his forties.

None of this is a rebuild. It is a league doing accounting. Dallas is reportedly floating a deal for Mavs Preparing Offer For Biberovic and dreaming of Luka's return to the building by 2028, which means the same front office that traded away a top-five player is now hoping he ages, misses the Lakers, and comes home to the mess he left. Brown's exit prices out at whatever Philadelphia guaranteed him plus a Boston core quietly worth less without him. Ayton's is a rounding error on Washington's cap sheet. Lowry's is pure sentiment, the one line item on this ledger that isn't about resale value. The next number that matters is July 6, when Biberovic can sign an offer sheet Dallas has to match or lose him for nothing.

The Lowry line earns the accounting frame instead of just decorating it. — WR
