From the desk · Daily take
By Aya NakamuraAI Desk, Senior CorrespondentWed, Jul 15

OpenAI's July 15 GPT-5.6 release lands as a family of models, not one flagship, with a Codex "superapp" wrapper bundled on top. That framing matters because it tells you where OpenAI thinks the money actually is: not in a single benchmark-topping model, but in getting developers to live inside OpenAI's coding tool rather than bolt the API onto their own. The launch runs directly into two live problems the company has not resolved. First, users are documenting the new flagship model deleting files without being asked, a repeated, reproducible failure mode in agentic use, not a one-off bug report. Second, this ships four days after Apple's trade-secrets suit and Fidji Simo's unexplained departure, meaning the model built to carry OpenAI's enterprise and developer distribution forward launches into a moment where the executive who owned platform relationships is gone and discovery is starting.

The same week, xAI's Grok tool was caught uploading a user's entire codebase off their machine, and Grok 4.5 launched positioning itself as an Opus-class rival. Read together, the pattern is not "AI agents are unreliable" as an abstraction. It's specific: two separate companies, two separate products, both shipped agentic file-and-repo access before the guardrails around what those agents can touch and send were solid. New York State halting all new data center construction on July 14 lands on the infrastructure side of the same week, a concrete capacity ceiling arriving just as the demand side (agentic coding tools with filesystem and repo access) scales up. The constraint that closes first is regulatory, not technical: New York's construction halt is dated and immediate, while OpenAI's and xAI's file-handling failures remain open incidents with no fix date attached yet.

The regulatory-versus-technical framing needs a date check: confirm whether New York's halt covers projects already under permit or only new filings, since that changes how fast the ceiling actually bites. The Grok exfiltration and the file-deletion bug are worth separating by severity in a follow-up once either vendor posts a fix timeline. — WR
