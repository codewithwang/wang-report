From the desk · Daily take
By Aya NakamuraAI Desk, Senior CorrespondentMon, Jul 13

Apple's July 9 trade-secrets suit and Fidji Simo's exit as OpenAI's number two, cited as illness, land four days apart and read as one event, not two. The suit alleges OpenAI engineers pulled proprietary Apple silicon and camera-pipeline code into projects tied to the GPT-5.6 launch on July 9, the same release Microsoft made the default model inside 365 Copilot. Simo's departure removes the executive who sat between Sam Altman and OpenAI's commercial contracts, the person a company puts forward when a partner (Microsoft, in this case) needs a steady hand during a dispute. She leaves without a named successor, which means OpenAI heads into discovery on the Apple suit and the ongoing NYT copyright trial, where it has already been accused of withholding chat logs, with no single executive owning the relationship to its largest distribution partner.

The distribution math is what makes the timing bite. GPT-5.6 becoming the default model in Microsoft 365 Copilot is not a benchmark win, it's OpenAI's revenue engine: every enterprise seat that stays on Copilot without switching models is subscription revenue that funds the next training run, the same logic that ties Anthropic to SpaceX's Colossus cluster and Google to its own GCP capacity. Losing the executive who managed that Microsoft relationship right as two separate courts start compelling internal document production is the kind of gap a company fills only if it can name a Simo-level replacement, and none exists yet. Watch whether OpenAI names a new chief operating counterpart before the Apple suit reaches its first discovery deadline; until then, the Microsoft contract is being managed by a team, not an owner.

The suit and the exit read as one event only if discovery timing forces them together. Watch whether the Apple case and the NYT case land on the same discovery calendar; if they diverge, the no-owner claim weakens to one dispute, not two. — WR
