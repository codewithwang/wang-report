The Week Hong Kong Rebuilt Its Own Walls

A city that spent the week discovering how many of its systems, financial, physical, and administrative, were built for a threat that has already moved on.
Charmaine Lo · News Anchor

Futu went dark for mainland clients on a Tuesday, and the number that mattered wasn't the client count. It was August 3rd, the date HKEX's new futures contract goes live, arriving with the tidy choreography of a demolition crew that shows up right after the eviction notice. Beijing isn't shutting the door on capital markets. It's swapping the locks and keeping the old key as a souvenir.

Someone in Xinjiang finished building a full-scale warship replica this week, and on the same day in Beijing, six generals disappeared from the PLA's leadership roster before lunch. Two stories, one ledger: an institution either sharpening its edge or quietly discovering how much of its middle is rotten. Sixty of a hundred-plus generals gone under Xi is not a purge anymore. It's a renovation with the tenants still inside.

A stress test landed this week showing every major US bank could survive a 2008 rerun, which is a bit like a fire drill that only tests for fires that already happened. Nobody modeled the $300 billion sitting in the shadow banking system next door, because the exam wasn't written to look there. Hong Kong's own banks passed a similar test on similar terms, and the regulator grading the paper knows exactly which questions it left off. Careful readers will notice both tests were open-book, and the bank wrote its own textbook.

At Wang Fuk Court, the tower that never burned left the buyback deal fastest, at 87.5 percent, faster than towers that did. That single fact tells you the fire was never really the variable. The sign-up order is: first-come priority for Kai Tak flats has turned survivors of the same catastrophe into competitors for the same address, which is its own kind of second disaster, just one that moves at the pace of a queue instead of a blaze.

Outdoors, the mercury hit 32 degrees and Hong Kong's event calendar did not blink. Dragon boats drew their crowds on the harbour, sports days ran at full tilt, and a child died at a sports ground because no protocol exists to pause a schedule for heat the way one exists to pause it for a typhoon signal. The city has a number for wind. It does not yet have one for this.

Further out, Seoul pledged $550 billion this week to fix what looks like a chip shortage and is actually a power shortage, the distinction that will decide who trains next year's frontier models and who watches. Washington loosened export controls on Mythos the same week Alibaba's Qwen clone dispute surfaced, reminding everyone that compute maps redraw themselves faster than trade policy can print new copies. And on a pitch in Qatar, Paraguay ambushed Germany on penalties in the shock of this World Cup, landing in the same week that record US ratings quietly confirmed FIFA sold its own broadcast rights for roughly half of what they were worth. Sport, it turns out, keeps finding the bargain that finance missed.

Next week: the first Kai Tak allocation letters go out, and Hong Kong will learn whether a queue can settle what a fire could not.

-- CL
