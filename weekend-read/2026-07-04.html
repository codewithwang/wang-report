The Week Hong Kong Ran the Numbers

From a stress test with an outdated clock to fire survivors racing each other for flats, this week Hong Kong kept grading itself on exams it had already rewritten to pass.
Charmaine Lo · News Anchor

Eighty-five point six percent. That is the signing rate on the Wang Fuk Court buyback, the number the government wanted you to read as consensus. Read the fine print and it is something else: a queue, first-come first-served, fire survivors now competing against each other for the same flats in Kai Tak. Grief does not usually come with a waitlist. This week, it did.

-- CL

Start with the banks, because everyone else did. The stress test said yes, HSBC and the rest could survive a rerun of 2008. What it did not ask is whether they can survive 2026, where the attack clock runs faster than the exam and three hundred billion dollars now sits in the part of the shadow banking system the test does not look at. The Fed built a test for the last war. Wall Street took the trophy anyway. Meanwhile the tower at Wang Fuk Court that never burned, the one with the least damage, left the buyback deal fastest of all. That is not a coincidence. That is a family reading the exit sign before anyone else does.

-- CL

Beijing spent the week doing its own grading, and it did not like everyone's marks. Six generals removed in a single day is the kind of number that either means the People's Liberation Army is being cleaned up or that it is coming apart at the seams, and Beijing is not in the business of telling you which. Same week, a warship replica finished construction in the middle of Xinjiang, landlocked and built anyway, because you train for the war you plan to fight on a coastline you do not yet control. And down in the market plumbing, Beijing rolled out August futures on the exchange while Futu went dark. Call it an accident of scheduling if you like. Nobody in Central called it that.

-- CL

The chip war got a location this week: the power grid. Korea pledged five hundred and fifty billion dollars to fix what everyone kept calling a chip shortage, which is really a shortage of electricity to run the chips once you have them. Washington, for its part, loosened the leash on export controls the same week Alibaba's Qwen clone fight exposed the one thing Chinese labs still cannot buy off a shelf: somewhere to put the model once it is trained. Compute is not a chip anymore. It is a permission slip.

-- CL

A note on ambush and appetite. Paraguay knocked out Germany on penalties, the shock of the tournament, in the same week that record American ratings quietly told FIFA it had sold its own broadcast rights far too cheap. And in Riyadh, the Public Investment Fund took seventy percent of LIV Golf off its own balance sheet while keeping every lever that matters. Not a retreat. A vanishing act, performed for the accountants.

-- CL

And then Saturday, thirty-two degrees, a sports ground with no heat protocol and a crowd on the harbour watching dragon boats because nobody told them to stop either. A child died at that sports ground. No policy paused the calendar for it, and the calendar did not pause itself.

-- CL

Next week the Kai Tak sign-up queue keeps moving, one family's certainty built on another's wait. Watch what Monday does with it.

-- CL
