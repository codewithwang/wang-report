The Week Hong Kong Rebuilt Instead of Repaired

From the Kai Tak buyback queue to the HKEX futures desk, this was a week about who gets to go first and who writes the rules for the line.
Charmaine Lo · News Anchor

Start with a number: 87.5 percent. That is the share of Wang Chi House owners who signed the Wang Fuk Court buyback this week, and it is the highest of any tower in the complex, including the ones that actually burned. The building with the least damage left the deal fastest, which tells you the buyback was never really a damage assessment. It was a queue, and queues reward whoever gets to the desk first, not whoever needs the money most.

Kai Tak runs on the same logic now. The government's sign-up order determines who gets first pick of the replacement flats, so survivors who lost everything are competing on paperwork speed against neighbours who lost far less. Nobody designed this as a cruelty. It is just what happens when a compensation scheme borrows the mechanics of a ticket line.

Beijing ran its own version of first-come-first-served this week, only in reverse: it decided who doesn't get a line at all. The HKEX's new August 3 futures contract and the abrupt shutdown of Futu's mainland operations point at the same renovation. Capital markets in China are not closing. They are being rebuilt so that only the state-sanctioned pipes carry water, and everything else gets condemned. Xi's removal of six generals in a single day reads the same way from a different building: not a purge for its own sake, but a renovation crew deciding who still has keys.

Hong Kong's banks, meanwhile, passed their stress test this week, and the regulator writing the exam knows exactly what it left out. The test still models 2008. The Fed ran the equivalent exercise in Washington and found every major US bank could survive a rerun of the last crisis, which is a bit like grading a driving test on parallel parking the week autonomous trucks entered the highway. The actual exposure, some $300 billion of it sitting in private credit and shadow lending, doesn't sit for the exam. It doesn't have to. It isn't on the syllabus.

The AI desk spent the week on the same question in a different currency: not who has the smartest model, but who has the outlet to run it. Korea pledged $550 billion this week to what it's calling a chip initiative and is actually a power grid initiative, because the chips were never the bottleneck, the electricity was. Washington's rollback of the Mythos export controls reopens the compute map for US labs at the same moment Alibaba's Qwen dispute exposes the one thing China's labs still can't buy off a shelf: somewhere to deploy at scale. Everyone this week was shopping for infrastructure. Nobody was shopping for cleverness.

And on a Saturday in Sha Tin, a sports day ran at full temperature, thirty-two degrees, with no protocol written anywhere to pause it, while several thousand people a few kilometres away watched dragon boats on a harbour that offered no shade either. A child died at the sports ground. The calendar had made no allowance for the weather to be the story. It rarely does, until it is.

Put those threads on one table and they rhyme more than they differ: a fire buyback that rewards the fastest signature over the greatest loss, a capital market rebuilt around who still holds a license, a stress test that grades the crisis already survived, a power grid standing in for the AI race it's supposed to enable, and a calendar with no clause for extreme heat. Hong Kong did not lack rules this week. It had rules for nearly everything. What it lacked, in every one of these rooms, was a rule for the case actually in front of it.

Next week the district's Kai Tak flat allocations move from sign-up to selection, which means the ordering problem stops being theoretical and starts being addresses. -- CL
