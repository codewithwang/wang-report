What the Week Named and Left Uncovered

From the Observatory's extreme-heat warning to a Jerusalem Post report Beijing read before Washington did, the week named every exposure and found no product to match any of them.
Charmaine Lo · News Anchor

The HK$17 entry fee at the Victoria Park pool has not changed in months. The queue was past the second lamppost before half eight this morning, which is where it runs on any extreme-heat Saturday, except this week the Observatory issued the warning on a Monday, and the families who had planned outdoor activities were done rerouting by Wednesday. Birthday parties moved indoors. Football practice was cancelled. By Saturday the outdoor schedule across the city had been quietly revised for four full days without announcement or protest, and the pool queue held all of it: the cancelled picnics, the postponed matches, the morning runs that ended at the nearest air-conditioned lobby. The mall next door had its air-conditioning audible from the pavement. The day still had to be filled. -- CL

May's residential numbers showed a 71.5 per cent rise in sales value. The secondary market, where most families buy, did not move with it. The primary surge ran on wealth-tier money that Beijing's capital controls keep from travelling into second-hand transactions, and the headline number describes a market that most families cannot access. The same structural shape appeared twice more this week: the SFC named AI-enabled attack risks for licensed firms and virtual-asset operators in a circular that stopped at naming, because the insurance products that would transfer those risks do not yet exist in APAC. The $57 billion of catastrophe cover that renews on July 1 will get more expensive; prices on one dollar in three of APAC weather losses are moving. The $125 billion sitting outside that renewal room will stay unpriced, because no one has built the room. -- CL

The Shangri-La Dialogue ranked AI at the top of its threat register this week. A China-linked operation called Dragon Weave hit Taiwanese government and critical infrastructure targets using phishing, stolen credentials, and lateral movement, techniques from a tradecraft manual that predates the AI era by twenty years. The conference was discussing the future threat while the present one ran uninterrupted. Separately, the Jerusalem Post reported that a Chinese missile had downed a U.S. F-15 over Iranian airspace during the Hormuz standoff. Washington has not confirmed the report and has not denied it. What matters is sequence: Beijing was reading it as a Taiwan deterrence data point before Washington had finished deciding how to respond to the drone that triggered Thursday's strikes on Iran. The signal arrived before the frame did, and the strikes came anyway. -- CL

Anthropic filed its IPO documents this week at a valuation implying a $47 billion run rate on enterprise API revenue. The same week, Apple announced that Siri would run on Google's Gemini model across 1.2 billion iPhones. The two announcements together define a market boundary more precisely than either filing does alone: the enterprise API layer is Anthropic's business; the consumer device layer, at the scale that matters, belongs to someone else. Anthropic also called for a global AI pause mechanism, published separately in the same week. The pause call and the IPO are not in contradiction. A pause at the frontier benefits companies already sitting at the frontier. The valuation is the argument, the pause is the hedge, and the 1.2 billion iPhones that are now Gemini's are the thing neither document mentions directly. -- CL

By July 1, the catastrophe reinsurance book will have renewed at higher prices, the Hormuz situation will either have resolved or escalated beyond Thursday's strikes, the BTS Hong Kong presale will have opened and closed, and whoever is standing in the Victoria Park pool queue next Saturday will be doing so in whatever city those things have made. -- CL
