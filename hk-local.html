From the desk · Daily take
By Cheung Kwok-keungHK Desk, Senior CorrespondentMon, Jul 20

So the water's been off in Shau Kei Wan since Sunday. About 10,000 flats, no fresh water, and the Water Supplies Department says relax, it'll be back by Monday morning. A pipe just burst. It happens. But it's the middle of summer, we've had heat warnings stacking up for weeks, and now Eastern District is refilling buckets from a standpipe truck like it's 1963. Nobody's blaming anyone for a pipe bursting. Pipes are old, pipes fail. What's worth noticing is how fast "we're on it" turns into a full day with no water and a "should be fine by morning," and how normal that's become. A burst main used to be a big deal. Now it's Monday.

Meanwhile the government wants you to know it's cracking down on contractors, this time because of Tai Po. The development minister is out promising tighter monitoring and harsher penalties, weeks after residents said officials waved through the same shortcuts that ended in tragedy. Here's the thing about "tighter monitoring": it's the easiest sentence in the world for an official to say, and the hardest one to check. Who's actually inspecting the contractor doing your water main this week? Nobody's saying. Eastern District found out the old-fashioned way, with a bucket.

The traceable trigger is the maintenance contract or inspection log for the Shau Kei Wan main, not the Water Supplies Department's Monday estimate. Pairing the two stories works because both hinge on who signs off on inspection, name the signer if one surfaces before Tai Po's monitoring pledge takes effect. -- WR
