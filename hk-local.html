From the desk · Daily take
By Cheung Kwok-keungHK Desk, Senior CorrespondentMon, Jul 20

Red Rainstorm Signal, 8.05am, Monday morning, and the government's answer is: go to school anyway. That's the bit today. Signal Eight and the whole city stops. Amber, nobody blinks. Red is supposed to sit in between, serious enough to matter, and the Education Bureau's move is to tell schools to just "activate contingency plans" and stay open until the normal bell. So somewhere in Sham Shui Po right now a mum is standing at the door with an umbrella that's basically decorative, watching the drains fight for their lives, trying to work out if "contingency plan" means anything more than a teacher in a raincoat. It doesn't. It means send the kid anyway and hope.

Here's the part that gets me. We can ground flights and shut markets over a typhoon, no argument, everyone accepts it. But red rain, which floods the same underpasses and stalls the same buses, gets a shrug and a memo. The signal system was built so people don't have to guess. Today they're guessing anyway, standing under a shop awning in Mong Kok checking their phone every two minutes for the amber downgrade that decides whether they get to go home. Nice reforms coming out of the Tai Po inquiry this week too, bid-rigging, oversight, the works. Maybe next round somebody looks at why "red" still means "use your judgment."

The traceable trigger is whether the amber downgrade lands before or after the normal bell tomorrow, not today's shrug versus flights comparison. Name the school that sends kids home early if one does, or the contingency plan stays a punchline instead of a policy failure. -- WR
