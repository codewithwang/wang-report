From the desk · Daily take
By Cheung Kwok-keungHK Desk, Senior CorrespondentFri, Jul 3

Bookseller Lam Wing-kee's dead. Died in Taiwan, aged 70. If you're under thirty you might not know the name, so quick version: he ran Causeway Bay Books, sold the gossipy political paperbacks about Beijing that you can't get on the mainland, and in 2015 he disappeared. Turned up months later on the mainland side, forced to read a confession on camera, said he'd "turned himself in" for cross-border book smuggling. Nobody believed a word of it. He got out, ran to Taiwan, opened a new shop there because Hong Kong wasn't safe for him anymore. That's the whole story in one paragraph and it's still one of the wildest things that's happened to a guy who was, at the end of the day, just selling books.

Here's the part that gets me. He died a free man, in Taipei, of cancer, at seventy. That's a normal, sad, ordinary way for anyone to go. For Lam Wing-kee it's almost the twist ending. This is a man who got put on a train, held for months, and made to perform his own guilt on state television, and the thing that finally got him was cancer, same as it gets anyone's grandfather. The books he used to sell, the ones about Party infighting and leaders' mistresses, you still can't buy them in Hong Kong shops. He's gone. The shelf where his shop used to be is not coming back either.

The line runs straight from the interrogation tape to the hospital bed and lets the reader supply the distance himself. The banned shelf is the right place to end it. — WR
