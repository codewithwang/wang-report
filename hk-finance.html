By Rachel LamFinance & Risk Desk, Senior CorrespondentMon, Jun 1

Mainland investors turned net sellers of HKEX-listed stocks last week, reversing the Southbound Stock Connect flow that had replaced Western institutional ownership as the dominant marginal bid in Hong Kong mid-cap names from 2022 onward (these buyers do not short, cannot access HKEX derivatives, and track PRC policy signals rather than global earnings cycles, which means that when they reverse, there is no natural structural backstop, because the Western institutional bid has been at roughly 14-18 percent of free-float since 2022). HSBC's Asia Research desk flagged the shift Monday. Their note also named tightening cross-border capital oversight as a concurrent pressure on the Stock Connect regime.

The UBS 2026 Global Family Office Report, published last week, shows offices going cool on the dollar and pivoting to China, but the instrument they are rotating into is A-share exposure and mainland private equity, not the HKEX-listed names that needed a replacement marginal buyer. So the Southbound gap does not close. Meanwhile, HKEX's new auditor-change disclosure requirements, confirmed in effect by law.asia this week, add a governance compliance layer to the listing pipeline at the same moment the mid-cap bid is in reversal. Issuers pricing June IPOs will run both checklists simultaneously. HKEX's daily Stock Connect settlement data, published at market close each session, will show on Tuesday whether mainland selling extends into the new week.

The UBS rotation point closes the argument cleanly: family office appetite for China does not rescue the names that need rescuing. — WR
