By Rachel LamFinance & Risk Desk, Senior CorrespondentSat, Jun 27

HKEX equity markets closed Friday with the Hang Seng Index off 1.76 percent on the session (Bloomberg terminal data, June 27), the weakest close in 12 months, as Nvidia's single-day drop pulled semiconductor and AI-adjacent names across the entire index rather than concentrating the damage in the usual tech cluster.

So the day's second story is more durable than the first. HKEX's Index Services division is moving to build proprietary AI-themed benchmarks, per the SCMP filing Saturday, which puts the exchange itself on the revenue side of the same thematic that just repriced its market by 1.76 percent in a session -- the index licensing business is countercyclical to the equities desk in exactly this way, because benchmark construction demand peaks when allocators are resetting their thematic exposures, not when they are adding to them. The T+1 extended-session optimization goes live July 20, per the Moomoo regulatory notice, and the first real read on whether that change moves institutional participation in AI-linked names will come from HKEX's own monthly market statistics release, due in mid-August.

The countercyclical read on index licensing is the correct frame and most desks would have missed it. — WR

From the desk · Daily take
By Magnus HoneyfieldFinance & Risk Desk, Senior Correspondent (Risk)Sat, Jun 27

Swiss Re's sigma No. 1 / 2026 puts APAC natural-catastrophe insured losses at $68 billion for 2025, against total economic losses of $161 billion. The protection gap, the share of weather losses that no insurance covers, ran at 58 percent. Swiss Re's own reinsurance segment wrote roughly $14 billion in property-catastrophe premiums globally last year, which means the firm quantifying the gap is simultaneously pricing the cover that would close it. The 2025 wet season produced the Philippines' worst flooding in a decade, with Typhoon Karding alone accounting for $4.2 billion in economic loss and less than $300 million in insured claims, according to the Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration damage assessments and industry loss estimates from Aon's Q4 2025 catastrophe report. That is a recovery rate below seven percent in a country where non-life insurance penetration sits at 1.1 percent of GDP, per Philippine Insurance Commission figures for 2025.

The renewal cycle is the pressure point. January 2026 Southeast Asia property-catastrophe treaty renewals came in at flat to plus-five percent on risk-adjusted rates, per Guy Carpenter's January 2026 renewal report, which is soft by the standards of a year that cost the market $68 billion. Softening rates extend the protection gap by making parametric cover, the type of policy that pays out on a named index rather than an assessed claim, harder to price competitively against traditional indemnity products for sovereign and municipal buyers. The Philippines' Project NOAH parametric pilot, covering 25 municipalities in the Cagayan Valley, is due its first full actuarial review by the Insurance Commission of the Philippines in September 2026, which will determine whether the programme scales to the 78 provinces the Department of Finance has identified as priority coverage zones.

The conflict-of-interest line on Swiss Re is the right place to open and Magnus holds it without letting it become the story. — WR
