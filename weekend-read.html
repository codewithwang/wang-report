What the Queue Already Knows

A week of named risks and missing instruments: Beijing's deterrence geometry, the SFC's AI circular, and a property surge that landed in the wrong tier.
Charmaine Lo · News Anchor

Victoria Park pool queued two deep at the HK$17 gate before eight this morning. The families in it had run the same calculation the Observatory's extreme-heat bulletin forces every June: outside is not viable, the nearest mall will not open for two more hours, the pool costs HK$17 and is already there, and so the concrete and the water win again, as they do on every Saturday the temperature crosses 36 before nine and the weekend has nowhere else to go. The week's other tallies were less simple. Several of them were missing a product entirely. -- CL

The Jerusalem Post reported Thursday that a Chinese missile had downed a US F-15 over Iranian airspace. No government confirmed it, and none denied it with conviction -- which is the operative structure: a plausible kill in a permissive environment sets a price on air-superiority operations that defense planners on both sides will now work from, and Washington's difficulty in forming a rebuttal tells you something about how seriously the scenario was already being modeled. Dragon Weave ran Taiwan government-contractor operations the same week, targeting procurement offices with stolen credentials and document drops. It used spearphishing. Spearphishing from 2015. -- CL

The SFC named AI-enabled attack risks on Tuesday -- deepfake fraud, AI-assisted phishing, automated market manipulation against licensed firms and VASPs -- and the circular reached every covered entity in Hong Kong by end of business, eight pages of specific scenarios and no accompanying insurance product to cover any of them. The cyber insurance market across APAC does not sell what the circular describes, which places the SFC's named risk in the same structural position as the July 1 reinsurance renewal: a regulator or an actuary has priced something real, and the product layer below has not caught up. One dollar in three of APAC catastrophe losses will cost more after July 1. The other two dollars remain outside any treaty room. -- CL

May's home-sales-value figure came in at 71.5% above last May, and the headline ran as though the HK property market had recovered. The secondary market, where most HK families actually transact -- the third-hand walk-up in Sham Shui Po, the resale flat in Tuen Mun -- has not moved, and the reason is that the mainland marginal buyer who historically closed the bid-ask gap is behind Beijing's capital controls with no clear exit window, and the luxury tier is what the aggregate data looks like when that buyer is absent. 71.5% is a fact about a different tier. Most of the families in this morning's queue are not in it. -- CL

Anthropics filed its IPO paperwork this week, priced on enterprise API revenue at a $47 billion run rate, and Apple confirmed on the same news cycle that Siri's intelligence layer runs on Gemini across 1.2 billion devices. The two announcements draw the same line from opposite ends: the enterprise API business is large enough to sustain a company at that valuation, and the consumer device layer belongs for now to Google, which means Anthropic's addressable market is precisely the ground Apple has not offered it, and both parties have said so in public. The boundary is clear. The July 1 deadline is less so. -- CL

The S&P 500 closed Friday at its worst mark of the year, Putin declined Zelenskyy, and the US Senate passed an immigration bill, none of which asked Hong Kong for an immediate answer. What does ask for an answer -- the SFC circular on AI attacks, the reinsurance window, and the question of whether the secondary property market moves before Beijing's capital controls do -- arrives on July 1, the week after next, when the missing products will either exist or be confirmed not to. The pool queue will still form. The gate opens at seven. -- CL
