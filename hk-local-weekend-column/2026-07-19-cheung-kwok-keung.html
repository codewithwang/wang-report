The Water Department's Tai Po Moment

The same week officials vowed to end self-policed contractor checks after Tai Po, an 18-inch government pipe proved you don't need a contractor to defer maintenance.
張 國 強 · Cheung Kwok-keung HK Desk, Senior Correspondent Sunday, July 19, 2026

Six A.M. In Shau Kei Wan

Roger Wong walked out Sunday morning and called the ruptured main 'old material that has been used for many, many years,' which is the water department's polite way of saying the pipe was tired. About 10,000 households across Yiu Tung Estate, Tung Yuk Court, Ming Wah Dai Ha and Shau Kei Wan Main Street East woke up to nothing coming out of the tap from around 6am, and by midday they were queuing at tanker trucks the way you'd queue for the last discounted mooncakes on Mid-Autumn morning, except this queue doesn't end when you check out. The main itself was 18 inches of cast iron sitting under a tram-line intersection, and when it went, some of that water didn't even make it into anyone's bucket. It ran into the storm sewers and out to sea, water the tanker trucks then had to replace litre for litre. Restoration is pencilled in for 6am Monday. If that holds, Shau Kei Wan gets its water back roughly 24 hours after it lost it, which the department will file as a job well done. It is, by the metric that matters most on a Sunday: did the tap turn back on. The bigger question is why this particular pipe was still cast iron in 2026.

The Replacement That Stopped

Here is the part the tanker trucks don't show you. Between 2000 and 2015, the Water Supplies Department ran a HK$23.6 billion programme that replaced about 3,000km of aged mains, a proper, funded, government-owns-this-one campaign. Since it wrapped, the department has replaced roughly 240km in a decade, per a Legislative Council Development Panel paper from July 2025, which is not a rounding error, it's an eightfold drop in pace. Left mostly untouched: about 36km of cast iron and asbestos-cement mains over 50 years old, sitting under some of the busiest roads in the city, failing at a rate of 3.3 per 100km against 0.4 for ductile iron and 0.2 for polyethylene. The highest-risk pipe in the network is the slowest one to get swapped out. The same week this main gave way, Housing Secretary Winnie Ho Wing-yin was promising an end to the 'self-policed' contractor checks the Tai Po fire inquiry blamed for deferred maintenance in private buildings. Fair enough. But nobody self-polices quite like a government department marking its own pipe replacement homework.

Winnie Ho's contractor crackdown will presumably come with inspections, deadlines, someone checking someone else's work. Ask the Water Supplies Department who checks its own replacement schedule and the honest answer is nobody outside the department, on a rolling contract with itself. Shau Kei Wan gets its water back Monday morning. The 36km of legacy pipe under everyone else's road does not get replaced by then, or visibly, by any announced deadline.
