ENCIRCLEMENT BY OTHER MEANS

China does not need to announce an encirclement to conduct one. At Scarborough Shoal, ships and a physical barrier are now blocking the entrance to a disputed feature that Beijing has contested for over a decade but never fully sealed. Simultaneously, live-fire drills have been scheduled across the South China Sea for April 15–17. Philippine troops stationed at contested outposts are reportedly being starved out via cyanide-laced waters, a charge Beijing dismisses as a farce — which is exactly what you would say if you were doing it and it was working. Zoom out to Taiwan and the picture clarifies further: experts are publicly modeling blockade scenarios that never require a single soldier to land on the island, Taipei is drilling to defend its energy supply routes, and active and former military officers have just been indicted for leaking intelligence to Beijing. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: a country conducting physical blockades, live-fire exercises, alleged sabotage operations, and running intelligence assets inside a rival's military is being described by each individual act as a routine sovereignty claim. The thesis of incremental encirclement does not require proving intent. It only requires reading the sequence.

THREE BIRDS, ONE BLOCKADE

The Hormuz blockade has a feature that any strategist would envy: it does three things at once. It pressures Tehran. It squeezes the energy imports of China and India, two countries Washington would very much like to see paying higher economic costs. And it has created a global natural gas shortage that is generating a windfall for American LNG exporters. The IMF, not prone to alarm, has warned that escalation could trigger a global recession. The IEA has cut oil forecasts. US officials describe the blockade as fully implemented while signaling a diplomatic off-ramp exists — the language of coercive diplomacy, where the door is left open precisely so the adversary retains a reason to walk through it rather than break it down. What makes this geopolitically durable is the asymmetry: the US does not import Iranian oil. China and India do. The trade war has already made Chinese manufacturers recalculate supply chains; an energy squeeze layered on top is a second front opened without a second declaration. Whether the compounding was designed or emerged from circumstance is the kind of question that will keep historians busy for decades. The market effects are happening now regardless.

PYONGYANG'S SECOND-STRIKE GRADUATION

There is a distinction between having nuclear weapons and having a credible second-strike capability, and North Korea appears to have spent the last several years quietly closing that gap. Kim Jong Un personally overseeing missile tests from a new naval destroyer is not a publicity exercise — it is an operational readiness demonstration. Naval delivery platforms survive a first strike in ways that fixed land-based systems cannot; if you can launch from a warship at sea, a pre-emptive disarming attack becomes vastly more complicated to plan and even harder to guarantee. The IAEA chief's statement that Pyongyang is showing a very serious increase in production capability is notable less for its content — analysts have tracked this trajectory for years — than for the forum. When the UN's nuclear watchdog speaks in those terms publicly, it is signaling that the window for comfortable denial has closed. The implications extend well beyond the peninsula. A nuclear-armed state with a survivable second strike does not merely deter invasion; it creates a protective umbrella under which conventional provocations become substantially harder to punish. The region's other capitals are doing that calculation, and so are the capitals that write alliance guarantees.

THE FALLBACK BECOMES THE PLAN

The most efficient way to accelerate European defense autonomy has apparently been to elect an American president who describes NATO as an alliance that was not there and won't be in future. Europe's response, as reported by the Wall Street Journal, is not to argue the point but to quietly engineer a version of the alliance that functions on European structures if the American guarantor withdraws. This is what rational actors do when a security guarantee becomes contingent rather than absolute. Ukraine is not waiting for the institutional architecture to settle: a joint drone-production deal with Germany and the largest-ever UK drone shipment suggest Kyiv is racing to build its own industrial base before any political shift forecloses the option. Russia's overnight assault with over 300 drones and missiles — targeting infrastructure, leaving darkness across the country — provides a backdrop that makes the urgency self-explanatory. There is something worth noting in the arithmetic: the alliance being disparaged is simultaneously the institution that has funded a war degrading Russian conventional capacity more than any post-Cold War development. The fallback plan Europe is building does not replace that history. It insures against the possibility that history becomes the only thing left.
