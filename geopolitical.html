THE DEAL THAT WASN'T

Iran and the United States concluded a second round of nuclear talks in Rome on Saturday — except, wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: Iran's state media spent the weekend denying that any second round had been agreed to at all. The US said one thing; Tehran said another. Both cannot be right, and the gap between those two positions is not a communication error. It is, as it has always been, the negotiation itself. Washington is threatening to end a ceasefire unless a deal materialises within days. Tehran is managing domestic audiences for whom any concession carries political cost. European allies, watching from the sidelines, are warning that an inexperienced American team may be sprinting toward a headline-grabbing framework that papers over the technical gaps — the centrifuges, the enrichment levels, the verification architecture — that actually determine whether a deal holds. The pattern is familiar: an administration eager for a foreign-policy win, a counterpart skilled at extracting concessions in exchange for showing up, and allies left to manage the fallout when the framework turns out to be thinner than advertised. The Strait of Hormuz is still being threatened. Oil markets are still pricing in risk. The urgency is real even if the agreement, apparently, is not.

THE PRICE OF PATIENCE

Russia is not in a hurry. That is the message from Moscow as Ukraine enters its fourth year of the war, and it is a message delivered with the quiet confidence of a side that has learned to absorb economic pain while its adversary's political support erodes. Russia was this week the beneficiary of a US decision to renew a waiver on Russian oil sanctions — a concession framed as relief for countries absorbing price shocks from the Iran war, but one that, by Ukraine's own calculation, could direct $10 billion toward Russia's war machine. Kyiv struck back in the only language available to it: drones against Russian oil refineries, hours after the waiver was announced. The symbolism is not subtle. Meanwhile, a NATO admiral has acknowledged what analysts have been saying for some time — there is no clear winner possible in this war — and Russia's leadership, in a rare moment of candor, admitted the economy is in trouble. But trouble, in this context, is relative. An economy that can sustain a war indefinitely at reduced output is not losing; it is simply paying a different price than its opponents expected. The Atlantic has concluded that Ukraine has finally given up on Trump. Moscow does not appear to have noticed, or particularly minded.

THE WATCHERS

Every major conflict produces a secondary audience of states recalibrating their own positions against the distraction it creates. North Korea has fired ballistic missiles twice in recent weeks — not because the timing is tactically relevant to the Korean peninsula, but because it is tactically relevant to a world in which one superpower is managing simultaneous crises in the Middle East, Eastern Europe, and its own domestic politics. The launches are a form of communication: we are still here, our programme continues, and your attention is elsewhere. China faces a more complex calculus. A planned bilateral summit in mid-May in Beijing was already freighted with tariff tensions; the Iran war has added a new layer, with Washington and Beijing holding structurally divergent interests in how the conflict resolves and what energy prices follow. Ukraine's president, separately, has warned that Russia may pull Belarus back into active participation in the war — an escalation that would threaten Kyiv's northern flank and stretch NATO's eastern posture further than the alliance has publicly planned for. None of these actors are driving the headline conflicts. All of them are watching the exits, measuring the room, and adjusting the ask accordingly.

STAGFLATION'S QUIET RETURN

The last time the global economy faced stagflation — slow growth and high inflation simultaneously — it took a decade and a brutal interest rate shock to resolve it. The IMF is now flagging Middle East conflict as a driver of reduced global growth and elevated recession risk. Bloomberg has revived the term explicitly. The mechanism is not complicated: energy price shocks raise input costs across every sector of a modern economy, central banks face the impossible choice between fighting inflation and supporting growth, and the political tolerance for pain is considerably lower now than it was in the 1970s. The US decision to renew a waiver on Russian oil sanctions — designed to calm markets rattled by the Iran war — illustrates how little room policymakers have to maneuver. Every lever has a cost. Releasing pressure on Russian exports to stabilise oil prices means financing, however indirectly, the war in Ukraine. Letting prices run means accelerating the recession the IMF is already flagging. The global economy is not yet in stagflation, but the conditions are assembling with a patience and structural coherence that the day-to-day headlines rarely convey. The 1970s took a long time to look like the 1970s, too.
