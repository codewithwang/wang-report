THE TAIWAN ANXIETY INDEX

A Chatham House paper published this week makes the arithmetic uncomfortably clear: a Taiwan Strait crisis would cause far greater global economic damage than a Strait of Hormuz closure. Taiwan sits at the nexus of semiconductor supply chains that underpin everything from smartphones to fighter jets. Yet a fresh poll finds that a majority of Taiwanese now doubt the United States would intervene militarily if China moved. The gap between what Taiwan is worth to the global economy and what its defenders are prepared to risk to protect it is the central strategic question of the decade.

That gap is widening. The US Pacific commander argued this week that victory over Iran is necessary to deter a Chinese move on Taiwan — a framing that treats Washington's credibility as a single interconnected balance sheet, where each withdrawal increases the cost of the next one. AUKUS offers some structural reassurance, with submarine rotations in Australia on track for 2027. But rotations in 2027 are cold comfort if Taiwan's public has already concluded, in 2026, that the check isn't good.

CHINA'S MARITIME CHOREOGRAPHY

There is a certain elegance to China's coercion toolkit when you step back far enough to see the whole canvas. This week: the Type 076 drone carrier begins South China Sea drills — the first operational deployment of a platform designed to project air power at sea without the political cost of a conventional carrier air wing. Simultaneously, PLA warships make rare transits off southwest Japan, close enough to require a response but calibrated to avoid the threshold that would formalize Japan's security posture.

The diplomatic flanks are covered in parallel. Beijing blocked South Korea's intelligence chief from visiting, a pointed reminder that peninsula security is not Seoul's alone to manage. China threatened the Philippines with energy cutoffs over US military drills near Taiwan. These are not random pressure events. They are sequenced, targeted, and designed to impose costs on every node in the US coalition without creating a single incident serious enough to galvanize it. The sea outpost construction reported by the NYT this week adds the physical infrastructure to sustain the pattern indefinitely.

THE HORMUZ PREMIUM

Oil crossed $100 this week. The Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil transits, saw Iranian actors seize two vessels — a data point that arrived while the White House described itself as satisfied with the blockade situation. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: ships were seized in a critical global chokepoint, the administration declared satisfaction, and then oil hit $100 while the IEA warned of the largest supply disruption in recent memory — all while a ceasefire was being extended through negotiations.

The Houthis, who had gone quiet during the ceasefire window, signaled a return to operations with warnings of broader regional escalation. Air fares have already risen nearly 25% as flight paths are rerouted around conflict zones. The ceasefire is holding in the political sense — there are talks, there are declarations — but markets are pricing the physical reality rather than the diplomatic one. A $100 oil price is a sanctions regime by other means: it transfers hundreds of billions from consuming economies to producing ones, with no legislation required and no veto available.

EUROPE'S RELUCTANT CONSENSUS

Hungary blinked. After months of blocking EU financial support for Ukraine, Budapest dropped its opposition this week, allowing a €90 billion loan package to proceed alongside a fresh round of Russia sanctions. The vote was less a strategic consensus than a political exhaustion — every available concession was extracted, the runway ran out, and the veto was lifted. The money moves. The question is whether it moves fast enough.

The Dutch warning that Russia could be ready for NATO conflict within a year of any Ukraine settlement is the context that makes the loan's timing feel both urgent and insufficient. Kyiv's simultaneous push for a direct summit with Moscow suggests Ukraine senses that the US-led peace architecture is stalling — and that a face-to-face meeting, however unpromising, might inject momentum that Washington is no longer providing. The proposal is partly a signal to European partners: we are trying everything, do not let this loan be the last thing you do for us. Whether Budapest's capitulation marks the beginning of European cohesion or merely the end of its internal negotiating drama is a question the next six months will settle.
