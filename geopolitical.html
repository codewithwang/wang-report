THE STRAIT STAYS CLOSED (SORT OF)

Iran declares the Strait of Hormuz open. Tanker tracking shows few ships moving. A nuclear deal is reportedly close. Wait — let me make sure I understand this correctly: the strait is simultaneously open, closed in practice, and the subject of active diplomatic resolution. This is the Iranian posture in miniature.

Tehran's logic is coherent even if the optics are not. Declaring the strait open costs nothing and hands diplomats something to cite. The ships not moving costs Iran nothing either — the blockade is American, not Iranian. So Iran collects credit for de-escalation while the actual chokepoint remains frozen. Meanwhile, the BBC found Tehranis who do not believe they lost this war. That framing matters: a population that feels undefeated will accept extended hardship as the price of continued defiance, not as the consequence of defeat.

The nuclear deal language from Washington creates its own ambiguity. A deal that is close is a deal that has not landed, and in the interim, every party with leverage has an incentive to stay in the room without resolving anything. Iran enriches. The strait stays nominally open. Carriers park in the Gulf. This is the Middle East equilibrium: every actor performing the gestures of resolution while none are ready to close the file.

RUSSIA'S ESCALATION LADDER

Russia's deadliest drone and missile strike in months killed 18 Ukrainians this week. A senior Russian official simultaneously published a list of 23 European and UK facilities manufacturing drones for Ukraine, describing them as legitimate military targets. Both moves belong to the same rhetorical architecture: keep escalating the cost of Western support without crossing the threshold that triggers a direct response.

The target list is the more revealing instrument. Publishing it is a threat, not a military plan — the point is to make European defense ministers calculate whether a drone factory in their constituency is worth the political exposure. This is information operations dressed as military doctrine, and it has worked before. The calculation inside several European capitals has already shifted; the list just makes that pressure legible.

Ukraine's concern that Russia is maneuvering to pull Belarus back into the conflict adds a second front to that calculus. Belarus as a re-entry point changes the geometry of the northern front in ways that ninety billion euros in EU loans cannot directly address. Kyiv watches the map as carefully as it watches the money. One tells you what is possible; the other tells you for how long.

THE TAIWAN ENCIRCLEMENT, DOMAIN BY DOMAIN

China's characterization of its own military drills around Taiwan as entirely justified and reasonable is, at minimum, consistent. The question is what consistent means when the drills are continuous, the information war is running in parallel, and the regional coalition against them is quietly assembling.

Japan sent a warship through the Taiwan Strait this week and is deploying 1,400 troops to the US-Philippine Balikatan exercise for the first time. Beijing registered formal objections on both counts. Those objections are now routine — a diplomatic tax on each incremental step that Tokyo and Manila are clearly prepared to keep paying. The more interesting signal is in the pattern: Japan is operationalizing its 2022 defense posture revision in real time, and each exercise is a rehearsal for something that three years ago Tokyo would not have publicly contemplated.

Meanwhile, the information operation Reuters documented is low-cost and durable. Using Taiwanese voices to seed doubt among Taiwanese audiences — about identity, about the cost of resistance, about whether the mainland is really the adversary — is a slower instrument than carrier battle groups but arguably harder to counter. Military drills can be answered with military drills. Narratives planted inside a society are a different problem entirely.

THE DISTRACTION DIVIDEND

The United States has a second supercarrier in the Middle East. The South China Sea has fewer American ships than it has had in years. These two facts are not coincidental.

Every carrier deployed to the Gulf is a carrier not patrolling the first island chain. China's acceleration in the South China Sea — the construction, the patrols, the systematic harassment of Philippine vessels — has been framed explicitly in a new report as opportunistic: Washington is managing multiple partial crises simultaneously, and Beijing is moving. Ukraine's ambassador voiced the same structural complaint from the other direction, expressing hope the Iran war would end so attention would return to Kyiv. Two theaters, one diagnosis.

The planned summit between Washington and Beijing, reported as potentially postponed again, adds another layer. A meeting that keeps not happening is not diplomacy — it is the appearance of diplomacy, which is politically easier to sustain but produces less. China has limited incentive to accelerate a meeting while its counterpart is overextended. Showing up to the table when the other side has too many open tabs is poor negotiating. Waiting until the bill comes due is better. The question is who defines when the bill is due, and on which front.
