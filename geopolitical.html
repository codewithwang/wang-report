BEIJING'S CONVENIENT WAR

US intelligence now assesses that China has shipped missiles to Iran and permitted companies under its jurisdiction to supply Tehran with dual-use materials feeding military production. This is either a dramatic escalation or, from a certain vantage point, a fairly logical expression of interests: every American drone lost over the Middle East is one not circling the Taiwan Strait, every carrier group repositioned toward the Persian Gulf is one absent from South China Sea freedom-of-navigation exercises, and every diplomatic calorie consumed in Islamabad is one not spent coordinating Pacific deterrence with nervous regional partners. The Iran war diverts American military attention from Asia at the precise moment Beijing is doubling warship patrols around Taiwan and consolidating positions in contested waters. China need not have engineered any of this to benefit comprehensively from all of it. That said, the intelligence suggesting active missile shipments implies something beyond passive beneficiary status — a willingness to deepen the American entanglement rather than merely observe it, which is a different kind of strategic calculation and one that the upcoming Trump-Xi summit will have to address without the benefit of a quiet regional backdrop.
