THE HUNDRED-DOLLAR STRAIT

The Strait of Hormuz is twenty-one miles wide at its narrowest point and handles roughly a fifth of global oil trade. This week Iran reminded everyone of this arithmetic by seizing two ships — somewhat awkwardly, during an active ceasefire extension. Oil crossed $100. The EU issued a fuel shortage warning. Japan and South Korea began reconsidering supply chains they had spent decades treating as settled fact.

The Houthis signaled a return to broader regional escalation — which is the polite way of saying that whatever diplomatic architecture was being assembled appears to have a structural problem. Iran's leverage here is not primarily military; it is topological. A strait that narrow, through which that much energy flows, does not require a great navy to threaten — only credible presence and tolerable risk appetite.

The downstream effect is underappreciated. Japan and South Korea are holding energy import calculations against military posture calculations and finding the columns don't balance. Europe, watching from the other end of the supply chain, is discovering that a conflict it considered peripheral is repricing its winter. The ceasefire extension is an interesting document: it extends a pause while the object of that pause continues pressing.

FACTS ON THE WATER

There is a pattern worth naming. This week: a Chinese aircraft carrier transited the Taiwan Strait for the first time since December; PLA warships made rare passes off southwest Japan; China's new Type 076 drone carrier moved toward South China Sea training drills; China blocked entrance to a disputed shoal with ships and a physical barrier; and Beijing threatened to withhold emergency energy from the Philippines over US military exercises near Taiwan. Japan joined those exercises anyway, drawing the expected response.

Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly. These are not unrelated events. They are a coordinated pressure campaign across multiple domains simultaneously — military presence, physical access denial, economic coercion — each individually defensible as routine operations or proportionate responses to provocation.

The New York Times reports China is building new outposts at sea. The shoal imagery suggests infrastructure is following military posture. What emerges is a fait accompli strategy that requires no single dramatic confrontation — just accumulated facts on the water, steadily, until the map changes. The novel element is the Type 076, a drone carrier that did not exist as a category three years ago, now operationalizing in the very waters whose legal status Beijing contests. The doctrine and the hardware are arriving together.

THREE LEGS OF DETERRENCE

A poll published this week found that Taiwanese have deep doubts about US military protection. This is not a surprise. A Pacific commander told the Washington Times that victory over Iran was needed to deter a Chinese attack on Taiwan — meaning US credibility on Taiwan is now explicitly contingent on the outcome of a separate war in the Middle East. The credibility chain is long and getting longer.

Taiwan's internal politics are doing their own work in the meantime. A polarized political environment threatens to reverse the resilience gains Taiwan has made against Chinese coercion. This is worth pausing on. Taiwan has invested significantly in civil defense, supply chain diversification, and military modernization. Political polarization is the vector through which those investments can be hollowed out without a shot fired: delayed procurement cycles, contested defense budgets, messaging incoherence that feeds precisely the information environment Beijing cultivates.

The deterrence logic for Taiwan has always required three things to be simultaneously true: US commitment is credible, Taiwan's will to resist is credible, and the cost-benefit calculation for Beijing stays unfavorable. The poll and the polarization analysis together suggest two of those three legs are wobbling. The third depends partly on the first two.

EUROPE'S TWO CLOCKS

The Dutch defense establishment warned this week that Russia could be ready for a NATO conflict within a year of the Ukraine war ending — a timeline that implies the war's conclusion is not the risk, but the starting gun for the next problem. The EU approved a €90 billion loan for Ukraine, breaking a funding deadlock. Zelenskyy is simultaneously pushing for a direct summit with Putin, framing it as a way to jolt stalled US-led peace efforts.

These three facts sit uneasily together. Europe is funding Ukraine at scale while its own defense establishments are modeling what comes next. The loan is not a peace dividend; it is a continuation of the position that Ukraine's survival is a European security interest. The Russia-ready-for-NATO warning, taken seriously, means the window between a Ukraine settlement and the next potential confrontation is shorter than most European capitals have publicly acknowledged.

The summit push is the most politically interesting element. A bilateral Zelenskyy-Putin meeting reframes the negotiation on terms Moscow has always preferred, bypassing the US-brokered multilateral framework. Whether that is a concession to realism or a tactical choice to regain agency from a distracted Washington is unclear. What is clear is that European money has become the most reliable constant in an equation where US attention is split across Iran, Taiwan, and a domestic trade war.
