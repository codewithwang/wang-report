BEIJING'S WINDOW OF DISTRACTION

The strategic logic is almost too clean to be accidental. While American carriers maneuver in the Persian Gulf and Washington's attention oscillates between Tehran and the next set of talks that will produce nothing, Beijing appears to be running a quiet checklist. An island seized here. Sixteen warplanes dispatched near Taiwan there — timed, with notable precision, to coincide with Xi's meeting with a Taiwanese opposition figure, lest anyone miss the symbolism. Meanwhile, the Philippines opened a coast guard district command on Thitu Island in the South China Sea to an absence of immediate Chinese reaction that itself speaks volumes: Beijing reserves its fury strategically, not reflexively. US intelligence now indicates China is preparing to deliver air defense systems to Iran, which is either arms proliferation or the construction of a durable relationship with a state Washington has been bombing — perhaps both. The United States has reportedly pulled military assets from the Pacific to support Middle East operations, a reallocation that analysts are now cataloguing as a gift. The thesis of strategic competitors exploiting great-power distraction is not new. What is new is the density of the evidence accumulating in a single week.

THE IRAN QUAGMIRE DEEPENS

Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: the United States and Iran sat down in Islamabad for talks, declared a stalemate over enrichment control, and then Israel struck Tehran while Washington warned of fresh strikes if negotiations fail — all within the same weekend. The Strait of Hormuz is tense enough that the United Kingdom convened a dedicated summit on it. Hezbollah, which multiple observers declared crippled after the previous round of conflict, has resumed operations at a scale that is causing its own set of recalibrations. Iran, for its part, appears to be attempting to assess the economic damage of sustained warfare while simultaneously searching for leverage to survive it. The pattern is familiar: a conflict that was supposed to be surgical and decisive has produced a theatre of operations that is expensive, ambiguous, and very difficult to leave. American drone losses are mounting. The Iranian economy is absorbing punishment but not collapsing. Neither side has a clean theory of victory. The diplomacy produces stalemates. The military operations produce news cycles. The exit strategy remains, as it so often does in the region, theoretical.

TAIWAN'S ARITHMETIC PROBLEM

The top US diplomat in Taiwan issued the standard admonition — China should abandon threats, talk to Taipei's leaders, avoid misunderstanding — which registers as diplomatic filler unless something reinforces it. What reinforces it this week is the dissonant note struck by an American wargame finding that the current US Air Force mix cannot defeat a Chinese invasion of Taiwan by 2035. A US general simultaneously asserted that China has no say in American arms sales to Taiwan, which is constitutionally unambiguous but strategically incomplete: the question is not whether the United States can sell weapons but whether the weapons, the doctrine, and the political will can be assembled into a coherent deterrent before the capability gap closes. Sixteen Chinese warplanes operating near Taiwan in a single day is not itself a capability gap — it is a demonstration that the pressure campaign runs continuously, indifferent to whatever else is on Beijing's diplomatic calendar. The wargame warning, the warplane sorties, and the diplomatic boilerplate exist simultaneously and in plain sight. The reader may weigh which of the three carries the most information about the trajectory of this situation.

UKRAINE'S ARITHMETIC OF WAITING

The Kremlin announced an Orthodox Easter ceasefire. Ukrainian drone brigades — one of which reportedly killed 8,000 Russian troops in a single month, a figure worth sitting with — did not pause. Russian strikes continued regardless. Both Moscow and Kyiv have apparently signaled, through separate statements, that an end to the war may be close, which is a sentence requiring careful unpacking: parties to a conflict expressing simultaneous openness to peace while conducting simultaneous military operations is less a contradiction than a description of how most negotiations that actually produce settlements tend to proceed. Zelensky has framed a small window for peace, an image implying both urgency and conditionality. The choreography matters: a ceasefire that collapses within hours can still function as a signal that neither party wants to be seen publicly as the obstacle to talks, particularly with American pressure — such as it currently is, and whatever form it currently takes — hovering in the background. NATO's internal debate about its own coherence continues in parallel, with the British prime minister making the case for the alliance's mutual value at a moment when that case apparently still requires active making.
