THE TAIWAN WINDOW NARROWS

China is not subtly signaling anymore. While US and Philippine forces run Balikatan 2026, Beijing stages parallel naval drills, conducts eight PLA sorties around Taiwan in a single reporting window, and deploys its Type 076 drone carrier — whose electromagnetic launch system represents a genuine capability leap, not an incremental improvement — to the South China Sea. Naval War College analysts describe PLA amphibious assault drills as markedly more realistic and sophisticated. Xi, meeting a Taiwanese opposition figure, offers the familiar line about unity. It lands differently when the carrier group is on AIS.

Meanwhile the US emerges from its Iran campaign with a missile stockpile problem analysts are no longer whispering about. The House has set aside $500 million in military aid for Taiwan, which sounds substantial until measured against what a contested-strait scenario actually consumes. The Navy's hellscape drone concept — flooding the Indo-Pacific with autonomous systems to complicate PLA planning — is being discussed publicly, which is itself a signal of how constrained conventional deterrence options have become.

The operating assumption among planners has always been that ambiguity buys time. What this week's coverage suggests, read together, is that the ambiguity is thinning on both sides simultaneously. China's drills grow more operationally realistic as US readiness signals grow more mixed. Whether that perception is accurate matters less than whether Beijing has updated its priors accordingly.

THE CODE NOBODY WANTS TO SIGN

The South China Sea Code of Conduct has been under negotiation for roughly two decades, which may itself be the point. The Philippines has called on ASEAN and China to finalize a framework by year end, framing it as something the world is owed. Radio Free Asia reports the upcoming summit is unlikely to deliver. These two facts have coexisted before and will again.

Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: talks establishing rules of maritime behavior in one of the world's busiest and most disputed waterways have stalled — again — at precisely the moment a Taiwanese minister makes a rare visit to a contested island in those same waters and China deploys a novel carrier platform to the theater. The code's perpetual incompleteness is not a diplomatic failure so much as an outcome that serves at least one party's interests. Rules of the road are most attractive to drivers who might otherwise be stopped.

Manila's year-end deadline will pass like its predecessors. The real negotiation is happening in hull counts, patrol frequencies, and basing arrangements, where no one has to put anything in writing and no summit communiqué can be cited later. The Philippines calling the code something the world is owed is accurate. That it will not materialize is equally accurate. Both things remain true without requiring anyone to acknowledge the contradiction.

EUROPE'S WAR WITHOUT AN EXIT

Russia fired 660 drones and missiles at Kyiv. Ukraine sacked a commander after images of malnourished frontline soldiers circulated publicly. The EU approved a $106 billion loan package after Hungary lifted its veto — the mechanism of capitulation left artfully unspecified in the coverage. Zelenskyy is conducting energy and security diplomacy in Azerbaijan and Saudi Arabia. Russia and Ukraine exchanged 193 prisoners each, which Reuters duly reported and which altered nothing on the front line.

The structural problem is that American dealmakers are consumed by the Iran aftermath, leaving neither a credible external mediator nor sufficient pressure to move Moscow toward terms. Europe is preparing — genuinely, materially — for a longer war while simultaneously acknowledging it has no strategy to end one. These are not contradictory positions if you accept that the policy goal has quietly shifted from resolution to endurance. Sustaining Ukraine indefinitely is now the operating framework, dressed in the language of solidarity, because the alternative requires a public conversation about what acceptable settlement terms look like — and no European capital wants to be the one to start it.

The prisoner swap is the conflict's most human and most politically inert gesture. Both sides will keep fighting. The EU loan will be spent. The drones will keep flying. Europe will keep preparing. The exit remains, as it has been for two years, theoretical.

ALLIANCE MATH AFTER IRAN

The post-Iran-war order is still resolving and the arithmetic is uncomfortable. Hegseth has warned that US forces will destroy any vessel laying mines in the Strait of Hormuz — a statement that would be unremarkable except that it needs making, which tells you something about the current temperature in the Gulf. Separately, an internal Pentagon email reportedly outlined mechanisms to punish NATO allies — including potentially suspending Spain — for insufficient support during Iran operations. Spain's government addressed it publicly. The email's provenance and author remain conveniently unspecified.

Meanwhile the US has sanctioned a major Chinese refinery in Dalian and forty associated shipping entities for handling Iranian crude, continuing the maximum-pressure playbook on two tracks simultaneously. Analysts are now openly asking whether Washington can maintain credible deterrence over Taiwan while sustaining Gulf posture. That question has a mathematical answer involving missile inventories and rotational force availability, and the math, by multiple accounts, is not favorable.

The compounding problem is structural: punishing allies for insufficient burden-sharing at the precise moment their burden-sharing is needed most is a tension that rarely resolves cleanly. The alliance system was designed for sustained collective commitment, not sequential coalition-of-the-willing operations followed by retrospective audits of who contributed enough. Whether the current approach strengthens deterrence or quietly hollows it out is the question that the next contingency will answer, probably faster than anyone would prefer.
