THE RING TIGHTENS AROUND TAIWAN

China called a Japanese warship's routine Taiwan Strait transit a deliberate provocation — a formulation worth sitting with, since the strait is international waters and Japan's navy has made the passage before without theatrical objection. The escalation in language arrived alongside satellite imagery showing ships and a physical barrier deployed to seal off a disputed South China Sea shoal, PLA drills around Taiwan described by Beijing as entirely justified, and serious analytical discussion — not from alarmists but from defence journals — of whether minelaying drones could enforce a blockade without a single landing craft crossing the strait. The information war front is equally active: China is systematically amplifying Taiwanese voices that align with Beijing's preferred narrative, a playbook that requires no bullets and leaves few fingerprints. Meanwhile, the U.S. Navy currently has no carrier strike group positioned for a Taiwan contingency. The cumulative picture is of a pressure campaign operating simultaneously across military, maritime, informational, and diplomatic dimensions — each individually deniable as routine, collectively forming something that looks very much like a methodical encirclement. The question strategists are quietly asking is not whether China is moving, but whether the window for a credible deterrent response is still open.

HORMUZ: THE WORLD'S MOST EXPENSIVE TOLLBOOTH

The Strait of Hormuz was closed this week, then open, then the subject of renewed peace deal optimism — all within roughly a news cycle. Let me make sure I understand this correctly: the country controlling the passage of roughly a fifth of global oil supply announced a closure while simultaneously conducting back-channel negotiations toward an interim nuclear deal, then reopened the strait as those talks generated cautious headlines. The U.S. response was blunt: no deal, bombing resumes. Iranian sources confirmed the core rifts remain — uranium enrichment, the same fault line that has broken every framework for twenty years. What has changed is the surrounding military texture. Iranian gunfire near Gulf vessels this week, reporting that Tehran does not believe it has lost this conflict, and a parliamentary speaker who frames passage through Hormuz as requiring Iranian authorisation — these are not the signals of a party preparing to capitulate. The Lebanese ceasefire model, where an off-ramp was accepted, may be informing American hopes that a similar exit exists here. Tehran's calculation appears to be that the bombing threat has a political ceiling, and that the distance between that ceiling and the current position is where the negotiating room lives.

UKRAINE HITS THE REVENUE LINE

Russia launched its deadliest aerial attack of the year on Ukraine this week, while Ukrainian intelligence assessed that Moscow is preparing to draw Belarus back into active participation for the first time since the 2022 opening phase. Ukraine's overnight response targeted neither the front line nor symbolic infrastructure: drones struck two oil refineries in the Samara region and a Baltic Sea terminal that exports petroleum products — the revenue chain, not the battlefield. Russia, separately, released what functions as a threat inventory: 23 sites across the U.K. and Europe described as legitimate targets. The underlying logic on each side has not changed since 2023. Russia escalates vertically — aerial mass, escalatory rhetoric, threatening third-party infrastructure — when horizontal advance stalls. Ukraine strikes deep when it cannot hold or retake ground, accepting the asymmetry of drone economics against refinery economics. Neither force has the resources to end this conventionally. Both are attacking the opponent's capacity to sustain it. The Belarus question is instructive: its recurring appearance in Ukrainian intelligence assessments suggests Moscow keeps the option warm as a pressure instrument, whether or not any troops ever cross that border — which is, of course, the point.

ONE CRISIS, TWO OPEN DOORS

The week's least reported story may be the strategic geometry connecting the Iranian and Taiwanese theatres. China's calculus in the Western Pacific is never independent of American capacity, and American capacity is measurably constrained when assets are committed to a Middle East contingency. The U.S. Navy carrier gap in the Pacific — confirmed by reporting this week — is not an abstraction; it is a real-time condition that Beijing's planners can observe in open-source vessel-tracking data. Separate analysis argued directly that the Middle East crisis is reshaping China's approach to both Taiwan and Japan, a linkage that is logical rather than conspiratorial: a distracted, militarily extended United States is simply a different deterrent proposition than a concentrated one. The planned Trump-Xi summit, already twice deferred, is now in question again as U.S.-China relations deteriorate along with the Iran conflict. Whether Beijing would exploit a genuine window of American inattention, or merely use the credible threat of exploitation as diplomatic leverage, is the question three continents of intelligence services are trying to answer. The honest answer is that the two are not mutually exclusive — and keeping the question unresolved may itself be the objective.
