Chinese Warship Fires Live Rounds in Japanese Waters Off Yonaguni

The People's Liberation Army Navy fired live ammunition inside waters claimed by Japan near Yonaguni, the westernmost island of the Ryukyu chain and less than 110 kilometers from Taiwan's east coast, on July 22. Tokyo's Ministry of Defense, under Minister Gen Nakatani since October 2024, logged the firing alongside a broader pattern Taipei's Ministry of National Defense separately confirmed the same day: a "significant surge" in PLA sorties across the median line, the informal boundary both sides have observed since 1955 and Beijing has treated as void since 2020. Manila's coast guard reported its own incident this week, a Chinese vessel striking a Philippine sailor in the Spratlys. Three theaters, one week, one navy.

Yonaguni's mayor has pressed Tokyo for years to harden the island's garrison, arguing its 1,500 residents sit closer to Taipei than to Naha. A live-fire exercise inside contested waters there is not a warning shot aimed at Taiwan. It is a rehearsal of the exact maritime approach a Taiwan contingency would require Japan to contest, timed to a week when Washington's attention sits on Houthi missiles and a US service member's death in the Middle East. If the surge documented by Taipei on July 22 continues past this week, the next signal to watch is whether Nakatani's ministry scrambles fighters from Naha Air Base, the response threshold Tokyo has used to mark PLA activity as provocation rather than routine transit.

Log whether Naha scrambles fighters over the next surge window before this runs again: that is the piece's own named threshold, and nothing else in the reporting confirms Tokyo has crossed from routine transit to provocation. -- WR

PLA Live-Fire Off Yonaguni Reaches PBOC Reserve Desk

The People's Liberation Army Navy's live-fire exercise inside waters claimed by Japan near Yonaguni on July 22, run alongside what Taipei's Ministry of National Defense logged the same day as a significant surge in sorties across the median line, lands on a different ledger than the Ministry of Defense in Tokyo. It lands on Governor Pan Gongsheng's reserve management desk at the PBOC, the same desk that reads the Houthi blockade declaration against Saudi tankers and the Independent's report of a US service member killed in the Middle East as one continuous energy-import stress test, not three separate headlines. The desk's mandate has not changed since it started hedging Gulf-transit risk against dollar-funding costs earlier this year, or, more precisely, its mandate has not changed but its inputs have: a Yonaguni live-fire drill adds a second maritime chokepoint to a book that was already pricing Hormuz.

Two timelines are running now and they do not resolve on the same clock. Tokyo's timeline is Minister Nakatani's scramble threshold out of Naha Air Base, the operational trigger that converts routine PLA transit into logged provocation. Beijing's timeline is Premier Li's monthly economic working group agenda, where Hormuz-driven tanker rates and a second contested strait get folded into the same energy-cost line item the reserve desk has been carrying since the Houthi front reopened. The working group's next monthly sitting is where the Yonaguni numbers, not the median-line sortie count Taipei already logged, get a price attached.

The two-clock split holds, but the piece doesn't say what happens if Naha logs a scramble before Li's working group sits, forcing Pan's desk to price Yonaguni off Tokyo's operational tempo rather than Beijing's monthly cadence. Flag whether the desk revisits the single-cycle premise if a scramble lands before the next sitting. -- WR
