THE HORMUZ GAMBIT

The United States has declared a naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, which is the kind of sentence that requires a moment to sit with. Let me make sure I understand this correctly: Washington has moved to physically interdict the world's most consequential maritime chokepoint — through which roughly a fifth of global oil supply transits — on the theory that Iran will blink before global energy markets do. The Council on Foreign Relations frames this as having a short fuse, which may be the most understated assessment of the year.

The structural problems are visible immediately. NATO allies have declined to join. Iran has responded not by retreating but by expanding the aperture, targeting five Gulf states and seeking reparations, while talks in Islamabad have collapsed entirely. The minesweepers now heading toward the region suggest the next phase will be kinetic rather than diplomatic.

The China dimension is the variable that presses most directly on the blockade's internal logic. Hormuz is not just Iran's vulnerability — it is the transit route for the world's largest oil importer, and Beijing's calculations about its own energy security are now embedded in every naval decision Washington makes at sea. Several observers have begun naming this directly: a blockade of Hormuz doubles as a pressure point on China, and China knows it. The alliance fracture visible in NATO's refusal to participate may not be the last such fracture this operation produces.

BEIJING'S TWO-TRACK TAIWAN

Xi Jinping met this week with Taiwan's main opposition leader to discuss shared culture and bloodlines — the kind of meeting conducted with the other party's elected government conspicuously absent from the room. The framing is worth examining: a family reunion narrative, constructed between Beijing and a domestic political opponent of Taipei's ruling coalition, while military pressure on the island continues undiminished. Analysts describe Beijing as shifting its Taiwan strategy; the shift appears to be toward making the outcome feel inevitable rather than violent, which is a more sophisticated form of the same pressure.

In the South China Sea, the methods are less elegant. The Philippines has accused China of using cyanide to poison marine life in a disputed atoll — a tactic that leaves no treaty violation to cleanly cite, no return address to attribute, and a dead reef that can no longer sustain the Philippine communities that depended on it. The word sabotage is now appearing in wire copy.

The American counter-signal is structural: 17,000 troops in the largest US-Philippines war drills on record, with US forces joining combat exercises even while engaged in a separate campaign against Iran. The signal is deliberate — alliance commitments in Asia will be honored even when attention is divided. Whether the signal is credited by the party it is intended to deter is, as always, a different question than whether it was sent.

SURRENDERING TO MACHINES

Ukraine's deployment of ground robots to the most dangerous sections of the frontline — and the subsequent surrender of Russian troops to those machines — is either a battlefield footnote or a doctrinal inflection point, and the historical verdict will depend on whether it scales. For the moment it is being treated as somewhere between the two: a presidential press statement, a Foreign Policy analysis, a data point.

The Easter truce provides the context. It expired Monday having accumulated thousands of violations by both sides' accounting, with a lull in Russian air raids that functioned more as a logistics window than a genuine pause. This is now the established grammar of temporary ceasefires in this war: both sides use them to reposition, rearm, and survive. The truce demonstrated nothing that prior truces had not already demonstrated.

The robots answer a specific problem: Ukraine is a country that has been bleeding manpower for three years and cannot replace it at the rate it loses it. Automation is not a futuristic aspiration — it is a present necessity, driven by the arithmetic of attrition. Zelenskyy's Berlin meeting with Chancellor Merz runs the political track in parallel: German support sustained, the European flank of the coalition held together, the weapons pipeline kept open while Washington's attention is absorbed by a naval blockade on the other side of the world.

TWO PROGRAMS, ONE LESSON

Two pieces of nuclear proliferation news this week, and together they trace the outline of a landscape that the post-Cold War arms control consensus was designed to prevent.

In Pyongyang, Kim Jong-un oversaw test launches of strategic cruise missiles and anti-ship missiles from a new warship — a sea-based capability assembled piece by piece, in public, over years. The announcement via state media is itself the message: a survivable second-strike capacity is the objective, the warship is the platform, and the tests are conducted openly because the deterrence value requires that you know they happened.

In Tehran, the collapse of talks in Islamabad has produced the predictable outcome: Iran holds its uranium enrichment program and cites sovereign right. That framing carries more historical freight than it might appear. A prior multilateral agreement on Iran's nuclear program was signed, sanctions relief was promised, and then the arrangement was abandoned unilaterally in 2018. Iran's position — that commitments extended to it were not honored, and therefore its own commitments need not be honored either — is not merely a negotiating posture. It has a factual foundation. The blockade now running at the Strait of Hormuz is the current chapter of a much longer story, and Tehran has read all the earlier chapters more carefully than the present diplomatic posture appears to credit.
