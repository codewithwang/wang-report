THE $100 BARREL RETURNS

The United States has not merely threatened an Iranian naval blockade — it has begun one. Oil crossed $100 a barrel in response, which is either an effective demonstration of coercive leverage or the opening move of a supply shock that will price itself into everything from European energy bills to emerging-market debt service for the next year; quite possibly both. Talks in Islamabad between Washington and Tehran concluded without a deal, with the American vice president departing empty-handed while the blockade machinery continued warming up. Iran's navy has been characterized, by the American president, as already destroyed — a remarkable claim about a country whose coastline commands the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly a fifth of global seaborne oil passes on any given day.

The secondary-leverage dimension is noteworthy: China has been warned that supplying Iran with weapons will trigger a 50% tariff surcharge, neatly threading the Iran and China files through the same needle. What is equally worth noting is the coalition that assembled for this exercise — or rather, the allies who declined to join it. Australia and the United Kingdom have both stepped back from the blockade, a development that says something interesting about the current operating definition of partner. The Wall Street Journal's assessment that the era of free seas is unraveling reads less like opinion and more like a receipt.

BEIJING'S TWO-TRACK TAIWAN PLAY

Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: China doubled its warships around Taiwan in the same week that Beijing hosted the leader of Taiwan's main opposition party for the first such summit in a decade — and then announced it would resume some bilateral ties with Taipei as a goodwill gesture. The military signal and the diplomatic signal are not in tension. They are the same signal, addressed to different audiences.

Taiwan's government, meanwhile, is drilling specifically for the scenario of a Chinese energy blockade — simulating how it would break one, testing air force responses, practicing the logistics of being economically strangled. The US diplomat corps has issued statements asking China to abandon its threats. Beijing has not updated its threat posture in response to these statements, which is consistent with prior practice.

The Kuomintang's positioning deserves scrutiny. The party is not simply engaging in dialogue; it is aligning itself more closely with Beijing in the name of peace — which is a serviceable frame if you are the KMT, and a rather convenient one if you are Beijing. The effect is to present the Taiwanese public with a choice: the governing party's confrontational posture, or the opposition's accommodating one, with a doubled naval presence offshore to help clarify the stakes.

THE COALITION OF THE WILLING-ISH

The United States is currently running simultaneous pressure campaigns in the Persian Gulf, the Taiwan Strait, and the South China Sea, which raises a clarifying question about the nature of American alliances: which crises are allies actually willing to show up for?

The answer, this week, is granular. Australia and the Philippines joined the US for their second joint maritime exercise in the South China Sea this year — a meaningful data point about where the Indo-Pacific coalition holds. The same Australia has declined to join the Iranian naval blockade, as has the United Kingdom. Washington has also signaled, in connection with the India-Pakistan file, that it may step back from its traditional role as crisis interlocutor between nuclear-armed neighbors — a significant departure from decades of operating procedure, delivered with minimal apparent fanfare.

The NATO dimension adds texture. American frustration with the alliance, expressed publicly and during an active naval blockade with two simultaneous theater crises, lands with a particular weight. The emerging picture is not withdrawal so much as selective engagement: act where chosen, expect allies to follow where convenient, grow impatient with those who calculate their own risk exposure first. That the allies are doing exactly what sovereign nations have always done is the irony that goes unstated.

THE MEASUREMENT FUNCTION OF CEASEFIRES

Russia and Ukraine agreed to an Easter ceasefire. Russia then violated it 7,696 times, according to Ukraine's General Staff, over the span of the holiday weekend. The figure is precise enough to be useful and large enough to be meaningless as a tactical description — which makes it, perhaps accidentally, the ideal summary statistic for where this conflict currently sits.

The ceasefire was proposed by Zelensky, accepted by Putin, and framed publicly as a humanitarian gesture for Orthodox Easter. That framing required a certain suspension of recent history on all sides. What the episode produced, in practice, was a measurement baseline: a defined window during which every artillery strike, drone launch, and ground assault could be counted and attributed. Russia reportedly lost over 1,100 troops in a single day during the same period — numbers that, if accurate, suggest the pace of operations was not appreciably reduced by the announced pause.

Ceasefires in this conflict have accumulated a specific function distinct from their stated one. They do not stop the fighting. They produce documentation. Each announced pause followed by thousands of violations builds an evidentiary record that someone, somewhere, presumably intends to use for something — accountability mechanisms, war crimes proceedings, or simply the historical record. Whether that was the intent hardly matters. It has become the outcome.
