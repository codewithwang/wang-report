THE BLOCKADE DRESS REHEARSAL

The PLA's recent activities around Taiwan don't require a classified briefing to interpret. The Liaoning carrier group transited the Taiwan Strait — a route China spent years insisting was sovereignly contested — while separately, Chinese warships conducted exercises in the western Pacific. Reports emerged that the PLA is evaluating underwater mining drones for a Taiwan blockade. And in a test that received almost no English-language coverage, China successfully operated a cable-cutting ROV at 11,400 feet — roughly the depth of undersea cables linking Taiwan to global internet infrastructure.

Each element is individually deniable. Carrier transits are routine. Drone concepts are speculative. ROV tests are maritime research. But stack them: a surface cordon, subsurface mining capability, cable-cutting capacity, and active Strait presence, and you have every component of a blockade architecture. Not deployed. Not announced. Simply assembled. The official Chinese position remains that reunification is a domestic matter — which it may well be. The question is whether 'domestic' now comes with undersea infrastructure demolition as a supporting instrument.

The doctrine being stress-tested is less about invasion and more about coercion: what does Taiwan concede when the internet goes dark and the fuel tankers stop?

THE COUNTER-ARCHITECTURE HARDENS

Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: Japan just sailed a warship through the Taiwan Strait on the anniversary of the Japan-US security treaty, and simultaneously joined South China Sea drills for the first time. Tokyo called these moves routine. Beijing called them provocations. Both may be right. What they undeniably are is a signal — that Japan has shifted from interested observer to active participant in a regional security architecture it spent decades carefully avoiding.

The broader picture is coherent and accelerating. BALIKATAN 2026 introduced maritime strike drills on a Philippine island explicitly described as near Taiwan. The US is establishing a forward fuel depot in the Philippines. Australia and Canada joined US naval operations in the South China Sea — a multilateral presence that would have been diplomatically unthinkable a decade ago.

The alliance structure here is no longer hub-and-spoke, American power radiating outward through bilateral treaties. It is becoming a mesh: overlapping exercises, shared logistics, distributed positioning. China's consistent pressure on every node of this system has had the counterproductive effect of pulling every node closer together. The question for strategists isn't whether this coalition can deter — it's whether it can signal deterrence clearly enough that neither side stumbles into a test neither intended.

A CEASEFIRE WITH 410 CASUALTIES

The Iran file offers a study in definitional flexibility. There is, nominally, a ceasefire between the United States and Iran. And yet: US forces have suffered 410 wounded in Iran-related operations, an Iran-flagged vessel was seized, Tehran has vowed retaliation, and the arrangement has moved, per reporting, toward the brink following resumed vessel attacks.

A ceasefire under which one party seizes ships, the other retaliates, and the first party absorbs hundreds of wounded is either operating under very creative parameters, or it is a word being applied to a situation it no longer describes. The operational reality appears to be a managed confrontation — not peace, not war, but a sustained friction layer where both sides calibrate actions to stay below escalation thresholds while neither side exits the cycle.

What makes this consequential beyond the bilateral is the force-protection math. Four hundred and ten wounded US service members is a substantial domestic political problem with no clean off-ramp. Every proxy action that produces US casualties creates pressure in Washington for a response that disrupts the nominal ceasefire. Every US response gives Tehran a justification for the next action. The machine runs itself, and the ceasefire designation provides just enough diplomatic cover for both parties to keep running it.

RUSSIA'S THREE-TRACK PRESSURE

Russia's strategy operates on simultaneous tracks, and the past 48 hours illustrated each one. The kinetic track: a hours-long overnight drone assault on Ukrainian cities, degrading infrastructure and testing air-defense depth. The expansion track: reports that Moscow is actively working to bring Belarus back into the conflict, which would open a northern front requiring Ukrainian force redistribution at exactly the moment the eastern lines remain contested. The diplomatic track: Germany summoning the Russian ambassador over what Berlin officially described as direct threats — an extraordinary step for a NATO member that still prefers quiet channels.

Three tracks, one coherent logic. The drone campaign keeps Western attention on humanitarian costs rather than battlefield initiative. The Belarus pressure threatens a second front as a negotiating lever or an operational one, depending on how subsequent weeks unfold. The direct threats against a NATO member test whether the alliance's political will can be separated from its operational commitments — whether individual capitals can be pressured into moderation faster than the collective can cohere.

What Russia appears to have calculated is that European resolve is load-bearing on political consensus, and political consensus has a fatigue threshold. Whether the ambassador summoning and the drone assaults are symptoms of a strategy working or symptoms of one that keeps the war's costs legible enough that electorates eventually push for settlement is the open question that 2026 will answer.
