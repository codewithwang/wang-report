THE GEOGRAPHY OF SALAMI

Beijing's response to a Japanese warship transiting the Taiwan Strait was characteristically layered: a formal protest, military exercises described as routine, and — almost simultaneously — the deployment of a floating barrier at Scarborough Shoal to block Philippine access to fishing grounds they have used for generations. The simultaneity is the point. Each action, taken individually, is deniable as proportionate or defensive. Taken together, they describe a methodical narrowing of the operational space available to any actor Beijing considers a rival. Japan joining US-Philippine drills for the first time prompted Chinese exercises in the East China Sea. The pattern holds: every assertion of allied presence is met with a demonstration that costs nothing to perform and something to ignore. A floating barrier is not a shot fired. A routine exercise is not a crisis. And yet the shoal is effectively closed, the drill is effectively answered, and the cumulative geography shifts, increment by increment, toward a fait accompli that no single event ever quite justified resisting. Assessments now circulating openly note that US distraction has accelerated the pace of this accumulation. Beijing does not need to win any single confrontation. It needs the threshold for resisting any single step to remain just slightly higher than the political will available to clear it.

THE CARRIER GAP

The United States has no aircraft carriers available for a conflict over Taiwan. This is not an editorial inference — it is the reported assessment circulating in defence analysis circles at the same moment Washington is pressing Taipei that arms procurement cannot be delayed. There is a certain awkward symmetry in urging an ally to arm faster while being unable to project the force that would backstop that ally's survival in the interim. The Iran war, meanwhile, is providing the PLA with something invaluable: a live laboratory of modern warfare conducted by adversaries and near-peers, without Chinese personnel at risk. Analysts have noted directly that lessons from Iran are being absorbed into PLA doctrine. Separately, the question of whether Beijing's behind-the-scenes role in that conflict grants it visibility into both attacker and attacked is being raised and not seriously disputed. The Trump-Xi summit, tentatively scheduled for May, is reportedly under pressure from these converging tensions. The scenario planners drafting blockade concepts — including drone-delivered minefields around Taiwan — are not operating in an abstract strategic vacuum. They are watching a real war, studying real outcomes, and refining real options. What gets discussed at that summit table, and what doesn't, will be more informative than any joint statement.

THE PRICE OF PASSAGE

Let me make sure I understand this correctly: a country has announced that any ship approaching the Strait of Hormuz will be targeted, the strait has been closed and reopened within what appears to be a single news cycle, and the policy response from Washington is a warning against blackmail. The Strait of Hormuz carries roughly a fifth of global oil supply. Its closure, even partial and temporary, is not a tactical inconvenience — it is an economic event. The cycling of closure and reopening is the signal. The message is not we have shut the strait but we can shut the strait, at a time of our choosing, with minimal notice. That is a different and more durable form of leverage. Every tanker captain and energy trader now prices Iranian decision-making into their calculus. The US blockade continuing even as commercial shipping resumes points to a bifurcated legal situation with no clean resolution in sight. Whether this is designed to complicate US force projection in the Pacific, extract concessions under pressure, or simply impose costs on adversaries facing their own domestic pressures is, at this point, less important than the fact that all three outcomes are served simultaneously. Effective ambiguity is its own strategy.

ESCALATION AS VOCABULARY

Moscow has published a list of European and UK sites it could attack. North Korea has test-fired a ballistic missile that, forty-eight minutes later, no longer posed a threat to Japan's vicinity. Zelensky has warned that Russia intends to pull Belarus back into active war footing. Three separate actors, three separate events, one structural logic: each is a public threat calibrated to impose a cost on resolve rather than on capability. A published target list is not an attack — it is an invitation to imagine one, repeated for European publics already experiencing aid fatigue. A missile test with built-in deniability is a capability demonstration that demands no formal response. Belarus re-entering the conflict would open a second front that Ukrainian planners have spent two years preparing for and hoping to avoid. The common thread is escalation deployed as a political instrument: raising the perceived cost of continued support without crossing the threshold that an actual strike would demand. Each threat exists in the space between coercion and action, designed to be just credible enough to influence decisions and just ambiguous enough to escape accountability. Whether this constitutes coherent strategy or the improvised pressure of actors running short of better options is a question the events themselves are careful not to answer.
