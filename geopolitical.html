REHEARSAL IN PLAIN SIGHT

The week's Indo-Pacific dispatches, read in sequence, sketch a picture Beijing would probably prefer not to have assembled quite so legibly. PLA amphibious assault drills are becoming more realistic and sophisticated, per Naval War College analysis. Eight sorties of aircraft and vessels detected around Taiwan in a single reporting period. Live-fire exercises in waters near Luzon. The Type 076 drone carrier — purpose-built for the kind of drone-saturated warfare the Ukraine conflict has normalized — deployed to the South China Sea. Meanwhile, Xi tells a Taiwanese opposition leader that unification is inevitable, which functions as either a diplomatic overture or a deadline, depending on how seriously you take the hardware moves happening simultaneously. The counterpressure is also visible. A Japanese warship transits the Taiwan Strait — Beijing calls it a provocation, which is precisely the signal Tokyo intended to send. Taiwan's minister visits Taiping Island for coast guard drills. The US Navy's hellscape drone plan, which envisions saturating the strait with thousands of autonomous systems, is receiving serious analytical attention. Congress moves a $500 million military aid bill. What none of these individual moves fully answers is the question the missile stockpile analysis has placed on the table: whether a US military still absorbing the costs of an Iran engagement could mount a credible Taiwan defense. Every piece is moving. The picture they form is not ambiguous.

WHEN MARKETS KNOW FIRST

A week ago, prediction markets assigned a 68% probability to a US-Iran nuclear deal by April 30. That figure has since collapsed to 10.1%. Markets move before headlines catch up — which is usually how it goes when official statements and operational signals point in opposite directions. New sanctions targeting Iran's missile and UAV programs landed while the Iranian foreign minister was literally airborne to Islamabad for a second round of negotiations. Warnings about destroying ships that lay mines in the Strait of Hormuz were issued in parallel with announcements that ceasefire talks with Lebanon remain open. The official frame is: diplomacy is ongoing. The sanctions frame says something else about where the parties actually stand. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: the US is imposing new weapons-program sanctions on a country with which it is simultaneously conducting nuclear negotiations, in the same news cycle, from the same government. This is either a deliberate pressure tactic — sanctions as leverage at the table — or evidence that different parts of the administration are not coordinating their signals. Either reading has consequences. In a region where a ceasefire is described as extended until a proposal is submitted, phrasing that reads less like peace and more like an invoice, the distance between talking and not talking is shorter than the official calendar suggests.

EUROPE'S ORPHANED WAR

Russia delivered 660 drones and missiles to Kyiv in a single overnight campaign. Putin's approval rating has reached its lowest point since before the invasion began, according to a state pollster — which means the real number is probably worse, and the fact that the state pollster is publishing it at all is a signal worth reading separately. Europe, meanwhile, is preparing for a longer war. The analytical frame gaining currency in European capitals is not how to win; it is how to sustain. The reason no end strategy exists is structural. American diplomatic bandwidth is fully committed to Iran. The administration's conception of alliance obligations, made explicit in the reported Pentagon communication about punishing allies who failed to support Iran operations, runs one theater at a time. Ukraine is not that theater right now. What Europe is left with is a war it didn't start, an adversary it cannot fully contain, a patron whose attention is elsewhere, and domestic mandates to keep spending on defense while the economic rationale for doing so is still being assembled retroactively. The drone campaign against Kyiv proceeds on schedule, indifferent to the strategic paralysis surrounding it.

THE LOYALTY TEST

An internal Pentagon communication reportedly listed mechanisms by which the administration could punish NATO allies it believed had failed to adequately support Iran operations. The specific proposals matter less than the underlying premise: that alliance membership now carries an implied obligation to participate in offensive operations the member states did not collectively authorize. This is a meaningful reinterpretation of what the alliance is actually for. The timing compounds the problem. Three simultaneous active theaters — Ukraine, Iran, Taiwan — and a documented analysis of missile stockpile depletion following the Iran engagement raise a question that alliance managers have historically preferred to leave unexamined: what happens when the guarantor's credible commitment to one theater is contingent on not having recently exercised it in another? Pakistan, simultaneously hosting US-Iran nuclear talks and marking a year since Pahalgam while warning India against escalation, adds a fourth variable that operates largely independently of the other three. The loyalty test being administered to European allies is being run at precisely the moment when the collective capacity to pass it — and to call on it in return — is most constrained. That asymmetry is not accidental. It is the condition under which tests of this kind are typically administered.
