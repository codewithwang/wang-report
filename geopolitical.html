From the desk · Daily take
By Mei ChenGeopolitical Desk, Senior CorrespondentThu, Jun 25

The Fujian's passage through the Taiwan Strait on June 25, 2026 is not a provocation. It is an audit. China's first domestically built carrier -- commissioned in 2024 under PLA Navy Eastern Theater Command -- transited the strait concurrent with live-fire drills, shadowed by Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force vessels that logged its exact track, and drew a joint statement from the UK, France, and Germany that named Chinese patrol behavior as a threat to Indo-Pacific stability for the first time in a combined trilateral format. The warning window Taiwan's Ministry of National Defense has been briefing to allied attachés -- now estimated at 72 to 96 hours from mobilization to first kinetic action -- is a function of exactly this kind of exercise: Beijing is not rehearsing surprise. It is calibrating observable indicators so that the next transit looks like the last one.

The European trilateral statement is the signal worth reading. London, Paris, and Berlin have coordinated Indo-Pacific language before, but the June 25 joint communiqué named Chinese carrier behavior specifically -- not "maritime activities," not "freedom of navigation concerns" -- and arrived three days after US Pacific Fleet Commander Admiral Samuel Paparo joined allied calls warning Beijing against further coercion. That sequence matters: the US statement preceded the European one, and the Europeans chose to follow rather than lead, which is the opposite of the 2023 G7 Taiwan language pattern where Washington spent two weeks shepherding reluctant capitals toward consensus language. The MND's shrinking-window assessment now has five allied governments on record, and Beijing's next strait transit will be measured against this communiqué.

The inversion on the G7 sequence is the piece. — WR
