CHINA REHEARSES THE ENDGAME

The pattern across this week's China dispatches is not ambiguity but clarity delivered at calibrated volume. A 40-day airspace shutdown near Taiwan, live-fire drills in the Yellow Sea, nuclear-exchange rehearsals for a Taiwan contingency, flares fired at Philippine aircraft in disputed airspace — and, for good measure, satellite imagery suggesting a new airbase rising from coral at Antelope Reef while a second facility acquires the infrastructure of permanence nearby. Each item, reviewed in isolation, can be filed under routine military activity. Reviewed together, they describe an environment being systematically conditioned: a region learning what the new normal feels like before the new normal arrives. The nuclear drill detail is worth sitting with. The scenario being rehearsed is not whether a Taiwan conflict escalates to nuclear exchange — it is how to absorb an American first strike and respond. That is a specific kind of planning. It is not preparation for war as a contingency. It is preparation for war as an eventuality, and preparation of regional psychology for the logic of acquiescence that would precede it.

TAIWAN'S CONVENIENT OPPOSITION

While Beijing shut down airspace for drills and rehearsed nuclear exchanges, Taiwan's main opposition party was boycotting its own country's defense budget negotiations — the same week its leader conducted peace overtures toward Beijing. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: a legislature representing a democracy under demonstrated military pressure is refusing to fund its own military, while its leadership publicly auditions for a diplomatic channel that exists, structurally, on Beijing's terms. The KMT position is not incoherent on its own terms — cross-strait détente has a genuine constituency in Taiwan, and not every defense hawk has thought through the arithmetic of asymmetric war. But the timing is instructive. Every budget delay is a capability gap extended. Every public declaration of openness to talks without preconditions is a data point archived for the negotiating table Beijing intends to set unilaterally. The regional response elsewhere is clarifying by contrast: Manila is opening coast guard bases in disputed waters, and Japan is now conducting combat exercises alongside Philippine forces — a posture that would have been diplomatically unthinkable in the previous decade.

PYONGYANG'S LESSONS FROM OTHER WARS

North Korea's announcement that its latest test series included ballistic missiles armed with cluster-bomb warheads lands with less shock than it perhaps should, which is itself a kind of accomplishment. Pyongyang has been running the world's most attentive after-action review of every armed conflict it doesn't fight in — Ukraine provided the drone and artillery syllabus; Iran's recent experience is apparently adding new modules. Cluster munitions mated to ballistic missiles represent a specific doctrinal choice: area-effect weaponry delivered at ranges that complicate point defense, optimized for degrading airfields, logistics nodes, and troop concentrations rather than single high-value targets. The lesson being absorbed from watching other people's wars is not purely technological. It is operational — what saturates modern air defense architectures, what slips through them, what forces triage decisions that favor the attacker. Pyongyang is constructing a military that is calibrated against the precise adversary it expects and the precise scenario it anticipates. The weapons, in this sense, are not just hardware. They are a legible statement of intent.

THE MIDDLE EAST AFTER THE BOMBING

Forty-one days into a war that began with the largest American military operation in the region since 2003, the ceasefire is described as on the verge of collapse, Lebanon has declared a national day of mourning after 254 killed in a single day, and the Israeli government has stated plainly that no ceasefire is coming. The Economist's assessment that the conflict has changed the Middle East for the worse is doing significant editorial work with the word worse. The American position has arrived at a peculiar incoherence: Washington is pursuing a truce with Tehran, warning that military forces will remain in theater until any agreement is honored, and simultaneously tolerating a campaign that appears structurally designed to foreclose the diplomatic space those same forces are nominally protecting. The Strait of Hormuz is being used as both leverage and grievance. The president's language — next conquest, warnings about toll collection — suggests an administration that has not yet resolved whether it wants a durable agreement or a durable demonstration. Iran, watching its proxy network dismantled and its chokehold on global energy flows threatened, is being asked to choose negotiation under conditions that make negotiation indistinguishable from surrender.
