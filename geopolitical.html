THE HORMUZ-TAIWAN CIRCUIT

The strait you are watching is not necessarily the one that matters most. Hormuz is the theater this week — blockades, live updates, maritime brinkmanship in real time. But analysts have spent the week pointing at a different chokepoint entirely. Taiwan accounts for the overwhelming majority of advanced semiconductor fabrication capacity; any sustained interdiction of the island's energy supply routes — most of which run through or near waters China can reach without crossing the Pacific — would do to the global technology economy roughly what closing Hormuz does to oil markets, except the recovery timeline would be measured in years, not weeks. Taiwan's military has been running drills specifically aimed at securing those energy corridors. The People's Liberation Army has scheduled live-fire exercises in the South China Sea. Neither side is speaking directly to the other about this. The Hormuz crisis has demonstrated, usefully for everyone watching, that you do not need a ground invasion to impose existential economic pressure. You need patience, persistence, and a willingness to let the other side's logistics do the work. The lesson is not lost on anyone with a map and a stake in the semiconductor supply chain.

BEIJING'S SHIFT, MEASURED IN DRILLS

China's approach to Taiwan has, by most serious accounts, moved away from the dramatic reunification-by-force scenarios that dominated threat assessments a decade ago and toward something more methodical: incremental pressure, sustained ambiguity, the steady accumulation of leverage. Ten Taiwanese nationals were charged with espionage this week. Live-fire drills commenced in the South China Sea on April 15. Beijing called Washington's characterization of these activities a distortion demonstrating malicious intentions — which is, to be fair, what one says when the characterization is largely accurate. The Australian Broadcasting Corporation reported a deliberate strategic shift in Beijing's Taiwan posture, though the precise direction depends on the analyst you consult. What is less contested is the operational picture: higher-frequency military exercises, improved intelligence penetration of Taiwanese institutions, and a blockade-without-invasion concept that maximizes leverage while keeping hands technically clean. Taiwan is responding — energy security exercises, reinforced supply-route planning — but the asymmetry of initiative remains. The side that sets the tempo has advantages the defending side cannot fully offset by simply reacting faster, and that asymmetry compounds quietly over time in ways that rarely make front pages until they do.

FORTY-EIGHT HOURS FROM PEACE, INDEFINITELY

Close to over. That was the assessment offered this week regarding the US-Iran conflict — a phrase with the unusual property of being technically consistent with any timeline whatsoever. The operational reality is a naval blockade of Iranian ports, Israeli operations intensifying in South Lebanon, and a proposed American diplomatic offer of a 20-year suspension of all Iranian nuclear activity, which is either a serious opening gambit or a maximalist demand dressed as one, depending on your priors and your reading of the last three similar negotiations. Saudi Arabia urged Washington to rein in the conflict, which is worth noting primarily because it indicates the regional actors most exposed to Iranian retaliation are not confident this ends quickly or cleanly. The IMF weighed in to warn of slower global growth — a statement whose gravitas has diminished from repetition, but whose arithmetic remains correct. The Trump administration's apparent logic is that economic pain, distributed widely enough and attributed clearly enough to Iranian intransigence, eventually produces concessions. The historical record of coercive blockades achieving that outcome is not empty, but it is not a foundation on which one would confidently build a timeline. Close to over has been the condition of several frozen conflicts for decades.

THE REPRICING OF COMMITMENTS

The week's sharpest juxtaposition: 17,000 American and Filipino troops deploying for the largest joint war drill in the alliance's history, while the American president described NATO as an organization that was not there and will not be there in the future. Both statements coexist, and together they describe a foreign policy that is not retreating from security commitments so much as repricing them — selectively, loudly, and in ways that are difficult to model from the outside. A US-China summit is apparently in prospect, with the trade representative describing the relationship as stable, a word that is doing extraordinary diplomatic heavy lifting against a backdrop of live-fire drills, semiconductor blockade scenarios, and active maritime confrontation in two separate theaters. North Korea tested cruise and anti-ship missiles from a new warship as its leader observed. Autonomous robotic units claimed a battlefield test engagement. The military-technological frontier is moving faster than the alliance architectures designed to govern it, which means every commitment that was written before large-scale autonomous systems entered the picture is now, in some meaningful sense, provisional. No summit communiqué will fix that. The repricing is structural.
