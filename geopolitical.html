THE HORMUZ DIVIDEND

The arrangement taking shape in the Gulf — a 10-day ceasefire between Israel and Lebanon, an interim nuclear understanding with Tehran that leaves uranium enrichment intact, a U.S. naval blockade at the Strait of Hormuz — is being reported as a diplomatic success. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: the United States blockades the world's most critical oil chokepoint, extracts a deal that explicitly does not require Iran to stop enriching, and this is the win? Perhaps. But the secondary effect is the real story. Every day the Strait of Hormuz is disrupted is a day the global economy rehearses what happens when seaborne energy flows are severed. Taiwan receives the overwhelming majority of its energy by sea. China watches that rehearsal carefully, and analysts have begun saying the quiet part aloud: a Hormuz blockade, whatever its intent, is a live demonstration that chokepoint coercion works. Iranian officials have been consistent that enrichment is non-negotiable. The interim framing buys time, but the structural tension remains unresolved. In the meantime, the Hormuz precedent circulates quietly through every strategic planning document from Taipei to Manila.

SALAMI BY SHOAL

China's approach to the South China Sea has always been incremental enough that each move can be framed as a response to something else. New satellite imagery now shows ships and a physical barrier tightening control of the Scarborough Shoal entrance — a position contested since 2012. Manila has separately accused Beijing of deploying cyanide at a nearby atoll, a method consistent with rendering a feature ecologically uninhabitable for anyone else's sustained presence. Japan has joined U.S.-Philippines military exercises, a meaningful shift given Tokyo's constitutional constraints, framed explicitly around Taiwan defense. The Pentagon is warning publicly about Chinese "invasion rehearsals" — large-scale exercises with the architecture of a dress rehearsal, absent only the declaration. One analysis suggests Xi's recent meeting with a Taiwanese opposition leader was shaped partly by the domestic imperative to project stability after military purges. The picture is of a power consolidating forward positions methodically, occasionally emitting a diplomatic signal, and continuing regardless. The question is not whether the pattern is legible — it is plainly legible — but whether the collective response is organized enough to alter the calculus.

EUROPE'S WAR NOW

Russia's largest missile barrage on Ukrainian cities in months, killing at least 18, arrived the same week NATO's secretary general pledged $60 billion in alliance military support for 2026. The United Kingdom's $1 billion delivery under the ERA initiative is notable less for the amount than for the mechanism: frozen Russian sovereign assets converted into weapons for the country Russia is fighting. The legal and diplomatic precedent was established without fanfare. Drone deliveries from allied capitals are accelerating to absorb the volume of Russian strikes. What is conspicuously absent from the recent announcements is Washington. The NATO pledge is a European commitment. The ERA funding is European. The drone surge is allied. Whether the U.S. withdrawal from the foreground reflects a deliberate strategic reorientation toward the Pacific or simply the current administration's ambivalence about European security architecture, the practical effect is the same: Europe is being asked to sustain Ukraine's defensive capacity largely under its own weight. That may prove sufficient. But the precedent being set — that alliance commitments carry conditions indexed to alignment with a particular administration's priorities — is being noted in every capital that holds one.

SANCTIONS DON'T SCALE

The analysis is blunt: Iran, Russia, and China have constructed a weapons supply network resilient enough that sanctions and tariffs cannot meaningfully disrupt it. Iranian drones fly over Ukraine. Russian oil moves through shadow fleets. Chinese dual-use components transit through intermediaries. North Korea ships artillery shells with apparent impunity, then spends the proceeds on a new destroyer class from which it publicly test-fires anti-ship and cruise missiles. The IAEA has separately warned that Pyongyang's nuclear production is accelerating at a rate it describes as "very serious." The sanctions architecture was designed for a world in which major powers valued access to the dollar system enough to enforce compliance on their clients. That premise is now structurally broken. Three of the four members of this informal axis have concluded that the costs of operating outside Western financial architecture are lower than the costs of compliance. North Korea reached that conclusion decades ago. Russia reached it in 2022. The significant question is what happens to global deterrence infrastructure when the marginal coercive value of sanctions approaches zero — and whether the institutions built around that assumption are prepared to answer it honestly.
