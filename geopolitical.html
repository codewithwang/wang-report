THE BLOCKADE AND ITS DISCONTENTS

The United States has blockaded Iran's ports and declared its intention to sink Iranian vessels that approach the Strait of Hormuz — through which, as a reminder, roughly a fifth of global oil supply transits on any given day. Middle East production has already plunged, per OPEC data. Qatar has grounded European flight connections. The Australian Broadcasting Corporation is reporting food crisis warnings alongside accusations of piracy.

Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: a naval blockade of a major maritime chokepoint is being described by the blockading party as a measured response, while the party being blockaded is the one accused of piracy.

The deeper problem sits in the gap between declared victory and lived experience. Israelis, by contemporary accounts, don't feel like victors in the war with Iran. This is the structural paradox of military campaigns that succeed on their own terms and still fail to produce security: you can degrade an adversary's nuclear program, reshape its proxy network, and still find your population living under a ceiling of anxiety no sortie removes. The blockade may tighten the economic noose, but nooses have a way of becoming the story. Global commodity markets are already writing the headline.

CEASEFIRE AS DIPLOMATIC THEATER

The Easter ceasefire in Ukraine was violated 7,696 times, according to Kyiv — a number precise enough to suggest it was being counted in real time, which implies nobody expected it to hold. Ukraine is pulling back in eastern Sumy Oblast. The front is moving in the wrong direction for Kyiv, incrementally but persistently.

Into this landscape arrives Peter Magyar, Hungary's new leader, who has just ended a political era in Budapest in a result Brussels greeted with visible relief. Magyar promptly declared he would ask Vladimir Putin to end the war because — his framing — it would be nice to end the killing. The sentiment is not objectionable. The implied method is.

The pattern here is durable: European leaders who win on reform platforms and pro-EU credentials discover, once in office, that their constituents' appetite for indefinite war funding is more limited than their campaign speeches suggested. Magyar is not his predecessor. But the gravitational pull of public war fatigue acts on every government eventually, regardless of how it arrived in power. The question for Ukraine's partners is whether the coalition holds not through conviction but through institutional inertia — and whether that turns out to be enough.

CHEMISTRY LESSON IN THE SOUTH CHINA SEA

The Philippines has accused Chinese vessels of deploying cyanide to poison a South China Sea atoll — a charge that, if substantiated, would represent a significant escalation beyond the water cannon skirmishes and laser incidents that have defined recent confrontations. China's state media, for its part, published a military spokesperson describing joint US-Australia-Philippines patrols as actions by "extraterritorial countries," which is a phrase worth sitting with: in Beijing's cartography, the Philippines is defending waters that belong to a country other than the Philippines.

The United States and its partners conducted their second joint drills in the South China Sea this year. Both the drills and the PLA's response statements are ritualistic at this point. What isn't ritualistic is the cyanide allegation, which introduces an environmental and biological dimension to what has been primarily a legal and political dispute about maritime zones.

Cyanide kills coral. Coral loss collapses the fishery. Fishery collapse undermines the livelihoods of the fishing communities on whose behalf territorial claims are partly made. The strategic logic, if the allegation holds, involves removing the resource that animates the contested claim while denying the tactic exists — a move that is either extraordinarily cynical or extraordinarily patient, depending on how one assigns intent.

THE ARITHMETIC OF DISTRACTION

China's posture during the Iran war has been a study in disciplined ambiguity. It has avoided explicit support for Tehran while observing the United States redirect military and diplomatic bandwidth toward the Persian Gulf. The analysis is not complicated: a Washington absorbed by Hormuz is a Washington with reduced capacity to manage the Taiwan Strait and the South China Sea simultaneously.

Against this backdrop, Beijing has signaled it will resume some ties with Taiwan following a visit by an opposition leader — a tactical softening that costs little, signals flexibility, and concedes nothing structural. Taiwan's president is simultaneously en route to Africa to shore up the island's last remaining diplomatic ally on that continent, a trip that highlights just how thin formal recognition has become.

Trump, for his part, has threatened China with 50% tariffs while simultaneously offering Venezuelan crude as a negotiating sweetener — a combination that suggests multiple pressure tracks running in parallel without obvious coordination between them. Beijing watches this and draws its own conclusions. The lesson absorbed thoroughly over the past several years is that American leverage is most effective when concentrated and credible. The current moment offers neither condition simultaneously. That is not an argument for Chinese adventurism, but it is an environment in which patience looks increasingly indistinguishable from strategy.
