THE STRAIT THAT SWALLOWED EVERYTHING

A two-week ceasefire, conditioned on Iran reopening a waterway it controls, is not really a ceasefire — it is a deadline with better branding. The IEA describes the current oil and gas disruption as worse than 1973, 1979, and 2022 combined, which suggests the world spent fifty years building elaborate contingency plans that are now performing roughly as expected. Iran's UN envoy, for his part, says Tehran is open to genuine talks but rejects temporary arrangements, which is a diplomatically elegant way of saying no while leaving the door ajar. The Houthis have entered the conflict belatedly and in weakened condition, which tells you something about the calculus: even a diminished card is worth playing when the pot is this large. Meanwhile, Russia and China exercised their Security Council vetoes on a Hormuz resolution, a reminder that the multilateral architecture for managing energy crises runs directly through the capitals least interested in resolving this one. Pakistan's prime minister has reportedly asked Washington to extend the deadline. That request is either a serious diplomatic intervention or a test of how the clock actually works. The answer to that question will tell you more about the next six months than any ceasefire announcement.

UKRAINE FINDS ITS TARGET

There is a certain strategic elegance to Ukraine striking Russian oil export terminals at precisely the moment Russia might have expected Iran's war to generate a windfall. The Ust-Luga terminal has now been hit at least five times in a week. Novorossiysk, the Black Sea energy hub, followed. The cumulative effect is less a single dramatic blow than a sustained, methodical degradation of the infrastructure Russia depends on to fund continued operations — which is a different kind of war than the one being fought on the front lines, and in some ways a more consequential one. A former CIA director has assessed that Russia no longer holds the upper hand in Ukraine, which is the kind of statement that arrives quietly but carries significant weight. Zelenskyy's Easter truce offer sits alongside these strikes without apparent contradiction: the offer is for Orthodox Easter, the strikes are for the other fifty-one weeks. What is emerging is a Ukrainian strategy that understands the relationship between Russia's revenue capacity and its military endurance — and has decided that the most efficient path to the negotiating table runs through oil terminals rather than territorial advances.

TAIWAN DOES THE MATH

Taiwan is watching the Iran war the way a graduate student watches a qualifying exam — taking notes on what gets asked, how the answers land, and what happens to those who guessed wrong. China restricting 40 days of airspace signals military activity at a scale and duration that is difficult to explain away as routine. An opposition leader's Beijing visit — the first by a major Taiwanese party head in a decade — has prompted warnings that it will serve as propaganda material, which may be true and may also be beside the point: the visit is itself a data point about how Taiwanese political actors are reading their options. CNN frames the question bluntly: US weapons or China's friendship, with Trump's volatility as the independent variable. The South China Morning Post, reading from a different vantage, asks what the mainland learns from watching how Washington manages a distant, costly, energy-disrupting war while Taiwan considers its own exposure. The honest answer is that everyone is learning something, and the lessons being drawn in Taipei, Beijing, and Washington are not the same lessons. That asymmetry is where the risk lives.

THE SUCCESSION PROBLEM GOES PUBLIC

South Korea's National Intelligence Service has offered its assessment that Kim Jong Un's daughter is the designated successor, which is either a precise intelligence finding or a public statement designed to shape behavior inside Pyongyang — and the distinction matters more than it might appear. A credible succession signal reduces uncertainty for internal power brokers while simultaneously raising questions about timeline, which is not a dynamic that historically produces restraint. Separately, North Korea appears to have completed maintenance on its submarine capable of launching ballistic missiles and may be preparing a test. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: a nuclear-armed state with an unconfirmed succession in progress is also refurbishing its sea-based second-strike capability, while the world's major powers are consumed by a war in the Middle East and an ongoing conflict in Europe. The submarine program is not new; the timing of the maintenance completion is. Sea-based deterrence is specifically designed to survive a first strike, which means someone in Pyongyang is thinking carefully about what comes after the first shot — a level of strategic planning that deserves more attention than the news cycle is currently allocating to it.
