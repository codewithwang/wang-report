THE DEAL THAT WASN'T HORRIBLE

In 2015, a nuclear agreement was reached with Iran that a subsequent administration called the worst deal ever made, tore up, and replaced with maximum pressure. This week, after a war that has wounded nearly four hundred American service members, triggered IMF warnings of a global recession, and required a naval blockade of one of the world's most consequential oil chokepoints, negotiators are apparently converging on an agreement that looks, by most accounts, rather similar to the original. One could call this irony. Diplomats would call it the negotiating range.

The Hormuz blockade is working, in the narrow sense that it applies pressure. It is also working in a second, less intended sense: a sanctioned Chinese tanker crossed the strait anyway, Beijing has been quietly hoarding discounted Iranian crude throughout the conflict, and the Treasury secretary has had to publicly rebuke China as an "unreliable partner" — which is, diplomatically, the equivalent of expressing surprise that water flows downhill. The sanctions architecture was never designed to hold against a buyer large enough not to care.

Meanwhile in back-channel talks, negotiators are rediscovering what the 2015 drafters knew: the basic geometry of a nuclear deal — enrichment caps, inspections, phased relief — does not change simply because one side spent seven years insisting it did. The window for a deal was always there. The cost of finding it again was not small.

LEARNING FROM HORMUZ

The Iran war has produced an unintended curriculum. While American planners are absorbed with Hormuz, a different audience is taking careful notes. The lesson, crudely stated: you do not need to invade an island if you can simply prevent anything from reaching it. Taiwan's economy is, among other things, the world's most concentrated source of advanced semiconductors — a chokepoint dressed as a country. The implications of a naval blockade modeled on current Hormuz operations are not theoretical; Taiwan launched military drills this week specifically to test its ability to secure energy supply routes against precisely that scenario.

Beijing appears to be shifting its Taiwan calculus in this direction. The strategic case for blockade over amphibious assault is obvious: lower visible casualty counts, deniable escalation, and the ability to let economic strangulation accomplish what missiles would achieve more messily. That the Philippines this week attributed what appears to be cyanide planted near disputed South China Sea infrastructure to deliberate sabotage suggests the gray-zone component is already running.

And then there are the spies. Ten Taiwanese civilians charged this week, alongside active and former military officers, for passing intelligence to Beijing. A blockade requires accurate charts — shipping lane depths, fuel depot locations, undersea cable routes. A fifteen-thousand-strong US-Philippines joint exercise is one kind of deterrence signal. Twenty-five people on the inside passing maps is a different kind of signal entirely.

NATO'S LIFEBOAT PROBLEM

Deterrence is a confidence game. NATO's value has never rested solely on collective capability — it has rested on the credibility of the commitment, the belief that Article 5 means what it says regardless of who is in the Oval Office. The moment that commitment acquires conditions, the calculus changes for everyone: allies, adversaries, and the nervous middle ground in between.

Europe is now building a fallback. Officials are reportedly accelerating plans for what some are calling "European NATO" — a parallel security architecture capable of functioning without American participation. The architects of this plan would presumably prefer it remain hypothetical. But hypothetical plans require real infrastructure, real command structures, real money, and real political will, all of which tend to generate their own momentum once the planning begins in earnest. You do not build a lifeboat because you are confident the ship is seaworthy.

The genuinely interesting strategic question is not whether such a plan exists — it clearly does — but what its existence signals to Moscow. A Europe that can credibly hold without American support is, paradoxically, a stronger deterrent than a Europe whose defense is entirely contingent on Washington's disposition on any given Tuesday. Whether that irony has been fully absorbed by its authors is unclear. Whether counterparts in Moscow have absorbed it is considerably less so.

THE HUMAN IS OPTIONAL

Two events this week sit at apparent opposite ends of the military technology spectrum but share a common vector. In Ukraine, forces captured a Russian position using only ground robots and drones — no infantry, the first documented instance in this war. On the Korean peninsula, a head of state watched missile tests conducted from the deck of a new destroyer, a piece of theater communicating naval ambition regardless of whether the underlying capability matches the staging.

Ukraine's achievement is consequential precisely because it was born of necessity rather than doctrine. When manpower is finite and replaceable only slowly, automating lethality is not a philosophical choice but an arithmetic one. The successful execution removes a barrier — psychological as much as technical — that defense establishments around the world have been waiting to see cleared. Every ministry quietly testing autonomous ground systems will update its deployment timelines.

North Korea's destroyer-based tests operate on a different logic. The performance is directed partly inward — a demonstration of national modernity — and partly outward, toward the peninsula and regional competitors. What is notable is not the tests themselves but the venue: surface naval power projection represents an expansion of ambition beyond the peninsula's land borders. Whether the hardware is reliable enough to match the ambition is a separate question from whether they intend to close that gap. The tests suggest they do.
