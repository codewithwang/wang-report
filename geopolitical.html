OMAN'S TABLE, EVERYONE ELSE'S TERMS

Nuclear diplomacy has a specific ritual quality: both sides must be seen talking while simultaneously signaling that no concession is forthcoming. The current US-Iran engagement in Muscat follows the form precisely. Iran arrives with a ten-point ceasefire plan, which is described as a significant step and also as not good enough, which is either a negotiating posture or a contradiction, depending on your generosity. A 45-day ceasefire framework is reportedly under discussion. Meanwhile, separate reporting indicates Washington and Jerusalem are actively planning to eliminate Iran's nuclear infrastructure by force. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: good-faith talks are proceeding in a neutral Gulf capital at the same moment as contingency strikes are being war-gamed, and Tehran is warning of devastating retaliation to threats that haven't happened yet. This is not incoherence. It is the standard architecture of coercive diplomacy — keep the channel open long enough that any eventual military action looks like a last resort rather than a first preference. The outcome hinges less on the ten points Iran tabled than on whether Washington's definition of acceptable nuclear limits and Tehran's definition of sovereign entitlement can be squared. Historically, they cannot. Muscat has hosted these conversations before. It is a very pleasant city for talks that go nowhere slowly.

BEIJING'S CALIBRATED AMBIGUITY

The China-Taiwan data from March tells two different stories depending on which dataset you consult. Air incursions dropped by half; eleven consecutive days passed without a mainland aircraft detected in Taiwan's air defense identification zone. A Taiwanese opposition leader landed in Beijing for a rare cross-strait visit framed around peace. Read only those headlines and the picture is one of managed de-escalation. Read the others: a 40-day airspace lockdown near Japan and South Korea interpreted as a large-scale PLA rehearsal, eight naval vessels and an official ship detected around Taiwan's territory, and analytical work underway on how lessons from the Iran conflict might inform a Taiwan calculus. The reduction in visible aerial provocation coincides precisely with a large-scale military exercise conducted at sufficient geographic remove to avoid triggering immediate alarm. This is not contradiction — it is sequencing. The opposition visit provides a diplomatic surface; the naval presence maintains the encirclement architecture; the rehearsal builds operational readiness. Separately, Beijing is working to erode Taiwan's semiconductor advantage, which is the one asymmetric card Taipei holds that no military exercise can neutralize by itself. A strategy that simultaneously pursues political wedge, economic attrition, and military preparation across different time horizons is not ambiguous. It is thorough.

TRUMP'S GIFT TO BEIJING

The ISEAS survey result — China returning as Southeast Asia's preferred superpower — deserves a moment of attention, because it was not achieved through any particular Chinese charm offensive. Beijing spent much of the past several years generating genuine alarm across the region: reef seizures, water cannon deployments, aggressive patrols. That anxiety did not disappear. What shifted is the comparative calculus. Washington's transactional turn has made hedging toward Beijing feel rational rather than compromised. Manila illustrates the regional adaptation clearly: it signed a visiting forces agreement with France, diversifying its security architecture toward a European power with no obvious Indo-Pacific equities, precisely because the reliability of the traditional patron now requires a footnote. Meanwhile, China quietly resumed artificial island expansion in the South China Sea after a decade-long pause — a structural investment in permanent presence that requires no diplomatic cover when the regional audience is already recalibrating. The irony is that deterrence in the South China Sea was never purely military; it relied on the credibility of a security guarantee that looked unconditional. The moment that guarantee looks conditional, allies do not simply accept risk — they diversify. Paris is the beneficiary. Beijing is the beneficiary. The survey numbers are a lagging indicator of a process already well underway.

UKRAINE TAKES THE WAR TO RUSSIA'S LEDGER

For the first time since the full-scale invasion began, Ukraine launched more cross-border attack drones than Russia in a single month. The significance is partly symbolic and partly structural. Symbolically, it marks the end of the period in which Ukraine's drone campaign was purely reactive — absorbing incoming fire and occasionally retaliating. Structurally, the target set has shifted toward the revenue base that funds the war: Baltic oil tankers, the Novorossiysk energy hub, shadow fleet vessels that carry crude the sanctions regime was supposed to interdict. Drones against refineries and tankers are not battlefield weapons. They are instruments of economic attrition, aimed at the fiscal arithmetic that sustains Russian operational capacity. Russia's air defenses, formidable on paper, have proven inadequate at the scale and geographic distribution that Ukraine's drone campaign now requires them to cover. A former CIA director's assessment — that Russia no longer holds the upper hand — reflects this cumulative erosion more than any single engagement. The caveat is that attrition cuts both ways, and Ukraine's industrial capacity to sustain drone production at this tempo remains the binding constraint. But the strategic logic has inverted. Russia's oil windfall, which was supposed to outlast Western support for Kyiv, is now itself under direct and persistent attack.
