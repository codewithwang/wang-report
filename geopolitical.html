BEIJING'S TWO-TRACK TAIWAN SQUEEZE

The Chinese military closed 40 days of airspace and ran nuclear-response simulations across the Taiwan Strait and toward Japan — not because war is imminent, but because sustained ambiguity is the point. Simultaneously, the Kuomintang leader flew to Beijing for a meeting with Xi Jinping, generating precisely the imagery that Beijing's propaganda apparatus exists to capture: a Taiwanese politician legitimizing the relationship while the People's Liberation Army rehearses the alternative. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly — the opposition party of a democracy that Beijing threatens with invasion is attending a summit with the man whose military just simulated nuclear responses in that democracy's direction. Taiwan's security bureau reported stepped-up infiltration efforts targeting rank-and-file military personnel, which rounds out the picture neatly. The three instruments — military signaling, political theater, and intelligence penetration — are not independent campaigns. They are the same campaign run on different frequencies, designed to erode the will to resist before the question of actual resistance ever arises. The implication is uncomfortable: the most effective deterrence in the Taiwan Strait is not the missile count on either side, but whether Taipei's political class remains coherent enough to make deterrence credible in the first place. One of those variables is being actively worked on.

THE STRAIT THAT SWALLOWED A DEAL

Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz — again — in response to Israeli strikes in Lebanon, having apparently decided that one active front is insufficient. This came as a ceasefire framework was simultaneously being negotiated, misrepresented, and disputed across multiple governments, none of whom appear to be working from the same document. Iran's 10-point plan circulated through diplomatic channels while US secondary sanctions continued strangling Iranian oil exports, which rather complicates the notion that Tehran is negotiating from goodwill rather than necessity. The Trump administration threatened 50 percent tariffs on any country supplying Iran with weapons — a tool that is either a serious coercive instrument or a signal that the traditional sanctions toolkit has been exhausted and Washington is improvising. The Strait is not merely a transit route; approximately 20 percent of global oil passes through it, which means closing it is less a military act than an economic one, targeted not at Israel but at the energy markets of Europe and Asia. The pattern is legible: every diplomatic move is accompanied by a military counter-move designed to preserve leverage, and every ceasefire announcement arrives alongside fresh evidence that the parties are still fighting. The question is not whether a deal can be structured, but whether any party currently has an interest in one holding.

NATO AT THE LEAVING-NOTICE STAGE

The White House confirmed that the president intends to discuss leaving NATO with the alliance's secretary-general — not as a bargaining chip, apparently, but as a genuine agenda item, prompted in part by allied reluctance to participate in the US-Israeli campaign against Iran. This is the logical endpoint of a years-long devaluation of the alliance's mutual-defense premise: once the United States begins conducting offensive operations that NATO members do not endorse, the distinction between an ally and a free-rider becomes a matter of perspective. Ukraine, watching with appropriate alarm, noted that American decisiveness had produced results with Iran and asked why equivalent pressure could not be applied to Russia. JD Vance's assessment — that Ukraine represents the hardest war to solve — is technically accurate and politically convenient. Meanwhile, a secret Ukrainian unit struck Russian shadow-fleet vessels operating from Libya, Russia was confirmed to have provided targeting assistance to Iran against US forces, and Cameroon's soldiers were dying in eastern Ukraine, a detail that should require considerably more explanation than it is currently receiving. The Russia-Ukraine conflict is no longer a bilateral European war. It is a contest in which the opposing coalition has already incorporated Chinese equipment, North Korean artillery, Iranian drones, and an expanding African mercenary footprint. Whether NATO remains intact or not, that architecture is already built.

THE PACIFIC AXIS NOBODY NAMES

China's foreign minister traveled to Pyongyang the same week North Korea fired ballistic missiles for the second consecutive day, which is either a coincidence or a reminder that Beijing's regional relationships are not always ones it can fully choreograph. The visit was framed as an effort to bolster loosened ties — a phrase suggesting the relationship has been under strain, possibly because Pyongyang's deepening military partnership with Moscow has reduced its dependence on Beijing as a diplomatic patron. On the other side of the ledger, Japan participated in combat exercises in the Philippines for the first time, a quiet normalization of a military role that constitutional interpretation had long constrained. The Pentagon simultaneously announced a new refueling depot in the Philippines, expanding the logistics infrastructure that would underpin any sustained US naval presence in the South China Sea. The geometry is not subtle: Washington and its partners are building the physical architecture of a containment network, while Beijing manages a northeastern flank growing less predictable by the day. North Korea's utility as a pressure valve and nuclear distraction has always been more valuable to Beijing than the bilateral relationship itself, but that calculation becomes harder to sustain when Pyongyang's most important patron is Moscow and its most important export is artillery shells for a war that Beijing officially opposes.
