KHARG ISLAND AND WHAT COMES NEXT

The IEA has described the oil and gas disruption from the current Middle East conflict as worse than 1973, 1979, and 2022 combined. Let that settle for a moment. Three of the most consequential energy shocks in modern history, stacked together, apparently surpassed — or nearly so. U.S. strikes on Kharg Island, Iran's principal oil export terminal, remove any remaining ambiguity about whether this conflict has moved beyond symbolic exchange. Iran has since cut all diplomatic channels with Washington, which is the kind of signal that, in quieter decades, would have triggered emergency sessions at the Security Council. Instead, the IMF's managing director is on record warning that all roads lead to higher prices and slower growth, while legal experts are noting that threatened destruction of Iranian infrastructure may cross lines established at Nuremberg and codified in Geneva. Whether international law functions as a constraint or merely as a post-hoc accounting mechanism is, at this point, an empirical question the world is answering in real time. The structural damage here extends beyond oil prices: it is the erosion of the assumption that escalation has a ceiling — that some threshold of mutual risk produces restraint. That assumption has been load-bearing for eight decades. Its absence is not yet priced in.

THE PACIFIC REHEARSAL

China's 40-day airspace lockdown near Japan and South Korea is an unusual piece of news to process without noticing what surrounds it. Taiwan's opposition leader is on a six-day peace visit to Beijing — a trip that, whatever its intentions, produces the optics of a divided Taipei at precisely the moment the island's defense spending is in political dispute. Antelope Reef is expanding. The Philippines has just signed a visiting forces agreement with France, suggesting Manila's security partnerships are broadening faster than the South China Sea's diplomatic temperature can cool. And North Korea appears ready to test submarine-launched missiles from a vessel that recently completed maintenance. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: the region's most unpredictable nuclear actor is preparing new delivery system tests while its largest neighbor runs what analysts are calling a major PLA war rehearsal in adjacent airspace. Each development, in isolation, is explicable. Together, they describe a theater in which multiple actors are simultaneously stress-testing assumptions that have preserved a rough stability for seven decades. The Philippines-France pact is either a sign of coalition deterrence assembling in time, or evidence that the existing order's defenders are improvising at speed. Both can be true simultaneously, which is the uncomfortable condition that defines this moment.

EUROPE'S FRACTURES, VISIBLE AND USEFUL

Ukraine launched more cross-border drones into Russia in March than Russia launched into Ukraine — the first time that arithmetic has run in Kyiv's favor since 2022. The significance is partly tactical and partly psychological: it suggests production and logistics gaps are narrowing, or that Ukrainian operational tempo has found a ceiling-breaker. Russia's response was to threaten the Baltic states if they continue enabling Ukrainian strikes — a threat that NATO must treat as credible even while its most prominent patron is describing the alliance as a paper tiger and declining to endorse members who did not support the Iran operation. Into this environment, a European government leader has reportedly offered direct assistance to Moscow — not as a denial awaiting confirmation, but as a Bloomberg exclusive met with conspicuous silence where rebuttal should be. Zelenskyy, for his part, offered an Easter pause on energy strikes; Russia responded by killing four people in a bus strike. The gap between rhetorical restraint and operational reality has rarely been this legible. What makes this moment structurally significant is not any single defection or threat, but the cumulative signal: the set of states that can be relied upon to behave predictably within the Western coalition is contracting at precisely the moment the demands on that coalition are expanding.

THE ARCHITECTURE OF TRANSACTIONAL ALIGNMENT

Russian intelligence providing Iranian forces with a list of Israeli energy targets is, if confirmed, not a leak or an intelligence failure — it is a policy. The architecture it reveals is not new, but it is now operating without much pretense of concealment. What exists is not a formal bloc. There is no treaty, no shared ideology in any coherent sense, no coordination mechanism that Western intelligence can map and disrupt at a single node. What exists instead is a series of bilateral transactional accommodations — targeting data flows one direction, munitions another, diplomatic cover a third — each actor gaining something without formally committing to anything, the whole producing bloc-like strategic effects without bloc-like coordination costs. A European government offering assistance to Moscow fits the same pattern: deniable, useful, accumulating. North Korea's submarine program advances. The containment problem this creates is genuinely difficult. The Western response architecture — sanctions, diplomatic isolation, alliance solidarity — was designed for adversaries with legible command structures and identifiable pressure points. It is less obviously suited to a network of actors who share primarily a preference for an environment in which the current order cannot enforce its own rules. That preference, it turns out, is sufficient common ground.
