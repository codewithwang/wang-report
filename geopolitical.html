THE COORDINATED SQUEEZE

China is not reacting to events in the Taiwan Strait. It is conducting a coordinated, multi-domain pressure campaign that has the texture of a rehearsal. The Liaoning's southward passage, the Type 076 drone carrier heading to the South China Sea for drills, drone-laid mines designed to complicate US naval access, a physical barricade erected at Scarborough Shoal — these are not isolated incidents. They form a pattern too consistent to be coincidental.

Japan transited the Taiwan Strait with a destroyer — a legal passage Beijing nonetheless treated as a provocation — and received a targeted economic and diplomatic pressure campaign in response. Meanwhile, Beijing physically blocked the entrance to a disputed shoal, raising the cost of Philippine resupply by increments small enough to avoid a casus belli but large enough to matter.

The strategic logic is layered. Each individual action is deniable as routine. The cumulative effect is a sustained compression of the operating space available to US allies in the region. The Indo-Pacific commander has told Congress he needs more naval firepower. He is right. The question is whether Washington, currently consuming considerable strategic bandwidth in a shooting war with Iran, has the attention span to notice what is being constructed, methodically, just outside its peripheral vision.

THE WAR THAT DEFIES ITS OWN LOGIC

Let me make sure I understand this correctly: there is an active ceasefire, a continuing blockade, ongoing negotiations showing cautious progress, Iran attacking a container ship off Oman, and — if one former CIA analyst is to be believed — a nuclear strike order that was overruled by the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs over the weekend. All of this is happening simultaneously. The contradictions are not bugs in the policy. They appear to be features.

The blockade of Kharg Island is designed to starve Iran's oil infrastructure, with the Treasury Secretary putting a timeline on it in days. Iran, meanwhile, is responding with maritime harassment rather than capitulation. NATO is fracturing over the appropriate level of alliance involvement — testing cohesion in ways that recall every prior NATO stress-test but with a nuclear dimension that prior ones lacked.

What emerges is a conflict being run on multiple contradictory tracks simultaneously: kinetic operations, economic siege, diplomatic backchannel, and alliance management, each governed by its own logic and each undermining the others. The implication for the ceasefire talks is not encouraging. Progress measured in cautious increments while the oil wells are being shut in suggests someone is trying to negotiate a surrender without calling it one.

THE CREDIBILITY THAT POLLS

A Taiwan poll now reveals deep public doubt about whether the United States would actually defend the island militarily. This is not surprising. It is, however, instructive timing. The US Pacific commander told Congress that victory over Iran is necessary to deter a Chinese attack on Taiwan — a theory of deterrence that links theaters in ways Beijing has certainly noticed. The message reads: US credibility is fungible across conflicts. A faltering performance in one theater degrades the deterrent in another.

The US is simultaneously planning a fuel depot in the Philippines for South China Sea operations, and the Indo-Pacific commander is requesting additional naval assets. These are the correct moves. They are also moves being made while Washington's strategic attention is substantially absorbed elsewhere, its alliance relationships under visible strain, and its credibility the subject of open polling questions in the very territory it claims to deter aggression against.

Taiwan's doubts are strategically rational. Extended deterrence has always rested on a combination of capability and will. The capability investments are visible and real. The will — in an administration simultaneously managing a ceasefire, a blockade, a fractured NATO, and a reported internal argument about nuclear weapons — is considerably harder to read. Taipei is reading it carefully regardless.

UKRAINE LEAVES THE ROOM

Ukraine is escalating strikes on Russian oil infrastructure, which suggests it has not run out of reach. What it appears to be running out of is attention. Zelensky's public complaint that US envoys have not visited Kyiv — and his characterization of the absence as disrespectful — is not a diplomatic misstep. It is a signal about how far Ukraine has drifted from the center of Washington's strategic map.

Russia is threatening Europe in response to the infrastructure strikes — a pattern now sufficiently ritualized that it has almost lost meaning, except that the threats land in an alliance already strained by disagreements over a separate war entirely. The bandwidth problem is genuine: NATO members being asked to hold a coherent deterrent posture across a European ground war, a Middle East air campaign, and a simmering Indo-Pacific flashpoint are operating at the outer edge of institutional capacity.

The danger is not that Ukraine loses on the battlefield in the near term. The danger is that it loses on the priority list — that the diplomatic infrastructure sustaining its position quietly erodes while the principals are occupied elsewhere. Zelensky clearly understands the dynamic. The visit that didn't happen is, in its absence, more revealing than most visits that do.
