THE TAIWAN SQUEEZE TIGHTENS

A Japanese destroyer transited the Taiwan Strait last week and China responded with military drills — routine enough, on the surface. Less routine was the week's broader disclosure of how China is actually thinking about the problem. Stealth drone swarms targeting vessels. Underwater drones seeding minefields to enforce a blockade. CJ-10 cruise missiles now reportedly extended to 2,500 kilometres, which puts American bases in Japan within sustained strike distance. Read together, these aren't separate capability announcements — they're a coherent doctrine. Deny Taiwan relief, complicate any U.S. response, impose costs steep enough that the calculus of intervention shifts. The combination of saturation, mines, and long-range precision is designed to make a conventional naval rescue operation expensive before it begins. Meanwhile, Taiwan's own civil-defense readiness is described this week as suffering from a widening 'resilience gap' — a phrase that translates, roughly, to: the population's ability to absorb and sustain a shock has not kept pace with the sophistication of the threat being built across the strait. The asymmetry is stark. One side is publishing doctrine with increasing specificity. The other is still debating whether to stock enough bandages.

HORMUZ AS NEGOTIATING INSTRUMENT

The U.S. Navy seizing an Iranian vessel in the Strait of Hormuz while simultaneously engaging Tehran in nuclear talks is either a sophisticated form of coercive diplomacy or a scheduling problem. Iranian negotiators, per Reuters, are already discussing an interim deal — scaled back, enrichment questions unresolved — which means both sides want something without wanting to be seen wanting it. The credible threat of a Hormuz closure is Iran's primary leverage in that room, and this week's seizure is a reminder that the strait remains genuinely contested territory rather than a stable passage that America controls by default. Analysts drew the through-line explicitly: what Hormuz demonstrates at modest scale — energy markets shocked, transit costs spiking, naval assets suddenly very conscious of anti-ship missile envelopes — is precisely what a Taiwan strait closure would deliver at civilisational scale, with semiconductor supply chains added to the wreckage. Iran has spent the Ukraine war years studying the relevant lessons: drone economics, sanctions evasion, how to sustain a conflict against a wealthier adversary. It has been doing its homework. Whether the party across the table in these talks has absorbed the same curriculum is a question the interim deal's architecture will eventually answer.

SANCTIONS RELIEF, MEET THE REFINERIES

Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: the United States granted sanctions relief on Russian oil exports, and within hours Ukraine struck Russian oil refineries. Zelensky's position is that the relief directly funds continued attacks on Ukrainian cities — a claim that is arithmetically difficult to contest given that Russia's shadow fleet is reportedly moving ten billion dollars in oil at any given moment. Russia's economy is, by official admission, under genuine strain: interest rates are elevated, inflation persistent, war expenditure running ahead of projections. And yet the money keeps moving. The shadow fleet operates across jurisdictions, exploiting enforcement gaps, and those revenues buy next week's Shahed production run. Ukraine, lacking the diplomatic tools to interrupt the flow, is doing the next available thing: destroying the infrastructure that processes the oil before it reaches the tankers. Lavrov confirmed this week that Moscow sees no urgency in resuming peace talks — a posture that is easier to sustain when revenues are flowing. The refineries burning in Taganrog are Ukraine's editorial response to a diplomatic decision it had no vote on. Whether the logic of the strikes reaches the people who made that decision is a separate question entirely.

WHEN MISSILE TESTS BECOME WEATHER

North Korea fired two short-range ballistic missiles into its eastern waters this week. They flew approximately 140 kilometres, landed in the sea, and generated a UN warning about nuclear advances. The collective response was to note the range and move on. That normalisation is the actual story. The DPRK has achieved something of genuine strategic value: it has converted its missile tests into weather events — predictable enough to be priced in, persistent enough to resist decisive response, routine enough that each new launch carries slightly less diplomatic weight than the last. Every test that produces no meaningful consequence is a data point. Every sanctions regime that fails to halt the program is a proof of concept for states watching from a distance and running their own calculations. The lesson embedded in the North Korean trajectory — that a sufficient threshold of nuclear ambiguity dramatically reshapes the cost structure of external pressure — has not gone unobserved. The missiles themselves matter less than what their continued flight demonstrates about the gap between stated international norms and actual enforcement capacity. That gap, measured in kilometres and indifference, is the real payload.
