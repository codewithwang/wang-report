THE IRAN WAR'S NEGOTIATING THEATER

Let me make sure I understand this correctly: the United States is at war with Iran, has already struck Mehrabad air base and the South Pars gas complex — twice — and the current diplomatic posture involves threatening to destroy bridges and power plants unless a deal is reached by Tuesday. Tuesday. Iran, for its part, has rejected the American framework and submitted a ten-point counter-offer centered on what it calls a 'definitive end to the conflict,' which is either a principled demand or the most expensive opening bid in recent memory, depending on your priors. Meanwhile, a 45-day ceasefire framework is reportedly circulating in back channels, which suggests the actual negotiators have quietly agreed that Tuesday is a theatrical deadline rather than a real one. The infrastructure threat, however, is doing real work regardless of the outcome: international legal experts have begun invoking the laws of armed conflict, which adds a diplomatic cost to escalation that compounds over time. The war has no clean exit. Iran cannot accept terms that look like capitulation; the United States cannot walk away from a posture it publicly committed to. The gap between those constraints is where the ceasefire proposals live — and where they tend to die.

TAIWAN'S SIMULTANEOUS SQUEEZES

The geometry of Taiwan's position this week is almost instructive in its tidiness. On one axis: the main opposition leader lands in Beijing for a 'peace' visit while Taipei warns, with notable directness, that the trip is cover for lobbying against US arms sales to the island. On another axis: China reportedly spent 40 days locking down airspace near Japan and South Korea in what defense analysts are calling a large-scale PLA rehearsal. On a third axis: Beijing is accused of systematically targeting Taiwan's semiconductor expertise — recruitment, acquisition, industrial espionage — as a hedge against the hard-power scenario it is simultaneously rehearsing. And threading through all of it, the lessons being drawn in Chinese strategic circles from the Iran war: what does a US military commitment actually look like under sustained pressure, and how long does it hold? The opposition visit is the soft vector; the airspace exercise is the hard signal; the chip campaign is the long game. The Taipei government's response — calling for direct cross-strait talks on its own terms — is the correct move and almost certainly insufficient. What Taiwan is experiencing is not a crisis but a sustained, multi-domain pressure campaign that operates below the threshold of any single alarming headline.

THE VACUUM CHINA DIDN'T HAVE TO CREATE

China has resumed dredging at Antelope Reef. The Philippines has signed a visiting forces agreement with France. Southeast Asian publics, per the ISEAS survey, now prefer China as their regional superpower over the United States — a preference that, the fine print clarifies, has less to do with enthusiasm for Beijing than anxiety about Washington's current posture. This is worth sitting with. The strategic competition for Southeast Asia has been a central project of American foreign policy for a decade. The Philippines pact with France is a direct consequence of uncertainty about US reliability — Manila is not abandoning Washington, it is hedging against it. And China, which has spent years generating genuine regional fear through gray-zone operations and reef construction, is now benefiting from a trust deficit it did not manufacture. Trump's NATO comments — calling the alliance a 'paper tiger' and renewing the Greenland claim in the same breath — feed directly into this calculus. Credibility in alliance politics is not replenished quickly. The reef construction continues; the surveys will not improve on their own; and France is not a substitute for the Seventh Fleet, however symbolically useful the pact may be for Manila.

UKRAINE FINDS ITS STRIDE, QUIETLY

For the first time since February 2022, Ukraine launched more cross-border attack drones than Russia in a single month. The same week, Ukrainian strikes hit Novorossiysk — a Black Sea energy hub — and separate drone operations in the Baltic are reportedly disrupting Russian crude exports at a moment when oil revenues should be benefiting from Middle East-driven price increases. Former CIA Director Petraeus says Russia no longer holds the upper hand. These data points form a coherent picture: Ukraine has adapted, industrialized its drone production, and begun targeting the economic architecture of Russian war finance rather than just military positions. The complications are structural, not tactical. The Iran war is consuming US munitions and political attention simultaneously, and the supply of American air defense components to Ukraine is described as 'at risk' if the Middle East commitment extends. The strain in US-Ukraine relations is not rhetorical — it is a resource allocation problem with a hard ceiling. Ukraine is winning the drone war and losing the supply chain negotiation. How long the battlefield trajectory can outrun the logistics problem is the question that the March data, however encouraging, cannot answer.
