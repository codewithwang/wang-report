IRAN NEGOTIATES FROM THE RUBBLE

The conventional wisdom after six weeks of conflict was that Iran would emerge diminished, eager to cut whatever deal was on the table. That reading misses something important. Former American envoys have noted, with some care, that war has given Tehran new leverage over its nuclear programme — the logic being that a state that has absorbed significant punishment and kept fighting is a state that has demonstrated it can absorb significant punishment and keep fighting. The Islamabad talks proceed against this backdrop, with Washington warning of fresh strikes if negotiations fail and Tehran's proxies simultaneously reminding everyone that Hezbollah, supposedly crippled, is still launching attacks of surprising intensity into Israel. Meanwhile, the Strait of Hormuz question hangs unresolved, with Washington signaling publicly that Tehran's current posture is not what any agreement contemplates. Gulf states, for their part, are quietly reassessing security arrangements that looked stable a year ago. The sum of this is not a defeated adversary suing for peace on American terms. It is a negotiation between two parties who have both paid costs they would prefer not to repeat, which is a meaningfully different thing — and produces meaningfully different outcomes.

BEIJING'S TWO-TRACK TAIWAN PLAY

On Friday morning, Xi Jinping hosted the chairwoman of Taiwan's largest opposition party in Beijing and delivered a statement about absolute intolerance for certain outcomes. By Friday afternoon, Taiwan's defense ministry was tracking Chinese warships and warplanes off its coast. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: the peace messaging and the military signaling are simultaneous, delivered within the same news cycle. This is not a contradiction in Chinese strategic communication; it is the point. The KMT visit provides political cover and a wedge into Taiwanese domestic opinion, while the persistent military pressure reinforces what the stakes of the wrong choice are. In the background, a wargame published this week suggested the United States lacks sufficient airpower to defend Taiwan against a Chinese move by 2035, and China has separately demonstrated a military AI system that its developers claim outperforms human commanders in assault scenarios with 90 percent recall accuracy. The intelligence picture is no more reassuring: Washington assesses that Beijing is preparing to deliver new air defense systems to Iran, a move that would complicate any American operation in a Middle Eastern theater and, by extension, the resource calculus for a simultaneous Indo-Pacific contingency. The two-track approach is coherent. That is what makes it worth watching.

THE SOUTH CHINA SEA HARDENS

The Philippines opened a coast guard district command on Thitu Island this week — a small piece of disputed reef in the Spratlys that Manila has held for decades, which now has something resembling permanent institutional infrastructure. The United States, separately, is establishing a fuel depot in the Philippines expressly to support South China Sea operations. Chinese officials did not immediately react. That measured non-reaction is itself a data point; the days of Beijing protesting every incremental Philippine move loudly have given way to something quieter. The pattern that has emerged over the past eighteen months is one of gradual, mutually reinforcing commitment: Manila extends its physical presence, Washington extends its logistical footprint, and each move provides political cover for the next. What this looks like from Beijing is a slow foreclosure of options in waters it considers historically its own. What it looks like from Manila is a hedging strategy against a neighbor whose coast guard vessels have made a habit of water-cannon diplomacy. Neither reading is wrong. The structural question is whether incremental commitment on both sides continues without a triggering incident, or whether the accumulated friction eventually produces one.

UKRAINE'S AMBIGUOUS SUMMER

A 32-hour Easter ceasefire was announced, observed imperfectly, and greeted by almost no one with any expectation of durability. This is not cynicism; it is pattern recognition. What is more interesting is the simultaneous emergence of peace-deal reporting — Bloomberg citing movement toward negotiations — alongside Ukrainian assessments that Russia is reinforcing and may expand operations or seek talks by summer, the two possibilities being presented as near-equivalents. The ambiguity is structural: a negotiation that occurs under continued military pressure is a negotiation that rewards the party with greater tolerance for sustained cost. Separately, the White House is reportedly weighing punitive measures against NATO allies who have not met spending commitments, stopping short of withdrawal but using the alliance's collective security guarantee as implicit leverage. Moscow, for its part, has threatened to attack Baltic states that allow Ukrainian drones to transit their airspace — a statement calibrated to test the threshold of Article 5 without formally crossing it. What the spring of 2026 looks like, across both the battlefield and the alliance structure, is a set of actors probing for the location of lines they officially cannot cross, while negotiators in various capitals attempt to construct a framework before anyone finds out exactly where those lines are.
