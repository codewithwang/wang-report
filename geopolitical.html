THE CARRIER SENDS ITS MESSAGE

The Liaoning's passage through the Taiwan Strait this week was preceded by something worth noting: a Japanese destroyer made the same transit first. Beijing's response — dispatching its carrier southward, tracking 24 aircraft and seven ships around Taiwan's ADIZ, circulating doctrinal analysis of drone-laid mine strategies for denying US naval access — suggests this was less a drill and more a communiqué. The sequence matters. When a Japanese warship exercises freedom of navigation through a strait China has long sought to treat as its own, and the response involves a carrier group plus apparent signaling on anti-access weapons, the message isn't aimed at Tokyo alone. It's aimed at every capital watching whether Japan's new defense posture will be tolerated without consequence. The drone-mine reporting adds texture: the PLA appears to be developing layered denial strategies that complicate the US calculus in a Taiwan contingency well before any shots are fired. What's emerging is a slow normalization of extraordinary pressure — each transit, each exercise, each mine scenario becoming the new baseline from which the next escalation departs. Escalation ladders work that way. You climb them one rung at a time until you look down and forget where the ground was.

THE COALITION BUILDS ITSELF

China is physically blocking a shoal in the South China Sea with ships and a barrier. The same week, the US and Philippines launched Balikatan — reportedly their largest iteration yet — with Japan joining as an observer in exercises that explicitly encompass the South China Sea. Australia and Canada are conducting multilateral operations in the same waters. Beijing's new Type 076 drone carrier is heading south for drills of its own. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: China is simultaneously blockading a disputed feature, deploying a next-generation carrier, and expressing fury that allies are conducting exercises in international waters. The geometry is not subtle. What has shifted in 2026 is less the presence of US forces — those have rotated through for decades — than the multilateralization of the response. Japan's participation in Philippine-based exercises marks an operational shift in what Tokyo is willing to do, not merely say. The emerging coalition lacks a formal security architecture, but it is acquiring the functional characteristics of one: shared intelligence, interoperable forces, a common geographic focus. Beijing is, in a meaningful sense, constructing it for them. Every shoal barrier and carrier deployment hands the alliance-builders another recruitment argument they didn't have to make themselves.

CEASEFIRE WITH THE BLOCKADE ATTACHED

The ceasefire with Iran has been extended — at Pakistan's request, pending a unified proposal from Tehran — while the US military maintains its blockade. Let's sit with that construction for a moment. The ceasefire pauses the shooting. The blockade does not pause. The IEA is already modeling how a prolonged Hormuz disruption might redraw global energy routes, which is the kind of scenario analysis that, once published, tends to accelerate the hedging behavior it describes. The combination — kinetic pause, economic pressure intact — is a recognizable template: keep enough military threat visible to constrain Iranian options while forcing negotiated capitulation on terms Tehran hasn't yet accepted. Whether Iran produces a proposal acceptable to Washington in the near term is genuinely uncertain; what is not uncertain is that Iran's economy faces compounding strain from both the direct military damage already sustained and the ongoing maritime chokehold. The implications extend well past the Gulf. Asian importers who built supply chains around accessible Iranian crude are quietly repricing their contingency plans. The Hormuz shock, even with the guns temporarily quiet, is already repricing the future — and the IEA publishing that analysis is not a neutral act.

EUROPE HOLDS THE LINE, ALONE

Ukraine is hitting Russian warships in occupied Crimea and oil infrastructure deep inside Russian territory. Russia is issuing threats serious enough that Germany has formally summoned its ambassador. And US special envoys have not visited Kyiv — an absence Zelensky described as disrespectful, which is diplomatic understatement given the operational tempo on the ground. The structural gap between the war's intensity and Washington's visible engagement is widening. Ukraine is escalating, demonstrating retained strike capability against high-value Russian assets. Russia is responding with pressure directed at European capitals rather than at Kyiv, which is its own signal: the strategy has shifted from breaking Ukrainian military capacity to breaking European political will. Germany's decision to summon the ambassador suggests Berlin is not, for now, flinching. But the arithmetic is getting harder. European governments are being asked to sustain support while Washington's attention migrates toward the Pacific and the Middle East, and while North Korea continues testing weapons that may eventually travel westward through channels the ceasefire with Iran does nothing to close. The war is not ending. The political coalition that sustained the response to it is under simultaneous pressure from multiple directions, which is precisely the desired effect.
