THE ARCHIPELAGO OF PRESSURE

China's pressure campaign against Taiwan has never been purely military, and this week is a useful reminder of how many instruments it actually involves. PLA warships conducted a rare transit through waters southwest of Japan, apparently in response to a Japanese destroyer's passage through the Taiwan Strait — a tit-for-tat geometry that keeps expanding its geographic scope. A new Type 076 drone carrier is heading for South China Sea drills, which matters because drone-carrier doctrine represents a qualitative shift in how China projects force, not merely a quantitative one. The Philippines is being told it may lose access to emergency energy supplies if it continues hosting U.S. military exercises near Taiwan — economic coercion dressed as logistics. Taiwan's own minister made a rare visit to a disputed South China Sea island, a gesture that signals resolve but also costs diplomatic capital with neighbors who have their own disputes with Beijing. A Chatham House analysis put the economic logic plainly: a Taiwan crisis would cause far more global damage than any Hormuz disruption. That is either an argument for why the international community should deter Beijing, or an argument for why the bet is worth making, depending on which capital is reading it. Taiwan's own polls show deepening doubt about U.S. military commitment. When reassurance needs to flow in both directions simultaneously, the deterrence architecture has developed something that looks less like stability and more like anxiety distributed across an alliance.

THE CEASEFIRE THAT ISN'T

The operative question about the Iran ceasefire is not whether it will hold but whether it was ever meant to, in the sense that word usually implies. Iran renewed attacks on shipping in the Strait of Hormuz during the same window that Washington announced an indefinite extension of the ceasefire. Let me make sure I understand this correctly: a ceasefire under which one party continues attacking ships is a ceasefire the way a thermostat set to off that keeps running is off. The market has its own translation — oil above $100, with the IEA issuing what it characterized as its largest supply disruption warning in recent memory. The Houthis, watching the same diplomatic theater from Sanaa, signaled their return to Red Sea operations; whether that is opportunism or coordination, the timing is the message. What the current arrangement appears to offer Tehran is the ability to continue exerting pressure at a sub-escalation threshold while Washington manages the optics of a deal. The energy markets, less susceptible to optics, are pricing the underlying reality. Thousands more U.S. forces are heading for the region, which is either a deterrent deployment or a hedge against the ceasefire's continued non-holding. At $100 a barrel, the market has already rendered its verdict on which reading is more likely.

THE DETERRENCE CHAIN

The head of U.S. Indo-Pacific Command said something this week that deserves more attention than it received: that prevailing against Iran is a prerequisite to deterring China from moving on Taiwan. This is not strategic color commentary. It is a public articulation of a deterrence chain — one theater's outcome setting the credibility terms for another — that has typically lived in classified strategy documents rather than open testimony. The logic is coherent; the exposure of it is new. AUKUS submarine rotations are confirmed for 2027, with the suggestion that rotational operations out of Australia could begin sooner, which the Indo-Pacific commander offered as evidence of progress rather than aspiration. The Dutch defense establishment assessed this week that Russia could be ready for NATO conflict within a year of concluding operations in Ukraine — a timeline that, if accurate, means the three-theater pressure the United States has publicly acknowledged is not sequential but simultaneous. Meanwhile, a South Korean minister's comments about a suspected North Korean nuclear facility created friction in U.S. intelligence-sharing arrangements, a reminder that the alliance network underwriting this deterrence posture has its own internal fault lines, which adversaries read as carefully as they read hardware inventories. Whether three-theater deterrence articulated this publicly reads as confidence or as a warning sign of overextension depends entirely on the observer. The adversaries have their assessment. The bond market has another. They are unlikely to be the same.
