THE SIMULTANEITY PROBLEM

The core tension in US Indo-Pacific strategy has always been sequencing: handle one crisis without creating an opening for another. This week's reporting suggests that sequencing is failing. US officials are now openly acknowledging that an Iran conflict complicates contingency planning for Taiwan — a sentence that, read carefully, admits the two theaters are competing for the same finite inventory of assets. Meanwhile, China deployed live-fire exercises near Luzon during US-Philippine war games, tested its Type 076 drone carrier in the South China Sea, and conducted naval drills that analysts describe as explicitly capture-oriented. The US House earmarked $500 million in military aid for Taiwan, which is real money, but Taiwan's own defense budget remains hostage to domestic political deadlock, suggesting the client isn't fully synchronized with the patron. The hellscape drone plan — deploying thousands of drones to complicate PLA planning — may shift calculus at the margins. But layering deterrence architectures on top of overextended force posture is a different proposition than having the force posture in the first place. A Taiwanese minister made a rare visit to a South China Sea island for drills. Xi, meeting a Taiwanese opposition figure, said the two sides of the strait will unite. That is not a new line. The timing is.

IRAN'S TRIANGULATED OFFER

The geometry of the current Iran nuclear talks requires a diagram. Iran's foreign minister flew to Islamabad. Kushner and Witkoff followed. Trump told Reuters that Iran plans to make an offer aimed at satisfying US demands. The EU, watching from Brussels, called sanctions relief premature and pressed for a uranium enrichment cap at 5% by April 30. And while these diplomatic planes were in the air, Washington announced sanctions on a major Chinese oil refinery in Dalian — 400,000 barrels per day of processing capacity — along with forty shippers, for buying Iranian crude. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: the administration is simultaneously courting Iran toward a deal and squeezing the buyers who have been sustaining Iran's oil revenues, and apparently considers these two moves compatible. Whether they are depends entirely on what Iran's offer contains. The EU's insistence that relief is premature suggests that the transatlantic read on what constitutes a satisfying deal diverges meaningfully from Washington's. That divergence is itself the story. If the US and EU are not coordinating definitions, the deal that gets announced and the deal that Europe will honor may not be the same deal — which is precisely how the original JCPOA eventually unraveled.

RUSSIA'S COMPOUNDING ARITHMETIC

There is a particular kind of war news that presents as a battle update but functions as an economic data point. Russian oil exports are slumping as Ukrainian drones systematically hit ports and refineries. The EU has formally approved a ninety-billion-euro loan to Ukraine and added a new sanctions package. Putin's approval rating, per the state pollster — an institution with structural incentives to report favorably — has fallen to its lowest point since before the invasion. Ukraine, meanwhile, describes its frontline position as the strongest in a year, following a drone strike that killed twelve Russian FSB officers at a command post in occupied Donetsk. The FSB's presence at a forward command post is its own signal: intelligence services running frontline operations suggest coordination failures in the conventional command structure. Each of these data points arrives in isolation; assembled, they describe an attritional dynamic moving against the initiating power on three simultaneous axes — battlefield, treasury, and domestic legitimacy. That does not mean the war ends soon. Attritional dynamics are precisely defined by their refusal to resolve quickly. It does mean that the structural position is deteriorating in ways that even a compliant polling apparatus cannot indefinitely obscure.

ALLIANCES, TERMS AND CONDITIONS APPLY

Two stories this week, easy to miss individually, form a single pattern when placed side by side. First: an internal US government email apparently floated suspending Spain from NATO and reconsidering Washington's stance on the Falkland Islands — leverage, presumably, over spending commitments or political alignment. Second: the Philippines pushed for a South China Sea code of conduct by year-end, noting with some weariness that the world is owed a resolution, while ASEAN-China talks on that same code have stalled for years without consequence. The common thread is what happens to collective security architecture when the anchor power treats membership as conditional, and when the rules-based maritime order is a negotiating position rather than a floor. Spain's potential suspension matters not because it will happen but because the option was written into an internal document — which means it exists as a tool. The Philippines' urgency on the maritime code matters because it implies Beijing will not move without external pressure, and Washington's bandwidth is, by every account this week, stretched thin. Allies do not recalibrate based on reassurances. They recalibrate based on what they observe. This week, they observed quite a lot.
