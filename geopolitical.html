THE LOOP IS THE POINT

A Japanese destroyer sailed through the Taiwan Strait. China launched military drills. This is now the established call-and-response — each transit triggers a proportional demonstration of displeasure, which in turn validates the next allied transit as a freedom-of-navigation statement, which triggers another set of drills. Neither side is willing to let the other establish precedent by default, so the precedent that actually accumulates is mutual escalation on a fixed schedule.

Meanwhile, satellite imagery shows China tightening its grip on a disputed South China Sea shoal with ships and a physical barrier. Analysts are now asking whether PLA minelaying drones could enforce a Taiwan blockade without firing a single conventional weapon. Taiwan, for its part, is being warned of a widening resilience gap in its civil defences — the infrastructure that would matter most if the blockade scenario ever moved from think-tank paper to operational reality.

The pattern across all of this is deliberate ambiguity. No single action constitutes an act of war. Each is defensible in isolation. But taken together — the drills, the shoal closure, the theoretical minelaying, the resilience gap — they describe an encirclement tightening incrementally, each turn of the screw too small to be a casus belli, the accumulation large enough to matter enormously.

THE HORMUZ NEGOTIATION

Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly. Iran has declared the Strait of Hormuz closed, announced that any approaching ship will be targeted, and is simultaneously planning a phased airspace reopening — suggesting the conflict is winding down enough to manage civilian aviation while remaining hot enough to threaten one-fifth of global oil supply. The United States, for its part, is threatening to end a ceasefire if a deal is not reached next week, which implies there is a ceasefire in place that is also somehow consistent with a closed strait and 3,400 Iranian dead.

The Strait of Hormuz is not a surgical instrument. Closing it does not distinguish between adversary and neutral; it shocks the system broadly. The logic, then, is either that Iran calculates the shock is more painful for its adversaries than for itself, or — more plausibly — that the closure is a negotiating posture, raised to maximum threat precisely because a deal is close enough to be worth the leverage.

China's behind-the-scenes role is the least legible variable. Its interest in stable energy flows through Hormuz is self-evident; its interest in not visibly breaking Iran's leverage is equally clear. Threading those without appearing on either side of the ledger is the kind of quiet statecraft that the current moment seems designed to test.

RUSSIA'S ARITHMETIC PROBLEM

Ukrainian drone strikes are costing Russia an estimated $100 million per day in lost oil revenues, with roughly 880,000 barrels per day removed from Moscow's export capacity. That is not a symbolic number. It is the kind of attrition that compounds — damaged infrastructure takes time to repair, disrupted export relationships take time to rebuild, and a federal budget calibrated to a specific oil price does not quietly absorb the shortfall across quarters.

Russia's response has been to escalate rhetorically upward. Moscow published a list of sites across Europe and the United Kingdom it could strike for supporting Ukraine. Zelensky has warned that Belarus may be drawn back into the conflict as a second front. A Moscow-born gunman opened fire in Kyiv, killing six — an incident that each side will frame to its own purpose.

The target list publication is worth reading carefully. States do not normally enumerate potential strike targets in public documents unless they want the enumeration itself to do work — to shift the psychological calculus of allied governments, to slow weapons transfers, to introduce friction into the coalition without a missile being fired. Whether it succeeds depends entirely on whether European capitals treat the list as a signal worth heeding or noise worth ignoring. History suggests they will disagree among themselves, which is probably the point.

THE PROLIFERATION ARC

North Korea launched multiple ballistic missiles on Sunday, prompting emergency security meetings in Seoul and Tokyo. This is now routine enough to have a rhythm: launch, emergency meeting, joint statement, quiet resumption of normal schedule. The launches are not militarily decisive — they are political communications, directed partly at Washington, partly at the region, partly at a domestic audience requiring periodic reminders of deterrent posture. The fact that the response is also now routine is either reassuring or alarming, depending on whether you think normalisation signals stability or accumulating desensitisation.

Less routine: a NATO defense contractor has apparently been licensed to supply satellite services to China's military — a disclosure that sits uncomfortably alongside every allied statement about technology controls and dual-use export restrictions. The mechanism by which that license was granted will interest parliamentary oversight committees, if they get to it.

And then there is the US intelligence assessment flagging nuclear conflict risk between India and Pakistan, driven by terrorism, regional tensions, and ISIS-Khorasan's continued footprint. South Asia is the theater where the proliferation architecture — built on deterrence theory, back-channel communication, and the shared understanding that use would be catastrophic — faces its least forgiving stress test. No other active regional flashpoint currently carries the same consequence for miscalculation, which makes it the one least covered by the news cycle.
