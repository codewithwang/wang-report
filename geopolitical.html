THE HORMUZ ARITHMETIC

The Strait of Hormuz closure is now less a crisis than a condition. Tehran has declared reopening 'impossible' — a formulation that serves dual purposes: it preserves domestic credibility while signaling that the diplomatic off-ramp will cost something. Meanwhile, oil markets are pricing sustained disruption, an American naval chief has stepped down mid-conflict, and a sitting president has asked the world not to rush him on a resolution timeline. The economic logic is elementary — roughly 20% of global oil supply transits the strait, and every week of closure reprices not just crude but every downstream assumption built on cheap energy. Less visible is the military economics. The conflict has drained US stockpiles of critical, expensive weapons at a pace that will take years to reconstitute. That is not a detail buried in procurement reports — it is a structural constraint that every strategic competitor on the planet can now read in the open press. A campaign that may have been intended to project decisive force has, as a secondary effect, published an inventory gap. The question is not whether the United States can win a fight; it is whether the deterrence ledger, which works on perception of readiness as much as actual capability, now looks thinner than it did six months ago. The answer, inconveniently, appears to be yes.

THE WINDOW BEIJING DIDN'T OPEN

Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: the US is fighting a war that is depleting its precision-weapons inventory, that US officials say complicates contingency plans for Taiwan's defense, and that is occupying the carrier strike groups and logistics chains that any Pacific response would draw on — and simultaneously, China is releasing video of a new aircraft carrier, conducting military pressure around Taiwan, running an intimidation campaign to block Taiwan's president from visiting Africa, and watching Taiwan's own defense budget stall in political deadlock. The US Indo-Pacific commander has apparently said out loud that victory over Iran is needed to deter a Chinese attack on Taiwan, which is the kind of statement that clarifies the dependency chain rather neatly. A US House bill earmarking $500 million for Taiwan is the legislative branch signaling concern that the executive branch's attention is elsewhere. Beijing did not engineer this situation — it did not start the Iran war, did not drain the US arsenal, did not create Taiwan's domestic budget paralysis. It merely allowed the situation to develop while teasing, probing, and watching. The carrier video is the geopolitical equivalent of clearing your throat at a board meeting where the other party's lawyer just left the room.

UKRAINE'S BORROWED ARITHMETIC

The European Union approving a $106 billion loan for Ukraine is, structurally, a statement about duration. Loans of that size do not get constructed for conflicts expected to end by summer. Zelenskyy calls Ukraine's position its 'most stable in months,' which is accurate and also carefully bounded — stable is not advancing, and stable is not resolved. Ukraine's military is striking FSB command posts in occupied Donetsk, which indicates operational capacity, while Zelenskyy separately warns that the Iran war may disrupt US missile defense aid, which indicates the supply chain anxiety running beneath every public-facing optimism. Turkey is attempting to revive Russia-Ukraine negotiations through back channels, Erdogan briefing NATO's secretary-general in what appears to be a positioning move ahead of any eventual settlement framework. US public confidence in the administration's handling of the conflict is falling, according to Pew — a domestic political signal that tends to precede either renewed engagement or conspicuous disengagement. The EU loan essentially bets that European institutional commitment can substitute for fluctuating American attention. Whether that substitution holds under the pressure of a simultaneously active Middle Eastern theater and a shifting Pacific threat calculus is the question the next six months will answer, probably without anyone framing it quite so directly.

XI SPEAKS, PRECISELY ONCE

After weeks of silence on the Middle East conflict, Xi Jinping has chosen this moment to speak. The timing deserves more scrutiny than the content. A conflict that has tied down US military resources, disrupted global energy markets, complicated Washington's Pacific posture, and generated broad international anxiety about escalation — and Beijing stayed quiet through all of it, watching the situation develop, until a moment of its own choosing to re-enter as a voice of measured concern. The 'why now' framing in the coverage is the right question. The answer is probably not that Beijing suddenly felt compelled to weigh in on humanitarian grounds. More plausibly, the intervention is calibrated: early enough to position as a constructive actor before any ceasefire framework crystallizes, late enough to have watched the full shape of American strategic overextension become visible. This is not cynicism about any particular government's motives — it is the basic grammar of great-power positioning, practiced by every major state in every previous era. The difference now is that Beijing is doing it in a moment when the convergence of the Iran war, Taiwan pressure, and European rearmament is creating a genuinely multipolar stress test of the post-Cold War order. Speaking once, carefully, at the right moment, is how you remind everyone that you haven't been absent — just patient.
