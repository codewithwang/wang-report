PEACE TALKS, WARPLANES OVERHEAD

The choreography is almost admirable in its brazenness. While Beijing arranges its first meeting between Xi Jinping and a Taiwanese opposition leader in a decade — a warm tableau of cross-strait goodwill — it simultaneously shuts down 40 days of airspace near Taiwan and positions warships in the strait. The message, if you squint past the diplomatic staging, is not contradictory: it is a demonstration of the full menu. Dialogue on Beijing's terms, or the other option. The opposition visit confers legitimacy on the framework that Taiwan's governing party has spent years rejecting, and it does so at a moment when a wargame is circulating in Washington showing the United States losing Taiwan by 2035 due to simple arithmetic — not enough planes. Meanwhile, a new airbase appears to be taking shape in the South China Sea, and the first sea-based rocket launch from those waters is being planned. The Philippines responded by opening a coast guard base, which is the correct move and also the move of a country that has read the wargame. The dual-track approach — handshakes in the meeting room, hardware on the horizon — has a long history of working precisely because the target audience wants so badly to believe in the handshake.

IRAN'S PECULIAR KIND OF WINNING

Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: a country absorbs a significant military campaign, watches its infrastructure take serious damage, and emerges from the experience with greater nuclear leverage, Gulf states rethinking their entire security architecture, and a generation-defining moment that its adversaries now have to manage indefinitely. That is what former American envoys and sitting prime ministers appear to be saying, more or less in unison. The mechanism is almost classical — war as accelerant for the very thing the war was meant to prevent. The nuclear programme was the pretext; it is now, apparently, the prize. What the conflict has reshuffled is the regional calculus. Gulf states that had been quietly normalizing relationships and hedging toward American security guarantees are now doing the arithmetic on what those guarantees are actually worth, with warships being reloaded and Saturday's Islamabad talks as the deadline. The NATO secretary-general invokes a North Korea moment — meaning a threshold crossed, a status locked in, a deterrence dynamic that no subsequent diplomacy fully unwinds. Iran's leadership, described as battered but confident, with new cards, understands this framing better than anyone. The confidence is not despite the damage. It may be because of it.

THE EASTER TRUCE AND ITS FOOTNOTES

Russia and Ukraine agree to a ceasefire for Orthodox Easter, and simultaneously Moscow threatens to strike Baltic countries that allow Ukrainian drones to transit their airspace, and simultaneously airpower analysts publish findings that the Russian air force is substantially more dangerous now than when it began the invasion. These things are all true at once, which is the correct frame for evaluating what a temporary truce actually means. The Bloomberg report of movement toward a peace deal has the careful hedging of sources who know that "movement toward" and "arrival at" are different continents. What has changed structurally is the Russian air force's capability curve — a military that entered this war with significant doctrine problems has spent three years developing them into habits, adapting to contested airspace, and improving its effectiveness in ways that will outlast any ceasefire. Easter is a 72-hour pause. The adaptation is permanent. The truce nonetheless matters, if only because both sides need to demonstrate to their respective audiences that they are not entirely opposed to the concept of not fighting. That is a low bar, and clearing it is not nothing. Peace talks, when they eventually produce something, will be negotiated in the shadow of what both militaries have learned.

THE ALLIANCE MATH GETS COMPLICATED

The architecture of post-war security alliances is undergoing a kind of stress test that its designers did not anticipate, largely because the designers assumed the patron would remain a reliable constant. It has not. The White House is reportedly weighing punitive measures against NATO allies — not withdrawal, the reports are careful to note, but something more surgical: retribution without exit, influence without obligation. The distinction matters to lawyers and treaty scholars; to the allies being targeted, it mostly clarifies the direction of travel. India's response to the Middle East conflict is instructive in a different register: a renewed push for defense self-reliance, the logic being that dependence on external suppliers is a vulnerability that recent events have rendered newly visible. This is not a new Indian position, but the conflict has given it fresh urgency. The Gulf states are running similar calculations. The through-line across these responses — punished allies, hedging partners, self-arming middle powers — is not anti-Americanism so much as a rational updating of priors. When the security guarantee becomes conditional, the guaranteed party starts asking what conditions they can live without. The answers to that question are being written right now, and they will shape alliance structures for the next several decades.
