THE PEACE HAND, THE MILITARY FIST

Beijing hosted Taiwan's main opposition leader this week — the first such meeting in a decade — and Xi Jinping used the occasion to invoke the threat of independence as justification for everything that follows. The framing is worth noting: the meeting was described as a peace gesture, yet within the same news cycle, fifteen Chinese military aircraft crossed the median line into Taiwan's ADIZ, and Taipei's defense minister was characterizing the military threat as increasingly severe. This is not inconsistency. It is choreography. The opposition visit generates headlines about dialogue and cross-strait goodwill, useful ahead of a Trump meeting in May and Taiwan's 2028 presidential election. The military sorties remind Taipei's current government — and Washington — what the alternative to dialogue looks like. The wargame result published by a US defense outlet this week adds the structural context that makes the choreography land: American air forces, as currently constituted, cannot stop a Chinese takeover of Taiwan by 2035. When deterrence calculations shift from 'we can stop this' to 'we might not stop this,' the diplomatic theater stops being theater. It becomes the opening of a longer negotiation about terms.

REEFS, ROCKETS, AND FUEL DEPOTS

Three moves, one week, one contested body of water. The Philippines opened a coast guard district command on Thitu Island in the South China Sea — a quiet but pointed assertion of administrative presence in disputed territory. Washington announced plans to establish a fuel depot in the Philippines to support South China Sea operations, converting a political alliance into physical logistics infrastructure. And Beijing announced its first planned sea-based rocket launch from the South China Sea, a development with obvious dual-use implications. Chinese officials declined to comment on the Philippine base opening. They didn't need to. The pattern of the past several years in the South China Sea has been incremental: each party advances its position by a degree small enough to avoid triggering a formal response, while the cumulative movement is considerable. The fuel depot matters because sustainment is strategy — a forward logistics node converts a distant ally into a reliable operational partner. The rocket launch announcement matters because it normalizes military activity in contested waters under the banner of civilian space enterprise. None of these moves individually constitutes a provocation. Together, they describe a waterway that is being systematically militarized by multiple parties simultaneously.

IRAN TALKS, IRAN LEVERAGE

Talks between the United States and Iran resumed in Islamabad this week, mediated by Pakistan, with Vance in attendance and Trump warning Tehran publicly even as diplomats met privately. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: a country that just lost a war is sitting across the table with new leverage. Former US envoys writing in the Guardian make exactly this argument — that the conflict has paradoxically strengthened Iran's nuclear position, because the war consumed the regional architecture that once contained it. Hezbollah, reported this week to be defying assessments of its decimation, continues to complicate the picture. The Islamabad talks surfaced immediate disagreements: Iran claimed agreement on releasing frozen assets; Washington disputed this within hours. Meanwhile, US intelligence indicates China is preparing to deliver new air defense systems to Iran — a transfer that would materially improve Tehran's ability to defend its nuclear infrastructure from a future strike. The frozen assets, the Strait of Hormuz, the air defense transfer, the ceasefire diplomacy: these are not separate negotiations. They are the same negotiation, conducted across multiple channels, with China now explicitly present as a supplier rather than a bystander. The administration's leverage calculus has to account for the fact that Iran's most important patron is also its primary geopolitical rival.

UKRAINE'S NARROWING WINDOW

Russia agreed to a 32-hour Orthodox Easter ceasefire — a gesture calibrated to be just long enough to generate goodwill and just short enough to mean nothing militarily. Bloomberg reported movement toward a potential peace deal; Zelensky acknowledged a small window for peace while declining to specify how small. Russia simultaneously threatened to attack Baltic states that allow Ukraine to use their airspace for drone operations, which is a notable escalation of rhetoric directed at NATO members even as ceasefire language circulates. The juxtaposition is instructive. A 32-hour ceasefire and a threat against Baltic airspace are not contradictory signals from Moscow — they are the same signal: negotiation proceeds on Russian terms, and the geographic scope of Russian demands has not contracted. For Kyiv, the window Zelensky references is real but constrained on multiple sides: by battlefield attrition, by Western political fatigue, and by the emerging contours of what Washington appears willing to pressure Ukraine to accept. The Easter ceasefire will expire. Whether the 'small window' Zelensky sees remains open afterward depends less on the diplomats in the room than on decisions being made in capitals that are not Kyiv.
