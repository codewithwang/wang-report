ONE WAR TOO MANY

The United States has spent the better part of a decade recasting its strategic posture around the Pacific — the great pivot, the rebalance, the various doctrine documents that all pointed the same direction. Then Iran. Three carrier strike groups are now committed to the Middle East, the war has drained stockpiles of exactly the high-end munitions that any Taiwan scenario would require, and the timeline for ending it is, in the president's own framing, not something to be rushed. American officials, speaking with appropriate anonymity, are frank: the Iran war complicates contingency planning for Taiwan. Complicates is doing significant work in that sentence. The weapons problem is structural. Guided munitions are not produced on demand; the industrial base that makes them runs on multi-year cycles. Every precision strike in the Strait of Hormuz represents a deferred capacity elsewhere. Meanwhile, oil prices are climbing as markets price in protracted instability over the world's most critical chokepoint. The irony is not subtle: a war nominally about regional security is degrading the conditions for the security commitment that arguably matters most to the long-term global order.

TAIWAN'S INSIDE GAME

An occupational therapist in New Taipei City shows up to work carrying emergency survival gear in her backpack. She takes the stairs now, on purpose, in case the elevators fail. This is the texture of Taiwan's civil defense effort — granular, voluntary, anticipatory. The government has moved to an all-hands model, weaving defense awareness into civilian life on the theory that deterrence is partly a matter of demonstrated will. Whether the will translates into capacity is a separate question. In the legislature, the main opposition has declined to back increased defense spending, with a KMT heavyweight explaining the reasoning in terms that amount to: we have doubts about the strategic direction. Washington has responded with a House bill earmarking $500 million in military aid — which is money, but money that does not resolve the domestic political problem of a society simultaneously preparing for war at the individual level and blocking the institutional resources that preparation requires. China, for its part, has been running an intimidation campaign against the Taiwanese president's Africa trip — a signal about the cost of asserting any international presence, diplomatic or otherwise. The survival gear in the backpack and the blocked defense budget tell the same story from opposite ends.

CHINA'S SELECTIVE PATIENCE

China released video teasing a new aircraft carrier and reaffirmed its commitment to building up islands in the South China Sea. Its navy has been conducting drills with an explicitly amphibious character — the kind that practice the logistics of moving large formations across water against opposition. Alongside this, a Taiwan-based analyst notes that China may have outpaced the United States in AI applications for drone swarms, even as China overall lags on military AI broadly. The picture that emerges is one of deliberate prioritization: not winning every dimension of the competition simultaneously, but identifying the specific capabilities — carrier projection, amphibious lift, autonomous swarms — that matter for the one scenario that matters, and concentrating there. Russia's experience in Ukraine has been studied carefully, and the lesson drawn, according to one analysis, is not 'don't invade' but 'if you do, don't do it the way Russia did.' Information operations, economic pressure, diplomatic isolation first — the Russia playbook, refined by watching Russia execute it badly. A Taiwanese minister's rare visit to a disputed South China Sea island, where she observed coast guard drills, is a reminder that the competition over narrative and physical presence runs alongside the competition over hardware, and neither side is conceding either.

THE FRAYING COALITION

An internal Pentagon email surfaced this week floating the option of suspending Spain from NATO as punishment for its position on the Iran war. The email is not policy — it is options development — but options development reveals what an institution is actually thinking about. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: the alliance that spent the post-Cold War era expanding is now internally floating the suspension of a member state over a war that the alliance as such was not convened to authorise. Meanwhile, the EU approved a ninety-billion-euro loan for Ukraine and a fresh round of Russian sanctions, which represents a different kind of coalition maintenance — Europe doubling down on the threat visible from its eastern border even as the Atlantic relationship develops new stress fractures over the one to its south. Russia is not obviously winning this test of endurance. Putin's approval rating has fallen to its lowest point since before the Ukraine invasion, according to a state pollster — data that requires discounting but not dismissal. The drones are still flying over occupied Donetsk. The money is still flowing to Kyiv. The West is fraying at the edges but not yet frayed through, which is the optimistic reading, and the only open question is whether the fraying is cosmetic or load-bearing.
