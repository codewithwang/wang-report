BEIJING'S COMPOSITE SIEGE

China is not operating sequentially. This week: a new carrier teased in state media, a drone-capable amphibious assault ship deployed to the South China Sea for trials, PLA warships making "rare" transits off southwest Japan — the kind of adjective that tends to depreciate quickly. Taiwan's defense minister made a rare visit to a South China Sea island for drills. Japan's ground forces joined exercises and drew a Chinese carrier response.

On the diplomatic track, multiple countries blocked Taiwan's president from transiting their airspace during an Africa trip. Congratulations were issued. The mechanism requires no explicit orders — only that third parties understand where their interests lie and act accordingly.

The Philippines, a US treaty ally running drills near Taiwan, received what amounts to an energy warning: emergency supplies might not flow reliably during a crisis. This is coercion styled as logistics.

The composite picture is a campaign running across every domain simultaneously — naval capability signaling, diplomatic encirclement, economic leverage, and infrastructure development across more than 11,000 Chinese-administered islands. No single move constitutes a red line. That is the point. The pressure accumulates without ever offering a clean moment to respond. By the time the trajectory becomes undeniable, the options will have narrowed considerably.

HORMUZ AS REHEARSAL

Iran attacked ships in the Strait of Hormuz. The US threatened strikes. A senior American admiral argued before Congress that winning in the Middle East is how you deter China from attacking Taiwan. This is either a sophisticated strategic calculation or a remarkable admission that deterrence is now a finite resource requiring triage — most likely both.

Let me make sure I understand this correctly: demonstrating decisive force against a regional power in a confined waterway signals resolve to a major power preparing options against an island 5,000 miles away. The same causal chain has been invoked, with irregular results, across most US military engagements since 2003.

Chatham House, in the same news cycle, published analysis quantifying what is actually at stake: a Taiwan crisis would cause far more global economic damage than a Strait of Hormuz disruption. The Strait moves roughly a fifth of global oil supply. Taiwan's fabs produce the advanced semiconductors embedded in virtually every piece of critical technology on earth. The comparison is not close. Which makes it worth asking why Western capital markets have not priced Taiwan risk more seriously, and why governments continue treating it primarily as a security problem rather than a civilizational one.

Hormuz is the stress test. Taiwan is the exam.

ALLIANCE ARITHMETIC

The White House has developed a tiered ranking of NATO allies — "naughty or nice" — scored by defense spending and alignment on US priorities. This is a negotiating tactic, or it is an alliance being converted into a transaction with one party holding the ledger, or it is both. The ambiguity is presumably intentional.

The Dutch government, separately, warned that Russia could be ready for a NATO-level conflict within a year of the Ukraine war ending. One year. The distance between that warning and current European defense investment levels is one of the more uncomfortable gaps in contemporary security policy, especially as the continent debates whether American commitment to Article 5 carries fine print.

The EU approved a $106 billion loan to Ukraine after Hungary's veto finally collapsed — a result requiring months of sustained diplomatic pressure. Zelenskyy described Ukraine's battlefield position as the most stable in months. These are real achievements. "Most stable in months," applied to a war running past two years, is a phrase that carries its own commentary without requiring editorial addition.

Russia is watching an alliance that is simultaneously more unified on Ukraine funding and more fractured on internal political economy. The incentive to run out the clock is obvious. The question is whether European defense investment scales faster than Russian reconstitution.

Sources: Defense News · bing news · The Washington Post · Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty
THE QUIET ARMS RACE

The UN's nuclear watchdog warned this week that North Korea's weapons program is moving "quite fast" — language that, from an agency constitutionally disposed toward understatement, deserves careful parsing. "Quite fast" from the IAEA is what a cardiologist means by "I'd like to run a few more tests."

Pentagon officials, presenting to Congress on the Pacific, articulated a "denial defense" posture for Taiwan: make any Chinese attack physically infeasible rather than merely strategically costly, through new missile deployments, allied integration, and contested logistics. The strategic logic is sound. The honest subtext is what it implies about earlier deterrence postures — that presence and reputation were always insufficient, and that the gap is now being acknowledged and systematically filled.

The two stories share a common thread: nuclear-era deterrence is being rebuilt from first principles across Asia, in response to actors whose programs were never fully arrested. North Korea's arsenal is not frozen. China's is expanding. The assumptions that made forward deterrence feel automatic in the 1990s have not aged well.

What the region is constructing now is the architecture that should have been built twenty years ago. Whether the construction timeline is shorter than the decision timelines it is meant to influence is a question the news cycle this week did nothing to resolve.
