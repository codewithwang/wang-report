BEIJING'S TWO-TRACK TAIWAN PLAY

China is running two simultaneous operations against Taiwan, and the genius of it is that they look like opposites. On one track: the PLA is simulating nuclear responses across the Taiwan Strait and restricting 40 days of airspace in patterns that analysts are struggling to explain as anything other than invasion rehearsal. On the other track: the head of Taiwan's opposition Kuomintang is in Nanjing invoking the island's founding father and calling for reconciliation, while Beijing's state apparatus amplifies every frame of it. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly — the same week the People's Liberation Army runs its most provocative nuclear drills in years, Beijing is also hosting Taiwan's largest opposition party for a charm offensive. This is not contradiction; it is coordination. The military track establishes the cost of defiance. The political track offers the off-ramp. Taiwan's National Security Bureau, meanwhile, is warning that Chinese infiltration has moved beyond elites and is now targeting rank-and-file citizens — the unglamorous, granular work of manufacturing consent before the question is even formally asked. The thesis Beijing is testing: whether enough of Taiwan's own political class can be made to feel that resistance is the provocation, not the drills.

THE PACIFIC FILLS WITH HARDWARE

The United States and its partners are quietly, methodically turning the Western Pacific into a logistics network that did not exist five years ago. The Pentagon has added a fuel depot in the Philippines. Japan has moved from observer to active participant in Philippine war games — a distinction that would have required a constitutional debate not long ago and now merits a paragraph in a wire story. F-22 Raptors, the aircraft the US declined to export to any ally for two decades, are now appearing at Philippine exercises as a deliberate signal. The hardware argument being made is plain: the cost of moving against Taiwan or the South China Sea is rising. The irony is that it may be working too slowly. ISEAS survey data show Southeast Asia has shifted back toward China as its preferred great power patron, driven less by affection than by anxiety about the reliability of the alternative. Vietnam's new president is heading to Beijing. An Indonesian fisherman just pulled a Chinese underwater drone from his nets — the kind of infrastructure you build when you intend to stay. The Americans are building a network; China is already inside it.

TWO CEASEFIRE DEALS, ZERO OVERLAP

There appear to be two entirely different Iran ceasefire frameworks being discussed, and both sides are describing the other's version as the agreed basis for talks. Iran has published ten points that reassert control of the Strait of Hormuz and preserve the country's right to uranium enrichment. The American position, as stated publicly, is no enrichment, removal of existing nuclear material, sanctions relief in exchange. These are not opening positions that will converge through negotiation; they are incompatible definitions of what Iran is. Meanwhile, Israel has carried out what it is calling its largest strikes of the war, Iran has shut the Strait of Hormuz in response, and at least 182 people have died in Lebanon in a single wave of strikes. The Strait closure is the move that focuses minds — roughly a fifth of global oil passes through it — and Iran knows this. The ten-point plan reads less like a ceasefire proposal than a document establishing that Iran's negotiating floor is everything the other side considers non-negotiable. Diplomacy conducted in parallel with a Strait closure is a specific kind of diplomacy. The gap between the two frameworks suggests either one side is not negotiating in good faith, or both are.

RUSSIA, IRAN, AND THE FRAYING WEST

Zelenskyy is making an argument that the American administration appears unwilling to hear: that Russia has been actively helping Iran target US forces, that the intelligence exists, and that the decision not to act on it reflects a political choice rather than an evidentiary gap. The underlying claim — that Moscow is providing targeting assistance to Tehran against American military assets — would, in an earlier period, be treated as a threshold event. It is instead being noted and set aside. At the same moment, Donald Trump is discussing NATO withdrawal in what the alliance's secretary general describes as a frank meeting, which in diplomatic vocabulary is what you call a meeting that went badly. The architecture of the post-1945 Western security order is being stress-tested at every joint simultaneously: a Middle East in open escalation, a Pacific filling with competing military infrastructure, a European alliance whose cornerstone is signaling ambivalence about its own membership, and a Russia-Iran coordination that crosses lines that were once considered inviolable. North Korea, perhaps sensing the moment, has tested cluster-bomb-tipped ballistic missiles and fired additional rounds toward the sea, as if to confirm that every actor with a grievance and a weapons program has concluded the same thing: the cost of provocation is lower than it has been in decades.
