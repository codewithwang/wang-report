CHINA'S TWO-HANDED TAIWAN SQUEEZE

The 40-day airspace closure near Taiwan, nuclear-strike drills simulating US attacks, a new airbase materializing in the South China Sea — and then, in the same week, Xi Jinping invites the KMT opposition leader to Beijing for a very public conversation about peace. The military and political tracks are not contradictions; they are the two jaws of the same vice. One hand applies pressure sufficient to make Taiwan's defense posture look existentially futile; the other offers the opposition party — which skipped its own government's defense talks in Taipei to make the Beijing trip — a vision of cross-strait accommodation that the ruling party refuses to entertain. That Washington has reportedly grown chilly toward Taipei adds a third variable the calculation didn't need but Beijing will gladly accept. The message to Taiwan's electorate is legible without being stated: your government is isolated, your patron is reconsidering, and the people willing to talk to Beijing are the ones willing to talk to you. Military calendars and diplomatic calendars have been synchronized, which is either coincidence or strategy — and Beijing does not do coincidence.

THE HORMUZ PARADOX

Iran fought a war, absorbed significant punishment, and has emerged from it — by the New York Times's accounting — confident and holding new cards. The card in question is the Strait of Hormuz, still closed, which is performing more negotiating work for Tehran than any ballistic exchange during the actual fighting. Trump casts doubt on the ceasefire over the strait's continued closure; the NATO chief signals that allies may need to act to secure it, while also noting an unhealthy codependence on American power — the White House's response being that NATO was tested and already failed. Wait, let me make sure I understand this correctly: the alliance that deterred Soviet armor for four decades has apparently failed a test whose rubric was set after the test was graded. The Hormuz situation has exposed something more uncomfortable than Iranian resilience: the measurable gap between collective Western declaratory policy and collective Western action. Every revisionist actor currently watching — and they are all watching — is now in possession of that measurement. A battered Iran holding a maritime chokepoint while its adversaries debate procedural questions is not a country that lost.

MANILA BRINGS A CAMERA TO A FLARE FIGHT

The geometry of China's South China Sea position is becoming less ambiguous by the week. Maritime militia vessels are anchored across multiple features in the Kalayaan Island Group. Chinese aircraft are firing flares at Philippine Coast Guard planes conducting entirely legal patrols in waters Manila administers. A large new airbase is reportedly under construction — which would, when complete, extend the operational reach of Chinese military aviation over waters that the Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, and Brunei also claim. Manila's response has been to open its own coast guard base in the disputed zone, a gesture of sovereign assertion that is proportionate in legal terms and asymmetric in every other dimension. The Philippines is not outbuilding China; it is establishing the evidentiary record of a state that showed up, maintained presence, and was met with flares and militia formations. That record will matter in international arbitral forums and allied consultations, and Beijing knows it — which is presumably why flares rather than something more kinetically decisive. The documentation strategy only works, of course, if the documented party eventually faces consequences. That part remains to be determined.

A CEASEFIRE AND ITS DISCONTENTS

A first official ceasefire in Ukraine — achieved after Zelenskyy's sustained push — should generate something approaching unambiguous relief. Instead it arrives alongside Russia threatening strikes against Baltic states that allow Ukrainian drones to transit their airspace, North Korea announcing ballistic missiles equipped with cluster-bomb warheads (a system whose distinctive feature is that it is banned under international humanitarian law for reasons that are not subtle), and a trilateral peace meeting described as happening soon, without a date or venue. The ceasefire, in other words, is a data point inside a negotiation, not the negotiation's conclusion. Russia's Baltic threat deserves to be read carefully: it is simultaneously a warning to NATO's eastern flank, a probe of Article 5 credibility, and a standing invitation for the alliance to argue internally about what constitutes an act of war under which circumstances. Pyongyang's weapons announcement, timed as it is, reminds every party watching that the eastern theater has suppliers with their own timelines and incentive structures. These are not side effects of the diplomatic moment. They are the strategic environment in which any agreement will have to survive.
