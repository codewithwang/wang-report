TAIWAN'S COMPETING PEACE OFFENSIVES

The week Taiwan's opposition leader flew to Beijing for what his office called a Journey of Peace, China simultaneously restricted its airspace over a forty-day window in a pattern analysts associate with intensive military exercises, and the island's defense ministry logged additional Chinese aircraft sorties and naval vessels operating near its territorial waters. The symmetry is instructive. The KMT visit — the first by a party chairman of significance since 2016 — arrives one month before a scheduled summit between the American and Chinese presidents at which Taiwan is expected to feature prominently. Beijing's calculations are not obscure: a domestic Taiwanese interlocutor willing to travel and be photographed smiling complicates the picture for any administration trying to frame the island's defense as a matter of unified Western principle. The military activity running concurrently is a reminder that the peace on offer has a specific architectural requirement. For Manila, watching all of this from a position of direct exposure in the South China Sea, the answer was a visiting forces agreement with France — a country with genuine Indo-Pacific interests and no Taiwan commitments to complicate its posture. The diversification of security relationships in the region is accelerating precisely in proportion to uncertainty about the anchor relationship.

THE VETO AS STRATEGIC INSTRUMENT

When Russia and China vetoed the Security Council resolution aimed at reopening the Strait of Hormuz, they performed a service that requires no elaborate coordination to explain: both states benefit from sustained pressure on the United States in the Persian Gulf, and the veto costs them nothing they were not already prepared to spend. Reports that China has been providing material assistance to Iran's missile program, if accurate, suggest the relationship is more than diplomatic. The architecture here is not a formal alliance — it is a shared interest in exhaustion, in raising the price of American forward presence until the arithmetic of maintaining it becomes electorally inconvenient. Ukraine's continued strikes on Russian oil export infrastructure at Ust-Luga, and the assessment from former American intelligence officials that Moscow's operational position has deteriorated, add a further dimension: the axis of obstruction is performing well diplomatically at a moment when at least one of its members is not performing especially well militarily. THE ALLIANCE THAT CANNOT BE LEFT

The legal observation that a president cannot withdraw the United States from NATO without congressional authorization is technically correct and practically beside the point. The statement of intent matters independently of its enforceability; allies who must hedge against the possibility of abandonment will begin hedging now, regardless of what the statute books say. The White House's scheduled meeting with NATO's secretary-general, set against the backdrop of the Iran conflict and European divergence over how to respond to it, captures the alliance's present condition with some precision: the institutional machinery is operating, the political consensus is not. North Korea's ballistic missile launches this week — timed, as they usually are, to coincide with moments of maximum American distraction elsewhere — underscore that the deterrence architecture in East Asia is equally dependent on the credibility of American commitments. Pyongyang appears to be completing maintenance on a submarine capable of launching ballistic missiles, which would represent a meaningful expansion of its second-strike capability. The common thread across the NATO debate and the Korean peninsula is the same: the value of a security guarantee is a function of the guarantor's demonstrated willingness to bear costs. That willingness is the thing currently under negotiation.
