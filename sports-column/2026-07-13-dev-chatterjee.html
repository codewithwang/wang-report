The $9.612 Billion Seahawks Sale That a Rounding Error Froze

This week sport's economics outran the referees meant to manage them, from World Cup ratings blowing past format critics to an NFL sale frozen by a rounding-error stake.
Dev Chatterjee Senior Correspondent Tuesday, July 14, 2026

The World Cup's Real Number

France meet Spain in Arlington on Tuesday and Argentina meet England in Atlanta on Wednesday, and between them sits the real center of gravity for world sport this month: Lionel Messi, three wins from a trophy he may never chase again, facing England for the first time in his international career. Kylian Mbappe carries a sprained ankle into the Spain match, and Lamine Yamal is waiting on the other side. That is the drama. Here is the number underneath it: Fox's broadcast of the USA-Belgium Round of 16 match drew 30 million American viewers, the most-watched soccer telecast in US history, outdrawing this year's NBA Finals Game 5 and the World Series Game 7. The 48-team format was mocked before a ball was kicked for diluting the group stage into filler. The audience showed up anyway, and it grew fastest exactly where FIFA's American broadcast partners needed it most: through the group stage, Telemundo's Spanish-language viewership rose 122 percent over the last World Cup, against a 92 percent increase on Fox's English-language channels. The tournament didn't just get bigger. It got more valuable in a specific, bilingual direction, and FIFA's current US rights deal, split between Fox and Telemundo, is the instrument that renewal math will run through next.

The Compliance Officer Wins

Meanwhile in Seattle, sport produced its biggest number of the week without a ball involved. Vinod and Neeru Khosla agreed to buy the Seahawks for $9.612 billion, an NFL record by 59 percent over the previous mark, the Commanders' $6.05 billion sale in 2023. It is the largest transaction in American sports history, and the league will not let Khosla sign anything until he does something almost comically small by comparison, parked behind the nine-point-six-billion-dollar headline like a rounding error: sell his 3.1 percent stake in the San Francisco 49ers, which the NFL's cross-ownership bylaw forbids him from holding alongside a controlling stake in a divisional rival. It is a fairly Californian irony that a venture capitalist who made his fortune betting on things scaling instantly is now waiting on a compliance review to catch up to his own check. He is not alone in discovering that ownership rules move slower than ownership money this year: Mark Cuban is suing Mavericks majority owner Patrick Dumont, alleging he was frozen out of the team's 104-acre Valley View arena project despite holding onto 27 percent of the franchise, a suit that remains active and unresolved as of this week.

Somewhere in the NFL's league office, a compliance officer is reviewing a 3.1 percent stake with the same seriousness they'd apply to a $200,000 deal, unmoved by the nine zeros sitting behind it in the same file. FIFA's broadcast partners, watching Messi and Mbappe fill American living rooms in two languages at once, have the opposite problem: too much value, arriving faster than anyone planned to price it, with the next US rights negotiation now the place that gap gets settled.
