The House Runs the Mavs Now

Miriam Adelson's decision to dismiss Nico Harrison and hire Mike Schmitz completes the Mavericks' transformation from Mark Cuban's equity experiment into a Las Vegas Sands property.
Dev Chatterjee Senior Correspondent Sunday, May 10, 2026

The Inheritance Runs Out

When Miriam Adelson and Patrick Dumont paid the equivalent of a $3.5 billion franchise valuation to acquire 69 percent of the Dallas Mavericks in December 2023, the transaction included the inherited front office. Nico Harrison, the Nike Americas executive Mark Cuban had recruited as general manager in 2021, stayed in place. He was not a hire the Adelsons made. Two and a half seasons later, with the Mavericks holding lottery positioning again, Harrison is out and Mike Schmitz, ESPN's former director of player personnel, is in. This is the first front-office decision the new ownership made without Cuban's original footprint attached to it.

The capital lineage is not incidental. Las Vegas Sands Corp, which Dumont heads as CEO following Miriam's inheritance of Sheldon Adelson's estate, draws its entire operating revenue from Asia: Marina Bay Sands in Singapore and the Sands China Ltd portfolio in Macau, after the company sold The Venetian Resort Las Vegas to Apollo Global Management in March 2021. The family now controlling a Mavs front office built its institutional identity on environments where information advantages are structured, personnel authority is vertical, and probabilistic outcomes are managed by design. An NBA draft lottery is the one venue where that design meets genuine randomness.

Schmitz and the Lottery Math

Mark Cuban's position offers contrast. Sports Illustrated reported this week that Cuban has identified a new ownership stake, consistent with NBA passive-investor rules permitting minority holdings under a principal owner. Cuban's organizational logic was always information arbitrage: the Mavericks were his proof-of-concept for analytics-first front-office management before that phrase existed as a category. The Adelson replacement runs on a different axis. Schmitz arrived from the analytical media layer, ESPN's draft-evaluation infrastructure, rather than from the player-representation or consulting pipelines that more frequently produce GM appointments. The preference appears to be for internal scouting capability over the brand-hire model Harrison represented.

The specific franchise stakes concentrate the picture. The Mavericks held the first overall selection in the 2025 draft and are now reported to be chasing a back-to-back lottery position in 2026. The Adelsons are running an asset whose value compounds at each lottery position, which from a capital standpoint is a recognizable structure: systematic variance extraction across a defined multi-year horizon. Whether Schmitz can execute the evaluation side of that structure is the first decision attributable entirely to Adelson ownership. The 2026 draft lottery runs in May. The first evaluation on record is already due.

The question the Adelson-Mavericks arrangement poses is not whether casino capital belongs in the NBA. The question is whether the organizational grammar Las Vegas Sands built, with vertical authority and expected-value certainty at its core, holds through the draft-lottery randomness the NBA imposes on franchise rebuilds. The 2026 lottery runs this month. Mike Schmitz's first draft board will be on record before the end of June. Patrick Dumont will know by June's draft night whether the house math transferred.
