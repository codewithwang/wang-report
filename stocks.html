Consumer discretionary names are absorbing conflicting headlines while chip stocks trade on faith. The tape is doing what it does when the calendar is thin: index funds drift, benchmarks hold their line, and every stock story reads a little more dramatic than the number underneath it.

Technology remains the loudest sector in the book, and today it is loud in both directions. Apple's rally continues to be described as money leaving other AI trades rather than money arriving new. NVIDIA's most valuable chip is one Jensen Huang says he never held a working sample of before betting the company on it. Adobe sits well off its highs while still drawing buy calls from the Street. Semiconductors, leveraged and otherwise, remain the engine room, with energy and the broad index sleeve continuing their quieter, supporting roles.

Individually, Amazon is being framed as historically cheap. Okta shows up in the news for the wrong reason, a vishing campaign borrowing its own security warnings. Lululemon is fighting a consumer lawsuit that will test whether shoppers separate the brand from the courtroom.

The advisors have been discussing trimming the portfolio's largest position in NVIDIA gradually, in tranches, and using the proceeds to open a new stake in Berkshire Hathaway. They are also weighing additions in healthcare and international exposure, and drafting hard exit rules for the portfolio's most speculative names. Then there is Intel, where the choice left is binary: commit, or cut it loose. Limbo, the advisors note, is not a position.
