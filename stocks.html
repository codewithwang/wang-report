The indices did not offer a clean read this morning. Corporate headlines did. Bank of America's bubble warning on chipmakers rattled Intel and TSMC and dragged AMD down with them, a reminder that a single analyst note landing at the wrong hour can move a sector faster than the rate mood does.

The portfolio's technology book, its largest and most concentrated holding, absorbed most of that turbulence, the semiconductor-linked leveraged position amplifying the AMD slide rather than cushioning it. Software infrastructure had the better morning: Okta traded up alongside Autodesk and Dynatrace on what looked like a sector-wide re-rating rather than anything Okta specific. Consumer cyclical was mixed. Amazon drew fresh analyst attention on a milestone no company has reached, while Lululemon filed into a tariff lawsuit. The fixed income and index fund sleeves of the portfolio sat out both moves.

Individually, Adobe's purchase of Topaz Labs answered the AI capability question Wall Street had been asking it for months. Apple's foldable phone order, at a price point near twenty five hundred dollars, is being read internally as a margin story more than a volume one.

Her advisors are trimming the NVIDIA position in tranches over the coming weeks, redirecting the first tranche into a new Berkshire Hathaway stake. They are drafting hard exit rules for the portfolio's smaller speculative names before any of them doubles again.
