Amazon's shares finished June down twelve percent, the sharpest single line on an otherwise mixed tape, with the S&P and Nasdaq holding their positions and the Hang Seng running on its own clock. Rate mood stayed watchful rather than decided, and nothing in today's index prints argued for a change of posture. Semiconductor sentiment carried the weight, as it usually does when the word bubble starts appearing in bank notes.

The technology book, the largest sleeve in this portfolio by a wide margin, took its cues from Bank of America's warning on AI chip valuations. AMD fell five percent, and Nvidia slid on news of a revenue-sharing arrangement for AI startups rather than on anything resembling a miss. Adobe spent the day patching, not trading, after an emergency release for seven maximum-severity flaws in ColdFusion and Campaign Classic. Consumer cyclical had its own reckoning: Lululemon sits seventy-eight percent below its all-time high, a number that no longer reads as a dip. Energy and the leveraged semiconductor sleeve moved with the broader chip anxiety rather than against it.

Inside technology, the advisors have opened a conversation about trimming Nvidia toward a smaller share of the book, in tranches over the coming weeks, with the freed capital earmarked to open a position in Berkshire Hathaway. Okta drew attention less for its stock than for its chief executive's renewed billionaire status on the strength of an agentic AI bet, a detail the advisors noted without acting on. Apple's Hide My Email feature was found leaking real addresses, a headline more reputational than financial for now. Healthcare and international allocations remain on the near-term list, alongside firmer rules under discussion for the portfolio's smaller, more volatile names.
